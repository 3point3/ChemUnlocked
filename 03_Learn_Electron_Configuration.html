Electron Configuration

Where do electrons live inside an atom — and why does it matter? This unit builds from the basics of energy levels all the way to writing configurations, drawing orbital diagrams, and understanding why light is the key to everything electrons do.

3.1 What Is Electron Configuration?

Every atom has electrons. Those electrons are not just floating around randomly — they live in specific spots based on their energy. The electron configuration is a shorthand code that tells you exactly where every electron lives inside an atom.

Think of it like a school building. The building has floors (energy levels), and each floor has different rooms (sublevels). Electrons fill the lowest floors first before moving up.

Example — Oxygen (8 electrons total) 1s² 2s² 2p⁴

Read this as: 2 electrons in the 1s sublevel, 2 electrons in the 2s sublevel, and 4 electrons in the 2p sublevel. Add them up: 2 + 2 + 4 = 8 total. ✓

▸

The superscript number tells you how many electrons are in that sublevel — not which energy level it is. So 2p⁴ means: sublevel p, energy level 2, with 4 electrons in it.

3.2 Energy Levels and Sublevels

The principal quantum number (written as n) names the main energy level, or shell. The larger n is, the higher the energy and the farther the electrons are from the nucleus.

Each energy level contains a set of sublevels: s, p, d, and f. The number of sublevels in a shell equals n. So shell 1 has only one sublevel (s), shell 2 has two (s and p), and so on.

3.3 Light as Waves and Packets of Energy

Light is how we know what electrons are doing inside atoms. Understanding wavelength, frequency, and energy is the foundation of this entire unit.

Light travels as a wave and also comes in discrete packets called photons. Two properties describe any light wave:

  • Wavelength (λ, lambda) — the distance between two wave peaks, measured in meters (or nm). Longer λ = lower energy.
  • Frequency (ν, nu) — how many wave cycles pass a point per second, measured in Hz (s⁻¹). Higher ν = higher energy.
↑ shorter λ, higher ν, more E 700 nm longer λ, lower ν, less E ↑
Wave Equation — connects wavelength and frequency c = λ × ν    where c = 3.00 × 10⁸ m/s (speed of light)

Rearranged:   λ = c / ν    and    ν = c / λ

The energy of one photon of light is given by Planck's equation:

Planck's Equation — photon energy E = h × ν    where h = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s (Planck's constant)

Combining both equations:   E = hc / λ

▸

Wavelength and frequency always move in opposite directions. As wavelength gets longer, frequency gets smaller — and energy gets smaller with it. Ultraviolet light (short λ) carries more energy per photon than infrared light (long λ). This is why UV damages skin and IR does not.

3.4 Atomic Emission Spectra and the Bohr Model

When a gas is heated or electrically excited, its atoms absorb energy and electrons jump to higher energy levels (excited states). When those electrons fall back to lower levels, they release that energy as light — but only at specific wavelengths. This produces a line emission spectrum, not a continuous rainbow.

Niels Bohr proposed that electrons orbit the nucleus only at specific fixed distances (energy levels, n = 1, 2, 3…). The energy of light emitted equals the difference in energy between the two levels:

Energy of emitted/absorbed photon ΔE = E_final − E_initial = hν

For hydrogen specifically, the energy of each level is:

Bohr's hydrogen energy levels Eₙ = −2.18 × 10⁻¹⁸ J × (1/n²)

The four visible hydrogen lines (red at 656 nm, blue-green at 486 nm, indigo at 434 nm, violet at 410 nm) are all Balmer series transitions. Electrons falling from n = 3, 4, 5, and 6 down to n = 2 produce exactly these four colors.

3.5 How Many Electrons Fit in Each Shell?

The total number of electrons that fit in each principal shell follows from adding the sublevels together. This is where 3.1 and 3.2 pay off:

  • n = 1: only s → 2 electrons
  • n = 2: s + p → 2 + 6 = 8 electrons
  • n = 3: s + p + d → 2 + 6 + 10 = 18 electrons
  • n = 4: s + p + d + f → 2 + 6 + 10 + 14 = 32 electrons

3.6 The Three Rules for Filling Electrons

Rule 1 — Aufbau Principle ("building up")

Electrons always fill the lowest available energy sublevel first. The filling order does not follow the sublevel number perfectly — 4s fills before 3d because 4s has lower energy when the atom has few electrons.

Filling order: 1s → 2s → 2p → 3s → 3p → 4s → 3d → 4p → 5s → 4d → 5p
Rule 2 — Pauli Exclusion Principle

Each orbital holds a maximum of 2 electrons, and they must have opposite spins (one ↑, one ↓). You cannot place two spin-up electrons in the same orbital.

Rule 3 — Hund's Rule ("bus seat rule")

When filling a sublevel with multiple orbitals (p, d, or f), put one electron in each orbital first (all with the same spin, ↑) before doubling up in any orbital. Electrons prefer to spread out and stay unpaired when they can.

Why? Electrons repel each other. Spreading out into separate orbitals reduces electron-electron repulsion and lowers the atom's energy.

An electron configuration lists each sublevel in Aufbau order followed by a superscript showing the number of electrons in that sublevel.

▸

Noble-gas shorthand replaces the inner electrons with the symbol of the noble gas that precedes your element on the periodic table. For example, K (Z=19) is written [Ar] 4s¹ because Ar (Z=18) is the noble gas just before K. The [Ar] accounts for all 18 inner electrons so you only have to write the outermost ones.

Ex 1 Wavelength ↔ Frequency ↔ Energy

Given: A hydrogen atom emits blue-green light with a wavelength of 486.1 nm (the Hβ line, Balmer series). Find: (a) frequency and (b) energy of this photon.

Step 1 — Convert wavelength from nm to meters

Always convert to SI base units (meters) before using the formula.

486.1 nm  ×  1 × 10⁻⁹ m1 nm  = 4.861 × 10⁻⁷ m
Step 2 — Calculate frequency using ν = c/λ
ν = (3.00 × 10⁸ m/s) ÷ (4.861 × 10⁻⁷ m) = 6.17 × 10¹⁴ Hz
Units: m/s ÷ m = 1/s = Hz ✓
Step 3 — Calculate energy using E = hν
E = (6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s) × (6.17 × 10¹⁴ s⁻¹) = 4.09 × 10⁻¹⁹ J
Units: J·s × s⁻¹ = J ✓

Ex 3 Hydrogen Energy Levels and Spectral Lines

Given: An electron in hydrogen falls from n = 4 to n = 2. Find: (a) Energy and wavelength of the photon emitted. (b) Which series? Is it visible?

Step 1 — Calculate the energy of each level
E₄ = −2.18 × 10⁻¹⁸ × (1/16) = −1.36 × 10⁻¹⁹ J
E₂ = −2.18 × 10⁻¹⁸ × (1/4) = −5.45 × 10⁻¹⁹ J
Step 2 — Calculate ΔE (final minus initial)
ΔE = E₂ − E₄ = −5.45 × 10⁻¹⁹ − (−1.36 × 10⁻¹⁹) = −4.09 × 10⁻¹⁹ J

The negative sign confirms this is emission (energy released as a photon).

Step 3 — Find wavelength using λ = hc/|ΔE|
λ = (6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ × 3.00 × 10⁸) ÷ (4.09 × 10⁻¹⁹) = 4.86 × 10⁻⁷ m = 486 nm

This falls in the visible blue-green range — Balmer series (n_final = 2). This is the Hβ line!
