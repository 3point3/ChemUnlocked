## 1.2 Lab Equipment: Which Tool to Use and Why
- Paragraph 1: Teachers typically have students learn common lab equipment in chemistry classes. Lots of equipment may
seem similar to others (e.g. beakers, graduated cylinders, test tubes, flasks), but it is important to
know which one to use for a specific use or situation:
- Paragraph 2: Quick review
- List item 1: If the question asks for an **accurate measured volume**, choose a [graduated cylinder](#unit01-graduated-cylinder),
pipette, or burette, not a beaker.
- List item 2: If the question asks for **sample mass only**, place the weigh boat on the [balance](#unit01-electronic-balance)
first and press **TARE** before adding the sample using a scoopula or mini-spatula.
- List item 3: Important: "holds liquid" and "measures liquid" are not the same job. Beakers hold liquid but do not
measure it accurately like a graduated cylinder, pipette, or burette.
- Table row 1: Task | Best tool | Why
- Table row 2: Hold or mix a liquid | Beaker | Good for holding and mixing, not for precise volume
- Table row 3: Measure liquid volume accurately | Graduated cylinder | Better for measured volume than a beaker
- Table row 4: Prepare one exact final volume | Volumetric flask | Built for one exact marked volume
- Table row 5: Deliver a precise variable volume | Burette | Used when the delivered amount must be measured carefully
- Table row 6: Transfer an exact volume | Pipette | Designed to measure and move a precise amount
- Table row 7: Measure mass | Electronic balance | Reads mass directly; tare removes the container mass
- Table row 8: Heat and stir a solution | Hot plate / stirrer | Designed for controlled heating and mixing
- Table row 9: Support glassware over a heat source | Ring stand + clamps | Holds equipment in place during heating
- Table row 10: Transfer solids | Spatula / scoopula | Moves solids cleanly without using your hands
- Table row 11: Rinse glassware | Wash bottle | Lets you direct distilled water where you need it

## 1.3 Measurement and SI Units: Writing Data to Use in the
Correct Equations and Keep Track of It
- Paragraph 1: Science uses the **International System of Units (SI)** so measurements mean the same thing
everywhere. Important: every measurement has a *number* and a *unit*. A number by itself is
not enough. Calculations in other topics like [atomic structure](/03_learn_atomic_structure), moles, stoichiometry, thermochemistry, gas laws, solutions, equilibrium, and acids and bases will only work if your units are under control.
Learn about SI Units in chemistry here:
- Paragraph 2: SI Base Units
- Paragraph 3: Common Metric Prefixes
- Paragraph 4: Temperature in chemistry is almost always in degrees Celsius for everyday lab measurements. You'll need to
convert back and forth to Kelvin when you get to gas laws and thermodynamics. That's where the simple
temperature equation, Kelvin = degrees Celciuis + 273 is used.
- List item 1: Every measurement is a number and a unit together. One without the other is not a measurement.
- List item 2: Write both the number and the unit every single time. You will lose points on exams, quizzes and
homework if you don't.
- List item 3: Keep units visible through every step of the calculation. Do not take shortcuts. It always pays
off.
- List item 4: Chemists use **Celsius (°C)** for everyday lab measurements and **Kelvin
(K)** for gas law and thermodynamics calculations.
- List item 5: Many intro chemistry problems round 273.15 to 273. Follow the directions given.
- List item 6: 0 K (absolute zero) is the coldest possible temperature — there are no negative Kelvin values.

## 1.4 Significant Figures: Which Digits Count and Which Do Not
- Paragraph 1: Sig figs are kind of weird at first, because most people don’t run into them in everyday life and have to wonder, “How many sig figs do I need here?” One place you might see them is at a gas station. A gallon of gas might be posted as
2.99 9/10 dollars. That really means 2.999 dollars. Those three decimal places matter when the price gets calculated at the pump.

Another place sig figs show up, is calories on a food label. For example a food label that says the serving size is 28 grams and there are 180 Calories in that amount. Why not 182 Calories or 177 Calorories? It is becuase the ones place is not a sig fig. Only the tens place is significant on that label. If Calories were measure at 186, the label would round to 190.

Significant figures communicate the **precision** of a measurement. The big
question is simple: which digits came from the measurement, and which zeros are only placeholders? If you
are confused, fix it now. Sig figs keep showing up in calculations all the way through moles and stoichiometry.
- Paragraph 2: Rules for Counting Sig Figs
- Paragraph 3: Sig Figs in Calculations
- Paragraph 4: This is where the two rules get mixed up. The operation type — not the number of digits — tells you which
rule applies.
- Paragraph 5: Common mistake
- List item 1: **Analog instrument:** record all certain digits plus one estimated digit.
- List item 2: **Digital instrument:** record every digit shown on the display.
- List item 3: **Multiplication/Division:** Answer has the same number of sig figs as the measurement with
the *fewest* sig figs.
- List item 4: **Addition/Subtraction:** Answer is rounded to the same decimal place as the measurement
with the fewest decimal places.
- List item 5: **Exact numbers** (counts, definitions) have unlimited sig figs and do not limit your
answer.
- List item 6: Very important: Do NOT round intermediate steps.
- List item 7: Carry extra digits through the entire calculation and round only the final answer.
- List item 8: Important:When you need to show that trailing zeros ARE significant, use a decimal point (2500.) or scientific
notation (2.500 × 103).
- List item 9: Do not confuse decimal-place rules with sig-fig rules. Addition and subtraction care about decimal
places. Multiplication and division care about sig figs.

## 1.5 Dimensional Analysis: How to Convert Units and Set-uo Chemistry Problems
- Paragraph 1: Want to get better at chemistry test and quiz problems? Here are two habits that will help a lot:
- Show your work every time. Write everything out completely, including the units.
- Use dimensional analysis, also called the factor-label method.
= If you always write out your work and use units to guide you, you’ll make fewer mistakes and get a lot more confident with chemistry problems. The factor-label method works because the units stay visible the whole time. You look at the units, choose conversion factors that cancel the units you don’t want, and solve the problem step by step. This helps you catch mistakes early and usually leads you to the right answer.
- Check out these examples:

- Paragraph 2: **Problem:** Convert 250.0 mL to liters.
- Paragraph 3: **Given:** 250.0 mL
- Paragraph 4: **Find:** L
- Paragraph 5: Start with **250.0 mL**. The answer needs to be in **L**.
- Paragraph 6: **250.0 mL = 0.2500 L**. The conversion factor is exact, so the answer keeps the
**4 significant figures** from 250.0 mL.
- Paragraph 7: **Problem:** A sample has a mass of 13.5 g. Its density is 2.70 g/mL. Find the sample
volume.
- Paragraph 8: **Given:** 13.5 g; 2.70 g/mL
- Paragraph 9: **Find:** mL
- Paragraph 10: The given unit is **g**, but the answer needs **mL**. Since
density = g/mL, flip it so **g** is on the bottom:
- Paragraph 11: **V = 5.00 mL**. This works because the density ratio was arranged to remove grams and
leave milliliters.
- Paragraph 12: **Problem:** A gold sample has a volume of 25.0 mL. Gold has a density of 19.3 g/mL.
Find the mass of the sample in kilograms.
- Paragraph 13: **Given:** 25.0 mL; 19.3 g/mL
- Paragraph 14: **Find:** kg
- Paragraph 15: Density tells you grams per milliliter, so start by multiplying the volume by the density to get
**grams**.
- Paragraph 16: **m = 0.483 kg**. The given numbers 25.0 mL and 19.3 g/mL each have
**3 significant figures**, so the final answer should also have 3 significant figures.
- Paragraph 17: **Problem:** Convert 8.50 nm to meters.
- Paragraph 18: **Given:** 8.50 nm
- Paragraph 19: **Find:** m
- Paragraph 20: **1 nm = 10-9 m**, so a matching factor-label ratio is
**1 m / 109 nm**.
- Paragraph 21: **8.50 nm = 8.50 × 10-9 m**. The unit conversion is
exact,
so the answer keeps **3 significant figures**.
- Paragraph 22: Quick check before you move on
- List item 1: If the wrong unit is still showing, the conversion factor is upside down.
- List item 2: For multi-step problems, do not stop until the unit left is the unit the question asked for.
- List item 3: Keep units written through the entire setup. They tell you whether the math makes sense.

## 1.6 How to Read a Graduated Cylinder Correctly

*downward*. It is extremely important to read the liquid at the meniscus from the **bottom** of that curve, at eye level. Many students read from the top of the curve or look from the wrong angle — both give the wrong number. It ruins the reading, and you will get the problem wrong or do your lab calculations incorrectly.

## 1.7 How to Use an Electronic Balance Without Ruining the
Measurement
- Paragraph 1: An electronic balance measures mass in grams. In chemistry, that tells you how much of a substance you have for a reaction or how much was produced in a reaction.
It’s really important to understand the tare button. TARE zeros out the balance after you place a container on it, like a weigh boat or a beaker. That way, the container does not count toward the mass of your sample. The usual process is: put the container on the balance, press TARE, and then add your sample. Now the balance will only show the mass of the substance you added.
This is one of the most common lab mistakes, but it’s also one of the easiest to avoid.
- Paragraph 2: Correct Balance Technique
- List item 1: Never skip taring.
- List item 2: TARE zeros the display so only the sample mass shows.
- List item 3: Always tare with the container already on the pan, before you add anything.
- Table row 1: Problem | Result
- Table row 2: Forgot to tare | The display includes container + sample.
- Table row 3: Did not record all shown digits | You lose precision.
- Table row 4: Removed the tared container and kept going | The reading is no longer valid.

## Do Not Miss These Common Errors
- List item 1: **Parallax:** Reading a graduated cylinder from above or below eye level gives an incorrect
meniscus position.
- List item 2: **Forgetting to tare:** Always tare the balance with the container before adding your
sample.
- List item 3: **Rounding too early:** Carry all digits through calculations; round only the final answer.
- List item 4: **Confusing mass and weight:** Mass (kg, g) is a property of matter; weight is the
gravitational force on that mass.
- List item 5: **Hypothesis ≠ prediction:** A hypothesis must be testable and falsifiable. "I think plants
will grow better" is not a hypothesis.
- List item 6: **Forgetting units:** A number without a unit is not a measurement.

## 1.8 Lab Safety: What You Need to Do Before Anything Goes Wrong
- Paragraph 1: Lab safety is very important and keeps you are your lab mates safe and healthy. Start here: match each hazard to the right action
before you touch chemicals, glassware, or heat. That is how you protect yourself, your lab partner, and your
data.
- Paragraph 2: Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)
- Paragraph 3: **Safety goggles** — worn whenever chemicals, heat, or glassware are in use. Regular
glasses do not count.
- Paragraph 4: **Gloves** — nitrile gloves protect skin from corrosive, toxic, or skin-absorbed
chemicals. Remove before touching your face.
- Paragraph 5: **Lab apron/coat** — protects clothing and skin from spills. Long pants and closed-toe
shoes are required.
- Paragraph 6: Before lab
- Paragraph 7: These three happen before you touch anything. If you skip them, the rest of the list doesn't protect you.
- Paragraph 8: During lab
- Paragraph 9: Notice: most of these are about habits, not knowledge. They only protect you if they become automatic.
- Paragraph 10: If something goes wrong
- List item 1: Read the entire procedure before beginning.
- List item 2: Know the location of the eyewash station, safety shower, fire extinguisher, and first aid kit.
- List item 3: Put on goggles and any required protective clothing before handling chemicals or glassware.
- List item 4: Never eat, drink, or apply cosmetics in the lab.
- List item 5: Read labels carefully before using a chemical.
- List item 6: Never smell a chemical directly — waft the vapors toward you with your hand.
- List item 7: Add acid to water, not water to acid.
- List item 8: Keep long hair tied back and loose items away from flames and chemicals.
- List item 9: Report spills, breakage, and exposure to your teacher immediately.
- List item 10: Use safety equipment right away when needed.
- List item 11: Dispose of chemicals only as instructed.

## 1.9 How Chemists Test an Idea and Defend a Claim
- Paragraph 1: Science is a way of learning about the world using evidence and experiments. It helps us answer questions we can actually test by asking, testing, measuring, and using data to support our conclusions.
- Start with a question you can answer by making observations or measurements.
Example: Does warmer water make an antacid tablet dissolve faster?
- Use what you already know to make a smarter test. For the antacid example, you might already know that higher temperature makes particles move faster. That kind of background knowledge helps turn your idea into a real hypothesis instead of just a guess.
- A hypothesis is your prediction, and it should also explain why you think that. It is testable and is often written in if-then form.
- Change only one variable at a time. Decide what you will measure, keep the other conditions the same, and include a comparison group when needed.
- Record your data clearly while it is happening. Don’t wait and try to remember it later.
- Look at what the data actually show, not what you hoped they would show. Good science follows the evidence.
- Write your method clearly enough that someone else could repeat your experiment. If another scientist can’t do it the same way, the results are not very reliable.
- Paragraph 9: Common mix-up: theory vs. law
- List item 1: Write observations and measurements the moment you make them.
- List item 2: Be specific: "the solution turned pale yellow" is useful. "It changed" is not.
- List item 3: Organize results so you can actually compare them in the next step.
- List item 4: If the data support the hypothesis, say so with specific evidence.
- List item 5: If they do not, that is still a real result — report it honestly.
- List item 6: Conclusions are based on evidence, not opinion.
- List item 7: A scientific *law* describes what happens consistently.
- List item 8: A scientific *theory* explains why or how it happens.
- List item 9: Neither word means "guess" in science. A theory is not a weak law, and a law does not explain the
cause.
- Table row 1: Variable type | Definition | In the example
- Table row 2: Independent | What you deliberately change | Water temperature
- Table row 3: Dependent | What you measure as a result | Time for the tablet to dissolve
- Table row 4: Constants | Everything else held the same | Tablet brand, water volume, container, stirring
- Table row 5: Comparison | Condition used for a fair check | Room-temperature water

## Example 1 · Applying the Scientific Method
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** A student wants to test whether higher water temperature changes how fast an
antacid tablet dissolves.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** The student can change water temperature and measure dissolving time while
keeping tablet brand, water volume, container, and stirring method the same.
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** The independent variable, dependent variable, and what result would support the
hypothesis.
- Paragraph 4: Question: Does higher water temperature make an antacid tablet dissolve faster?
- Paragraph 5: *If* the water temperature increases, *then* the tablet will dissolve faster, because
particle motion increases at higher temperature.
- Paragraph 6: Independent variable: water temperature. Dependent variable: time to dissolve. Constants: tablet
brand, water volume, container, and stirring method.
- Paragraph 7: Run trials at different temperatures, measure how long the tablet takes to dissolve, and compare the
results.
- Paragraph 8: If higher-temperature trials dissolve faster than lower-temperature trials, the data support the
hypothesis.
