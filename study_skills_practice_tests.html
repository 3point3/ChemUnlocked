Why Practice Tests Beat Rereading Every Time

Retrieval practice — the act of pulling information out of your memory rather than putting it back in — is the most consistently effective study strategy in cognitive science. Here's the research, and here's how to use it.

⊕ ChemUnlocked Study Skills ◷ 7 min read ✦ Research-backed

Re-reading is the most popular study strategy among students everywhere. It's also one of the least effective. A landmark review of ten major study techniques — published in Psychological Science in the Public Interest — rated re-reading as low utility, while practice testing received the highest possible rating: high utility across virtually all conditions, subjects, and learner types.

This isn't a minor effect. The difference between students who re-read and students who practice retrieval is large enough to span multiple letter grades. Understanding why this works — not just that it works — will change how you approach every class you ever take.

The Testing Effect: What's Actually Happening

When you re-read your notes, information flows into your brain. It feels productive. The words are familiar, the concepts seem clear, and you finish feeling like you know the material. This is called the fluency illusion — familiarity masquerading as understanding.

When you take a practice test, information must flow out of your brain. You have to construct an answer from scratch, without the cue of seeing the correct information on the page. This is effortful, uncomfortable, and — critically — exactly what a real test requires.

◈ Research Note
A study published in Science by Karpicke and Roediger (2008) compared students who restudied material four times versus students who studied once and then practiced retrieval three times. One week later, the retrieval group recalled 80% of the material. The restudy group recalled 36%. The gap is not subtle.

Low vs. High Utility Study Strategies

✗ Low Utility
  • Re-reading notes or textbook
  • Highlighting or underlining
  • Summarizing (passively)
  • Keyword mnemonics
  • Imagery for text learning
✓ High Utility
  • Practice testing (retrieval)
  • Distributed practice (spaced)
  • Interleaved practice
  • Elaborative interrogation
  • Self-explanation

Notice what's on the low-utility list: re-reading, highlighting, summarizing. These are the strategies most students default to, partly because they're familiar and partly because they feel like they're working. The high-utility strategies feel harder — because they are. That difficulty is doing something.

Why Getting It Wrong Is Part of the Process

One thing that stops students from using practice tests is the fear of being wrong. If you're studying and you get a practice problem wrong, that feels like failure. It's actually the opposite.

When you attempt to retrieve something and fail, your brain registers that gap. When you then see the correct answer, your brain encodes it more deeply than it would have if you'd simply read it without attempting retrieval first. The failed attempt makes the correction stick. This is called the hypercorrection effect, and it's one of the reasons practice testing works even when you're getting answers wrong.

How to Use This

Don't Skip the Attempt

When you sit down with a practice problem and aren't sure of the answer, try anyway. Write something. Commit to an approach. Then check your work and read the solution — even if you got it right, read the full solution to see if your reasoning was correct. The attempt-and-correct cycle is where learning actually happens.

Spaced Retrieval: The Timing Multiplier

Practice testing becomes dramatically more powerful when combined with spaced practice — distributing your retrieval attempts over multiple sessions rather than cramming them into one. Your brain consolidates memories during rest, particularly during sleep. Reviewing material once, sleeping, then retrieving it the next day is neurologically different from reviewing it twice in the same sitting.

Practical Application

The 1–3–7 Schedule

After learning a new concept, do a practice retrieval the same day. Return to the same material three days later for another retrieval session. Then again seven days later. Each session should be a genuine attempt to retrieve without looking at notes first. This pattern aligns with how memory consolidation works and produces dramatically better long-term retention than a single massed review session before a test.

◈ Research Note
The spacing effect was first documented by Hermann Ebbinghaus in the 1880s and has been one of the most replicated findings in all of psychology. Despite over a century of evidence, most students still cram — because massed practice feels more productive in the moment even though it produces worse outcomes.

The Honest Bottom Line

The students who perform best on chemistry tests are rarely the ones who studied the most hours. They're the ones who spent their study time doing something cognitively demanding — attempting problems, checking their reasoning, identifying gaps, and going back to fill them. That process is uncomfortable. It produces results.

Start with one unit. Open the practice set before you feel ready. Get some wrong. Read the solutions carefully. Then do it again three days later. That loop — attempt, check, space, repeat — is the most reliable study system available to you.

▸

A useful rule of thumb: if your study session is comfortable and fluent, you're probably not learning very much. Productive studying feels like a workout — some resistance, some struggle, and a clear sense of where you're still weak. That's the process working correctly.
