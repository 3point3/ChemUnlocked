# Concepts Tab

## 7.1 Start Here: What a Mole Actually Means
- Paragraph 1: Get this one idea clear and the rest of the unit starts making a lot more sense.
- Paragraph 2: A mole is a counting unit. One mole always means **6.022 × 10^23** representative particles, no matter what substance you are talking about.
- Paragraph 3: Think of it like a dozen, but for particles that are far too small to count one by one. You are not memorizing a weird number for no reason. You are learning the counting unit that makes chemistry calculations possible.
- List item 1: For an element, the particles are atoms. For a molecular substance, the particles are molecules. Notice that the counted thing changes with the substance.
- List item 2: Atoms and molecules are too small to count one by one or weigh individually, so chemists use the mole to connect particle count to measurable mass.
- List item 3: One mole means the same number of particles every time, but not the same mass every time.
- Label 1: Core Mole Idea

## 7.1A Mole Concept Spotlight: Same Count, Different Mass
- Paragraph 1: Notice the big idea here: one mole always means the same **number of particles**, but definitely not the same **mass**.
- Paragraph 2: The particle type depends on the substance. For carbon, the particles are atoms; for oxygen gas, they are O2 molecules; and for carbon dioxide, they are CO2 molecules. Same mole idea, different particles, different masses.

and has a mass of **31.999 g**.
- Paragraph 5: The balanced equation shows a **1 : 1 : 1 mole ratio**.
- Paragraph 6: This is what you need to notice: the coefficients compare substances in moles first, not in grams.
- Paragraph 7: Use the coefficients in the balanced equation to compare substances in moles, not in grams.
- Paragraph 8: Multiply by molar mass to turn the counted amount into a mass you can actually weigh in the lab.
- Paragraph 9: Do not miss this
- List item 1: The coefficients tell you the mole ratio.
- List item 2: They do **not** mean the masses are equal.
- List item 3: In this reaction, 12.01 g of carbon reacts with 31.999 g of oxygen to make 44.010 g of carbon dioxide.

## 7.2 Molar Mass: The Bridge Between Grams and Moles
- Paragraph 1: Moles connect particle counting to mass. The **molar mass** (symbol: MM) is the number that makes that connection. It tells you the mass of exactly one mole of a substance in g/mol.
- Paragraph 2: Start here when a problem gives you grams. Grams come from the balance, but chemistry comparisons usually happen in moles first. Molar mass is the bridge that lets you switch between those two ideas without losing the meaning of the substance.
- Paragraph 3: If you are confused here
- Paragraph 4: Use these five compounds as models. For each one, notice how you multiply the atomic mass by the number of atoms in the formula before adding — that is the calculation pattern you will repeat.
- List item 1: For an element, the molar mass is its atomic mass from the periodic table written in g/mol.
- List item 2: For a compound, find the total by adding the mass contribution from each element in the formula.
- List item 3: If atomic mass values feel unfamiliar, review [Unit 03 · Atomic Structure](/03_learn_atomic_structure).
- List item 4: If you need help locating and comparing element data, revisit [Unit 05 · Periodic Table & Trends](/05_learn_periodic_table_and_trends).

## 7.4 Percent Composition: Which Element Contributes the Most Mass?
- Paragraph 1: Percent composition by mass tells you **how much of a compound's total mass comes from each element**.
- Paragraph 2: Here is the idea: pretend you have 1 mole of the compound. Each element contributes part of that total molar mass. To find the percent for one element, compare that element's mass contribution to the whole.
- Paragraph 3: Notice what this calculation actually does: it connects a chemical formula to what a sample is made of by mass — which is why this shows up again in lab data and empirical-formula problems.
- Paragraph 4: **Worked example — Water (H2O, MM = 18.015 g/mol):**
- Paragraph 5: %H = (2 atoms × 1.008 g/mol) ÷ 18.015 g/mol × 100 = **11.19% hydrogen**
- Paragraph 6: %O = (1 atom × 15.999 g/mol) ÷ 18.015 g/mol × 100 = **88.81% oxygen**
- Paragraph 7: Notice that the percentages should add up to 100% or very close after rounding. If they do not, something probably went wrong in the arithmetic.
- Paragraph 8: Why this matters
- List item 1: Percent composition connects **experimental lab data** to the **empirical formula**.
- List item 2: In combustion-style problems, the measured masses lead to percent composition first.
- List item 3: From there, you can work back to the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms.
- List item 4: Be ready to work both directions: from formula to percent and from percent back to formula.
- Label 1: % Composition by Mass — How to Calculate It

## 7.5 Empirical and Molecular Formulas: Turning Data into a Formula
- Paragraph 1: The **empirical formula** shows the **smallest whole-number mole ratio** of the elements in a compound.
- Paragraph 2: The **molecular formula** gives the actual number of each type of atom in one molecule. The molecular formula is always a whole-number multiple of the empirical formula.
- Paragraph 3: 2. Divide by the Smallest Mole Value (3.406):
C: 3.407 / 3.406 = 1.00
H: 4.544 / 3.406 = 1.33
O: 3.406 / 3.406 = 1.00

3. Multiply Until Whole (Clear the .33 decimal):
Multiply all ratios by 3:
C: 1.00 × 3 = 3
H: 1.33 × 3 = 4
O: 1.00 × 3 = 3

- Paragraph 4: Common sticking point
- Paragraph 5: Do not skip the chemistry idea here: masses and percents are not the final ratio. To find an empirical formula, you must convert each element to moles first, because **moles are the ratio that matters.**
- Paragraph 6: Concrete check
- Paragraph 7: This is a very useful trick. If you assume you have exactly 100 g of the compound, each percent turns directly into grams. For example, 40.0% carbon becomes 40.0 g carbon. Now you have something you can actually convert to moles.
- Paragraph 8: Divide each element's mass by its molar mass. This converts grams into moles and puts every element on the same counting basis. That is what lets you compare them fairly.
- Paragraph 9: Find whichever element has the fewest moles, then divide every mole value by that number. You are rescaling the ratios so the smallest value becomes 1.000. Those new values are your preliminary atom ratios.
- Paragraph 10: Your ratios from Step 3 should be close to whole numbers. If they are not, multiply **all** ratios by the same small whole number until they become whole numbers together. Common fixes are ×2 for 0.5, ×3 for 0.33, and ×4 for 0.25.

Round n to the nearest whole number, then multiply every subscript in your empirical formula by n to get the final molecular formula.

# Examples Tab

## Example 1 · Grams → Moles → Molecules
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** How many molecules are in 25.0 g of glucose, C6H12O6?
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** 25.0 g C6H12O6
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** number of molecules
- Paragraph 4: **Road map:** grams → moles → molecules. Notice that you cannot jump from grams straight to molecules.
- Paragraph 5: Start by finding the molar mass. Add the contribution from each element:
- Paragraph 6: 6(12.011) + 12(1.008) + 6(15.999) = 72.066 + 12.096 + 95.994 = 180.156 g/mol
- Paragraph 7: Write the given quantity first. Then build the chain so the unit you already have cancels. If the units do not cancel cleanly, the setup is not right yet.
- Paragraph 8: Calculate:
- Paragraph 9: (25.0 ÷ 180.156) × 6.022×10²³ = 8.36 × 10²² molecules

## Example 2 · Mass of One Element in a Compound Sample
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** A 25.0 g sample of glucose, C6H12O6, contains how many grams of oxygen?
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** 25.0 g C6H12O6
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** grams of oxygen
- Paragraph 4: **Road map:** use the mass contribution of oxygen in 1 mol of glucose.
- Paragraph 5: In C6H12O6, oxygen contributes 6(15.999) = 95.994 g O per 1 mol of glucose.
- Paragraph 6: The full molar mass of glucose is 180.156 g/mol.
- Paragraph 7: Calculate:
- Paragraph 8: 25.0 × (95.994 ÷ 180.156) = 13.32 g oxygen

## Example 3 · Empirical Formula from Combustion Data
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Find the empirical formula first. Then use the molar mass to find the molecular formula.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** 1.000 g compound containing only C, H, and O; 1.466 g CO2; 0.600 g H2O; molar mass ≈ 88 g/mol
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** molecular formula
- Paragraph 4: Key idea: all C ends up in CO2, all H ends up in H2O, and O is found by subtraction. This looks long, but each step has a job.
- Paragraph 5: Roadmap
- Paragraph 6: Start by turning the combustion data into moles of each element. Do not try to guess the formula from the masses alone.
- Paragraph 7: Calculate:
- Paragraph 8: 0.03331 mol C → mass C = 0.4000 g
- Paragraph 9: Calculate:
- Paragraph 10: 0.06661 mol H → mass H = 0.06714 g
- Paragraph 11: Now compare the mole amounts to get the smallest whole-number ratio.
- Paragraph 12: Divide all mole amounts by the smallest value.
- Paragraph 13: That gives 1 : 2 : 1 → CH2O and MM_emp = 30.026 g/mol
- Paragraph 14: The molecular formula must be a whole-number multiple of the empirical formula.

## Example 4 · Particles → Moles → Volume at STP
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Find the STP volume from a particle count.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** 3.50 × 10²⁴ molecules CO2
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** volume at STP
- Paragraph 4: Road map: molecules → moles → liters. You must pass through moles.
- Paragraph 5: (3.50 × 10²⁴ ÷ 6.022 × 10²³) × 22.4 = 5.812 × 22.4 = 130.2 L
