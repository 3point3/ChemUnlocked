## 15.2 pH and pOH: Reading the Log Scale Correctly
- Paragraph 1: Because hydronium and hydroxide concentrations are often tiny numbers, chemists use a logarithmic scale. The **pH** and **pOH** compress those wide ranges into values you can actually compare.
- Paragraph 2: If you are confused, start with the neutral benchmark first, then decide whether the solution is acidic or basic before you calculate anything else.
- List item 1: Each unit change in pH represents a *tenfold* change in [H3O+].
- List item 2: A solution of pH 3 has 100 times the hydronium concentration of a pH 5 solution, not 2 times.
- List item 3: Notice the common mistake: the pH scale is logarithmic, not linear.
- Table row 1: Condition | [H3O+] vs [OH-] | pH | Example
- Table row 2: Acidic | [H3O+] > [OH-] | < 7 | Stomach acid (pH ≈ 1.5)
- Table row 3: Neutral | [H3O+] = [OH-] | = 7 | Pure water
- Table row 4: Basic | [H3O+] < [OH-] | > 7 | Bleach (pH ≈ 12)

## 15.3 Strong vs. Weak Acids and Bases: Do Not Mix Up Strength and Concentration
- Paragraph 1: A **strong acid** ionizes essentially completely in water. A **weak acid** ionizes only partially, so an equilibrium is established.
- Paragraph 2: **Strong** and **weak** describe how much a substance ionizes. **Concentrated** and **dilute** describe how much of the substance is present.
- Paragraph 3: Do not use strength and concentration as if they mean the same thing. A concentrated weak acid can still have a lower pH than a dilute strong acid.
- Paragraph 4: The acid ionization constant K_a describes that equilibrium. The larger the K_a, the stronger the acid.
- Paragraph 5: For bases, the analogous constant is K_b. A key relationship connects a conjugate pair:
- Paragraph 6: Three patterns connect acid strength to structure — use these when comparing acids that are not on the strong acid list:
- List item 1: Binary acids down the same group: strength *increases* going down because the H–X bond becomes easier to break.
- List item 2: Binary acids across the same period: strength generally increases as the bonded atom becomes more electronegative.
- List item 3: Oxyacids with the same central atom: more oxygen atoms usually mean a stronger acid because electron density is pulled away from the O–H bond more strongly.
- Table row 1: Strong Acids | Strong Bases
- Table row 2: HCl, HBr, HI | NaOH, KOH, LiOH
- Table row 3: HNO3, H2SO4, HClO4 | Ca(OH)2, Ba(OH)2, Sr(OH)2

## 15.4 pK_a and pK_b: The Strength Scale That Runs Backward
- Paragraph 1: Just as pH is the negative log of a concentration, **pK_a** and **pK_b** are the negative logs of the ionization constants K_a and K_b.
- Paragraph 2: Using a log scale makes these equilibrium constants easier to compare and easier to use in buffer calculations. It also creates a common mistake: the direction flips, and that is easy to lose track of.
- Paragraph 3: The direction flips on the log scale. The same logic applies to bases and pK_b.
- Paragraph 4: For a conjugate acid-base pair, pK_a and pK_b always sum to 14 at 25 °C — the same relationship as pH and pOH:
- List item 1: A stronger acid has a larger K_a → smaller pK_a.
- List item 2: A weaker acid has a smaller K_a → larger pK_a.
- List item 3: For a buffer made from a weak acid and its conjugate base, pH = pK_a when [HA] = [A-].
- List item 4: At that point the Henderson-Hasselbalch log term equals zero.
- List item 5: This is why buffers work best near the acid's pK_a.
- Table row 1: Acid | K_a | pK_a | Relative strength
- Table row 2: Hydrofluoric acid (HF) | 6.8 × 10^-4 | 3.17 | Relatively strong weak acid
- Table row 3: Acetic acid (CH3COOH) | 1.8 × 10^-5 | 4.74 | Moderate
- Table row 4: Carbonic acid (H2CO3) | 4.3 × 10^-7 | 6.37 | Moderate–weak
- Table row 5: Ammonium ion (NH4+) | 5.6 × 10^-10 | 9.25 | Very weak acid
- Table row 6: Hydrogen cyanide (HCN) | 4.9 × 10^-10 | 9.31 | Very weak acid

## 15.5 Polyprotic Acids: One Proton at a Time
- Paragraph 1: A **polyprotic acid** can lose more than one proton, but it loses them one step at a time.
- Paragraph 2: Each step has its own ionization constant. Later ionizations are usually much weaker because the next proton is being removed from a species that is already more negatively charged.
- Paragraph 3: Because K_a1 ≫ K_a2, the first ionization usually controls the pH. For most introductory problems, you can calculate the pH using only K_a1 unless you are told to analyze later steps.

## 15.6 Buffers: Why pH Stays Nearly Steady
- Paragraph 1: A **buffer** is a solution that resists changes in pH when small amounts of strong acid or strong base are added. Buffers contain an appreciable amount of *both* members of a weak conjugate acid-base pair. The acid component neutralizes added base, and the base component neutralizes added acid.
- Paragraph 2: The pH of a buffer is calculated using the **Henderson-Hasselbalch equation**:
- Paragraph 3: A buffer works best when [A-] ≈ [HA], meaning pH ≈ pK_a. Adding too much acid or base exhausts one component and destroys the buffer. This limit is called the **buffer capacity**.
- List item 1: Blood is buffered primarily by the carbonic acid / bicarbonate pair (H2CO3 / HCO3-, pK_a ≈ 6.1) and maintains a pH of 7.35–7.45.
- List item 2: Notice the key idea: a buffer only works if both parts of the conjugate pair are present in useful amounts.

## 15.7 Acid-Base Titrations: When Neutralization Does and Does Not Mean pH 7
- Paragraph 1: A **titration** finds the concentration of an unknown acid or base by reacting it with a solution of known concentration.
- Paragraph 2: The **equivalence point** is reached when the acid and base have reacted in the correct stoichiometric amounts. A **titration curve** plots pH vs. volume of titrant and helps you locate that point.
- Paragraph 3: Here is the point that catches everyone off guard: the pH at the equivalence point is not always 7. It depends on what species are present after neutralization.

## Ex 1 Identifying Conjugate Acid-Base Pairs
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Identify both conjugate acid-base pairs in the reaction.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** NH3 + H2O ⇌ NH4+ + OH-
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** Both conjugate pairs
- Paragraph 4: H2O donates one H+ to NH3. So H2O is the Brønsted-Lowry acid in this reaction.
- Paragraph 5: NH3 accepts that H+ and becomes NH4+. So NH3 is the Brønsted-Lowry base.
- Paragraph 6: **Pair 1:** H2O (acid) / OH- (conjugate base) — they differ by one H+.

**Pair 2:** NH4+ (conjugate acid) / NH3 (base) — they differ by one H+.

## Ex 2 Calculating pH of a Strong Acid
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Find the pH and pOH of a strong acid solution.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** 0.025 M HCl
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** pH and pOH
- Paragraph 4: HCl is a strong acid — it ionizes 100% in water. Therefore [H3O+] = 0.025 M exactly.

## Ex 3 pH of a Weak Acid Using K_a
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Find the pH of a weak acid using K_a.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** 0.100 M CH3COOH, K_a = 1.8 × 10^-5
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** pH
- Paragraph 4: CH3COOH is a weak acid, so it does not ionize completely like HCl. That means we cannot set [H3O+] equal to 0.100 M directly. We must use an equilibrium setup.
- Paragraph 5: CH3COOH + H2O ⇌ H3O+ + CH3COO-
- Paragraph 6: The approximation is valid because x / 0.100 = 1.34% < 5%.
- Table row 1: [CH3COOH] | [H3O+] | [CH3COO-]
- Table row 2: I | 0.100 | ≈ 0 | 0
- Table row 3: C | -x | +x | +x
- Table row 4: E | 0.100 - x | x | x

## Ex 4 Buffer pH Using Henderson-Hasselbalch
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Use Henderson-Hasselbalch to find the pH of a buffer.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** [HA] = 0.100 M acetic acid, [A-] = 0.150 M acetate, K_a = 1.8 × 10^-5
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** pH
- Paragraph 4: There is more conjugate base than weak acid: [A-] = 0.150 M and [HA] = 0.100 M. So the pH should be slightly above pK_a before we calculate anything.
- Paragraph 5: The pH is slightly above pK_a because there is more conjugate base than acid.
- Paragraph 6: That matches what Henderson-Hasselbalch predicts for a buffer on the basic side of its range.

## Ex 5 Converting Between K_a, pK_a, K_b, and pK_b
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Convert among K_a, pK_a, K_b, and pK_b.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** Acetic acid has K_a = 1.8 × 10^-5
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** pK_a, K_b of acetate, and pK_b of acetate
- Paragraph 4: A pK_a of 4.74 means acetic acid is a moderately weak acid.
- Paragraph 5: The large pK_b confirms that acetate is a very weak base.
