10.1Heat vs. Temperature

Heat is energy transferred because of a temperature difference. Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of particles. They are related but not the same thing.

IdeaMeaningExample
HeatEnergy moving from hotter to colder matterA hot metal block warms cooler water
TemperatureHow energetic particles are on average100°C water and 100°C steam have the same temperature but different total heat content
►

A large bucket of warm water can contain more total thermal energy than a tiny cup of hotter water. Temperature alone does not tell you total heat — mass matters too.

10.3Specific Heat and Calorimetry

When a substance changes temperature without changing phase, use the specific heat equation:

Temperature-change equation q = mcΔT
q = heat (J)   m = mass (g)   c = specific heat (J/g°C)   ΔT = Tfinal − Tinitial

In a calorimetry experiment, energy is conserved: heat lost by one substance equals heat gained by the other.

qlost = −qgained
►

Always calculate ΔT = final − initial. A negative q means the substance released heat. Water’s specific heat (4.184 J/g°C) is unusually high, which is why lakes resist temperature swings.

10.4Phase Changes: Melting, Freezing, Boiling, Condensation

During a phase change, temperature stays constant while energy breaks or forms intermolecular forces. Use a different equation depending on which change is occurring.

Phase-change equations q = mΔHfus    for melting or freezing
q = mΔHvap    for boiling or condensation
ProcessEnergy directionType
MeltingAbsorbs heatEndothermic
FreezingReleases heatExothermic

10.5Heating Curves

A heating curve shows how temperature changes as heat is added. You must split every heating-curve problem into separate segments and apply the correct equation to each — never use a single equation for the whole problem.

How to read each segment
  • Sloped segment — temperature is rising → use q = mcΔT (identify which phase is present to get the right c)
  • Flat segment at 0°C — melting or freezing → use q = mΔHfus
  • Flat segment at 100°C — boiling or condensing → use q = mΔHvap
  • Total heat = sum of q from every step that applies to your problem
Ice warming (q = mcΔT) Melting (q = mΔHfus) Liquid warming (q = mcΔT) Boiling (q = mΔHvap) Steam warming (q = mcΔT)
10.6Enthalpy, Endothermic, and Exothermic

Enthalpy change (ΔH) measures the heat flow of a reaction at constant pressure. Its sign tells you which direction heat moves.

ΔH = Hproducts − Hreactants
ΔH < 0 → exothermic (heat released to surroundings)
ΔH > 0 → endothermic (heat absorbed from surroundings)

10.7Catalysts and Activation Energy

A catalyst lowers activation energy by providing an alternate reaction pathway. It does not change ΔH — the reactant and product energy levels stay exactly the same.

10.8Hess’s Law and Heats of Formation

Hess’s law: because enthalpy is a state function, the overall ΔH for a reaction equals the sum of ΔH values for any set of steps that add up to that reaction. You can manipulate thermochemical equations algebraically.

ΔH°rxn = Σ nΔH°f, products − Σ nΔH°f, reactants
Reverse a reaction when needed

Flip the equation so a needed substance ends up on the correct side. Reverse the sign of ΔH.

Scale coefficients when needed

Multiply an equation so substances cancel. Multiply ΔH by the same factor.

Add all equations and enthalpies

Cancel substances on both sides. Sum all the ΔH values for the overall ΔH.

►

Elements in their standard states (O2(g), Fe(s), C(graphite), etc.) have ΔH°f = 0 by definition and contribute nothing to the sum.

10.9Thermochemical Stoichiometry

When a ΔH value is attached to a balanced equation, it applies to that reaction as written — for the exact molar amounts shown by the coefficients. Treat the enthalpy ratio like any other stoichiometric conversion factor.

Example: 2H2(g) + O2(g) → 2H2O(l)    ΔH = −572 kJ
→ 2 mol H2 releases 572 kJ   |   1 mol H2 releases 286 kJ
►

Always convert grams to moles first, then apply the mole ratio from the balanced equation to scale the enthalpy. Do not skip the mol conversion.

Example 1 — Specific Heat Calculation

Problem: How much heat is needed to warm 125 g of water from 20.0°C to 68.0°C? (c = 4.184 J/g°C)

Identify the given values

m = 125 g    c = 4.184 J/g°C    ΔT = 68.0 − 20.0 = 48.0°C

Apply q = mcΔT
q = (125)(4.184)(48.0) = 25,104 J = 25.1 kJ
Example 2 — Multi-step Heating Curve

Problem: A 20.0 g sample of liquid water at 25.0°C is heated to 100.0°C, completely boiled, then the steam is warmed to 118.0°C. Find the total heat. (cwater = 4.184 J/g°C, ΔHvap = 2260 J/g, csteam = 2.01 J/g°C)

Step 1 — Warm liquid water from 25.0°C to 100.0°C
q1 = mcΔT = (20.0)(4.184)(75.0) = 6276 J
Step 2 — Boil at 100.0°C (phase change — temperature does not change)
q2 = mΔHvap = (20.0)(2260) = 45,200 J
Step 3 — Warm steam from 100.0°C to 118.0°C
q3 = mcΔT = (20.0)(2.01)(18.0) = 724 J
Add all steps
qtotal = 6276 + 45,200 + 724 = 52,200 J ≈ 52.2 kJ
Example 3 — Thermochemical Stoichiometry

Problem: For 2H2(g) + O2(g) → 2H2O(l), ΔH = −572 kJ. How much heat is released when 5.00 mol H2 reacts?

Read the mole ratio from the balanced equation

2 mol H2 releases 572 kJ as written.

Scale using enthalpy as a conversion factor
5.00 mol H2 × 572 kJ2 mol H2 = 1430 kJ released
Example 4 — Hess’s Law

Problem: Find ΔH for C(s) + ½O2(g) → CO(g), given:

Reaction 1: C(s) + O2(g) → CO2(g)    ΔH = −393.5 kJ
Reaction 2: CO(g) + ½O2(g) → CO2(g)    ΔH = −283.0 kJ
Reverse Reaction 2

CO must be a product. In Reaction 2 it is a reactant, so reverse the equation and change the sign of ΔH.

CO2(g) → CO(g) + ½O2(g)    ΔH = +283.0 kJ
Add Reaction 1 and the reversed Reaction 2
C + O2 → CO2    (−393.5 kJ)
CO2 → CO + ½O2    (+283.0 kJ)

CO2 cancels. Net: C(s) + ½O2(g) → CO(g)

Sum the enthalpies
ΔH = −393.5 + 283.0 = −110.5 kJ
