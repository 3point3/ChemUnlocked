Periodic Table & Trends

The periodic table is one of science's greatest tools. Once you understand how it is organized, you can predict how any element will behave — without memorizing every fact. This unit shows you the patterns that make the table so powerful.

4.1 How the Periodic Table Is Organized

The periodic table arranges all known elements in order of increasing atomic number (number of protons). It is set up so that elements with similar properties land in the same column.

The table has two directions to know:

Periodic Table Organization: Periods and Groups
DirectionCalledHow many?What changes as you move?
↔ Horizontal rowPeriod7 periodsElements in the same period have the same outermost energy level. Atomic number increases by 1 each step from left to right.
↕ Vertical columnGroup (or Family)18 groupsElements in the same group have similar valence electron patterns. Moving down a group adds one energy level each row.
▸

Memory hook: "Period = row, Group = column." Period 2 elements all use the 2nd energy level as their outermost shell. Group 1 elements all have 1 valence electron.

4.2 Regions of the Periodic Table

The table is divided into regions based on the type of element and which sublevel is being filled.

The staircase line separates metals (left) from nonmetals (right). Elements touching the staircase are metalloids — they have mixed properties. Silicon (Si) is the classic example: it conducts electricity, but only a little.

4.3 Valence Electrons and the Group Number

Valence electrons are the electrons in the outermost energy level. They are the only electrons that participate in chemical bonding. The group number tells you exactly how many valence electrons a main-group element has.

Shortcut (main-group elements only) Valence electrons = last digit of group number
Group 1 → 1   Group 2 → 2   Group 13 → 3   Group 14 → 4
Group 15 → 5   Group 16 → 6   Group 17 → 7   Group 18 → 8 (He → 2)

Elements in the same group have the same number of valence electrons — that is exactly why they have similar chemical behavior. Sodium (Na) and potassium (K) are both in Group 1, both have 1 valence electron, and both react violently with water.

4.4 Atomic Radius

The atomic radius is roughly half the distance between two bonded atoms of the same element. It tells us how big an atom is.

Across a period (left → right): radius gets SMALLER

As you move right across a period, the number of protons increases, but the electrons are all in the same energy level. More protons pull the electrons inward more strongly. The atom shrinks.

Example — Period 3: Na (186 pm) → Mg (160 pm) → Al (143 pm) → Si (111 pm) → P (106 pm) → Cl (99 pm)

Down a group (top → bottom): radius gets LARGER

As you move down a group, each new period adds a whole new energy level. More energy levels mean electrons are farther from the nucleus. The atom grows.

Example — Group 1: Li (152 pm) → Na (186 pm) → K (227 pm) → Rb (248 pm) → Cs (265 pm)

▸

The biggest atoms are in the bottom-left corner (Cs, Fr). The smallest atoms are in the top-right corner (He, F). Picture the periodic table as a hillside — atoms get smaller as you climb toward the upper right.

4.5 Ionization Energy

Ionization energy (IE) is the energy needed to remove one electron from a neutral gas-phase atom. A high IE means the atom holds its electrons tightly. A low IE means electrons are easy to remove.

Across a period (left → right): IE gets HIGHER

More protons pull electrons in more tightly, so it takes more energy to pull one away. Noble gases have the highest IE in their period.

Exception: There is a small dip at Group 13 (B, Al) because the p-sublevel electrons are slightly easier to remove than s-sublevel electrons. There is another small dip at Group 16 (O, S) because of electron pairing repulsion.

Down a group (top → bottom): IE gets LOWER

Valence electrons are in higher energy levels — farther from the nucleus and shielded by inner electrons. They are easier to remove.

Example — Group 1: Li (520 kJ/mol) → Na (496) → K (419) → Cs (376)

▸

IE trends are exactly opposite to atomic radius trends. Smaller atom = harder to remove an electron. Larger atom = easier to remove an electron. If you know the radius trend, you know the IE trend.

4.6 Electronegativity

Electronegativity (EN) is a measure of how strongly an atom pulls shared electrons toward itself in a chemical bond. The scale runs from 0 to 4. Fluorine (F) is the most electronegative element at 3.98.

Across a period (left → right): EN increases

More protons → stronger pull on shared electrons.

Down a group (top → bottom): EN decreases

Larger atom → nucleus is farther from bonding electrons → weaker pull.

Using EN difference to classify a bond ΔEN = |EN(element A) − EN(element B)|

ΔEN < 0.4 → Nonpolar covalent (electrons shared equally)
0.4 ≤ ΔEN ≤ 1.7 → Polar covalent (electrons shared unequally)
ΔEN > 1.7 → Ionic (electrons transferred)

EN follows the same direction as ionization energy — both increase toward the upper right of the table (excluding noble gases). F is highest, Fr and Cs are lowest.

4.7 Ion Formation and Ionic Radius

When an atom gains or loses electrons to form an ion, its size changes significantly.

Metals LOSE electrons → cations (positive ions) → SMALLER than the atom

Na atom: 186 pm  →  Na⁺ ion: 102 pm  (45% smaller!)

Reason: Fewer electrons, same number of protons. The remaining electrons are pulled in tighter.

Nonmetals GAIN electrons → anions (negative ions) → LARGER than the atom

Cl atom: 99 pm  →  Cl⁻ ion: 181 pm  (83% bigger!)

Reason: More electrons, same number of protons. Electrons repel each other and spread out.

▸

Isoelectronic series: Ions with the same electron count can be compared by proton count. More protons = smaller ion. N³⁻, O²⁻, F⁻, Ne, Na⁺, Mg²⁺, Al³⁺ all have 10 electrons. Al³⁺ (13 protons) is the smallest; N³⁻ (7 protons) is the largest.

Ex 1 Identifying Period, Group, and Region

Given: Element sulfur (S), atomic number 16.  Find: period, group, number of valence electrons, and region of the periodic table.

Write the electron configuration

S (16e⁻): 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁴

The outermost occupied energy level is level 3, so S is in Period 3.

Count the outermost electrons

The 3rd energy level has: 3s² + 3p⁴ = 6 valence electrons.

Six valence electrons → Group 16 (also called the Oxygen family or chalcogens).

Identify the region

S is in the upper-right area of the table, filling p-sublevels. It is a nonmetal. It is not on the staircase, not a halogen, and not a noble gas.

Summary

Sulfur: Period 3 · Group 16 · 6 valence electrons · Nonmetal (chalcogen)

Ex 3 Using Electronegativity to Classify a Bond

Given: Bond between potassium (K) and fluorine (F).  Find: Bond type.

Look up (or recall) EN values

K: 0.82  |  F: 3.98  (F is the most electronegative element)

Calculate ΔEN
ΔEN = |ENK − ENF| = |0.82 − 3.98| = 3.16
Apply the bond-type cutoffs

3.16 > 1.7 → Ionic bond

This makes sense: K is a metal in the far bottom-left, F is a nonmetal in the top-right. Large EN differences always occur between metals and nonmetals.

Ex 4 Comparing Atom and Ion Size

Given: Sodium atom (Na) and sodium ion (Na⁺).  Find: Which is larger?

Write out what changed

Na is a Group 1 metal. It loses 1 electron to form Na⁺.

Na atom: 11 protons, 11 electrons  →  Na⁺ ion: 11 protons, 10 electrons

Think about what losing an electron does

Na loses its entire outermost energy level (3s¹). The remaining 10 electrons are all in level 2, much closer to the nucleus.

Na atom: 186 pm  →  Na⁺ ion: 102 pm

Conclusion

Na atom (186 pm) is much larger than Na⁺ (102 pm). Metals always shrink when they form cations.

Ex 5 Finding Valence Electrons from Position

Given: Phosphorus (P), atomic number 15.  Find: Valence electrons without writing out the full configuration.

Find P on the periodic table

P is in Period 3, Group 15.

Apply the shortcut

For main-group elements, valence electrons = last digit of the group number.

Group 15 → 5 valence electrons

Verify with electron configuration

P: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p³ → 3s² + 3p³ = 5 electrons in level 3 ✓
