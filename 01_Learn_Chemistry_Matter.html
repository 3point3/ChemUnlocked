Classification of Matter

Everything around you is made of matter. Learn how to classify matter into elements, compounds, and mixtures — and distinguish between physical and chemical changes and properties.

1.1 What Is Matter?

Matter is anything that has mass and takes up space (volume). All matter is made of tiny particles called atoms. Everything you can see, touch, or weigh is matter — chairs, air, water, even you.

Matter can be organized using a classification tree. At the top level, matter is either a pure substance or a mixture.

The Classification Roadmap
▸

Use this tree as a roadmap. Ask: Is it pure or a mixture? If pure, is it one type of atom (element) or more than one chemically bonded (compound)?

1.2 Pure Substances — Elements & Compounds

A pure substance has only one type of particle. Every sample has exactly the same composition and properties, including a definite melting point and boiling point.

Element Made from only one type of atom. Cannot be broken down further by chemical processes. Every element is listed on the periodic table.

Examples: Au (gold), O₂ (oxygen), Fe (iron), He (helium)
Compound Made from two or more types of atoms chemically bonded in a fixed ratio. Has completely different properties from its component elements.

Examples: H₂O (water), NaCl (table salt), CO₂ (carbon dioxide)
▸

A compound can only be broken apart by a chemical process — not a physical one. This is different from a mixture, which can be separated physically.

1.3 Reading Chemical Formulas

Chemists use formulas to show what a substance is made of. Follow these rules:

Capital letters start each element symbol. Symbols are 1 or 2 letters. The first is always capitalized: Na (sodium), Fe (iron), C (carbon).

Subscript numbers show atom count. H₂O = 2 hydrogen + 1 oxygen. H₂O₂ = 2 hydrogen + 2 oxygen. No subscript means exactly 1.

Diatomic elements exist as bonded pairs. H₂, O₂, N₂, F₂, Cl₂, Br₂, I₂ are all elements — every atom is the same type, just bonded.

State symbols go in parentheses. (s) = solid, (l) = liquid, (g) = gas, (aq) = dissolved in water.

Practice Reading: H₂O₂ (l) Hydrogen peroxide in the liquid state. Each molecule has 2 hydrogen atoms and 2 oxygen atoms bonded together. Two types of atoms bonded = compound.

1.4 Mixtures

A mixture contains two or more substances physically combined — not chemically bonded. Each substance keeps its own properties and can be separated by physical methods. The ratio of components can vary.

Homogeneous Mixture (Solution) Looks the same throughout. You cannot see individual parts — the substances mix evenly at the particle level.

Examples: salt water, air, steel, orange Gatorade, black coffee, brass
Heterogeneous Mixture You can see distinct parts, or composition differs from region to region.

Examples: salad, sand & water, chocolate chip cookies, granite, orange juice with pulp
▸

A solution is another word for a homogeneous mixture. All solutions are homogeneous, but not all homogeneous mixtures are called solutions — steel and brass are called alloys (homogeneous mixtures of metals).

1.6 Physical vs. Chemical Properties

Every substance has properties — characteristics that help identify it. Properties fall into two categories:

Physical Properties Observed or measured without changing the substance into something new.

Examples: color, odor, density, melting point, boiling point, hardness, malleability, ductility, solubility, state of matter
Chemical Properties Describe how a substance reacts to form a different substance.

Examples: flammability, reactivity with oxygen (rusting), reactivity with acids or bases, ability to decompose, corrosiveness, toxicity
▸

A physical property doesn't turn the substance into something new — you still have the same substance after you measure it. A chemical property describes what happens when a new substance forms.

1.7 Physical vs. Chemical Changes

Physical Change Form or appearance changes, but the same substance remains. Usually reversible.

Examples: melting ice, boiling water, cutting paper, dissolving sugar in water, bending a wire, evaporation
Chemical Change New substances form with different properties. Usually not reversible.

Examples: burning wood, iron rusting, baking a cake, cooking an egg, silver tarnishing, baking soda + vinegar

Because mixtures are physically combined, they can be separated by physical methods. The method depends on the properties of the substances. Compounds cannot be separated physically — they require a chemical reaction.

2.1 How to Read Particle Diagrams

In chemistry, matter is drawn as small circles to represent atoms. The color indicates the atom type; touching circles indicate bonding. Once you know the rules, you can classify any diagram.

Count how many types of particles you see. Look at every particle. Are they all the same shape and color, or are there different kinds?

All identical → Pure Substance. One atom type only → Element. Two or more atom types bonded in identical groups → Compound.

More than one type → Mixture. Evenly spread, no clumps → Homogeneous. Clumped in distinct regions → Heterogeneous.

Check spacing for phase. Packed tightly and orderly → solid. Close but random → liquid. Spread far apart → gas.

Color Key (by convention) Same-color circles = same atom type. Touching circles = bonded. Different-color circles NOT touching = mixture (not compound). Different-color circles touching = compound molecule.

2.2 Pure Substances: Elements

An element contains only one type of atom. All particles look the same. The atom may appear alone (monatomic) or bonded to an identical atom (diatomic).

In all three diagrams: only one color of atom is present → element. Pairs of same-color atoms bonded = still an element (diatomic form).

2.3 Pure Substances: Compounds

A compound contains two or more types of atoms chemically bonded together. Every molecule has the same ratio of atoms.

In all three diagrams: two (or more) colors of atoms are always bonded together in the same repeating pattern → compound. The fixed ratio and bonding are the defining features.

2.4 Homogeneous vs. Heterogeneous Mixtures

Both mixture types contain different particles that are not chemically bonded. The difference is how they are distributed.

▸

Heterogeneous test: Scoop from one region — does it look the same as a sample from another region? If no, it is heterogeneous. The key visual cue is always uneven distribution.

2.5 Side-by-Side Comparison

Study all four types together. Color tells you atom type; touching tells you bonding; distribution tells you mixture type.

▸

Quick visual checklist: One color? → Element. Different colors always in identical bonded groups? → Compound. Different colors spread evenly, not touching? → Homogeneous. Different colors in separate regions? → Heterogeneous.

Ex 1 Classifying Three Beakers

Given: Three unlabeled beakers in a lab. Find: Classify each and explain the reasoning.

  • Beaker A: Clear, uniform liquid. Boils at exactly 100°C.
  • Beaker B: Clear liquid, but has a definite salty taste. Boils above 100°C.
  • Beaker C: A white solid floating in a clear liquid.
Beaker A → Pure Substance (H₂O)

Uniform appearance and a single, fixed boiling point (100°C) indicate a pure substance. A definite boiling point is the key distinguishing feature of a pure substance vs. a mixture.

Beaker B → Homogeneous Mixture (salt water)

Looks uniform — you cannot see separate parts — so it cannot be heterogeneous. But the elevated boiling point shows it is not a pure substance. Dissolved salt raises the boiling point above 100°C. Result: homogeneous mixture.

Beaker C → Heterogeneous Mixture

You can see distinct parts — a solid phase and a liquid phase that are not uniformly mixed. The white solid could be undissolved salt, chalk, or any solid that does not dissolve. Visible non-uniform regions = heterogeneous.

▸

Exam shortcut: A definite, fixed boiling or melting point → pure substance. A boiling or melting range → mixture.

Ex 2 Identifying Physical vs. Chemical Changes

Given: Four scenarios. Find: Identify the type of change and the key evidence.

Iron fence slowly turns orange-brown and flakes away → Chemical Change (rusting)

Iron (Fe) reacts with oxygen and water to form iron oxide (Fe₂O₃) — a completely new substance with different properties (orange, brittle, flaky). The change is not reversible, and a new substance has formed.

A student cuts a copper wire into small pieces → Physical Change

Cutting changes the shape and size, but the copper is still copper — same formula (Cu), same color, same conductivity. No new substance was formed.

Table salt is added to water and stirred until it disappears → Physical Change (dissolving)

The salt seems to disappear, but no chemical reaction occurred. The NaCl and H₂O molecules are still the same. Evaporate the water and you recover the salt crystals — proof it was physical.

Baking soda and vinegar are mixed; vigorous bubbling occurs → Chemical Change

The bubbles are CO₂ gas produced in a chemical reaction between acetic acid and sodium bicarbonate. New substances (CO₂ and water) formed. The reaction is not easily reversible.

Ex 3 Reading a Particle Diagram Step-by-Step

Given: A closed container with: three pairs of touching blue circles, two groups of one large black circle bonded to two small red circles, and one lone orange circle. Find: Classify the contents.

Identify each particle type

Blue-blue pairs = diatomic element (e.g., N₂). Red-black-red groups = compound (e.g., CO₂). Lone orange = monatomic element (e.g., Ar). Total: 3 different particle types.

Pure or mixture?

There are 3 different types of particles → it is a mixture. A pure substance has only one type of particle.

Homogeneous or heterogeneous?

The particles are described as spread throughout the container without distinct layers or clumps → Homogeneous mixture.

Final answer

Homogeneous mixture of two elements (N₂ and Ar) and one compound (CO₂). This is a simplified model of air with a trace noble gas included.

Ex 4 Choosing a Separation Method

Given: Three different mixtures. Find: Select the best separation method for each and explain why.

Sand and water → Filtration

Sand does not dissolve in water — it forms a heterogeneous mixture. A filter paper will trap the sand particles while water passes through (filtrate). The sand particles are too large to fit through the pores of the filter paper.

Salt water (recover the salt) → Evaporation

The salt is dissolved and cannot be filtered. Heating drives off the water as steam, leaving solid NaCl behind in the evaporating dish. This works because salt does not evaporate at the temperatures used.

Black ink on filter paper (identify the pigments) → Paper Chromatography

The ink is a homogeneous mixture of several colored pigments. Water (mobile phase) travels up the paper (stationary phase) and carries the pigments at different speeds based on their affinity for water. The result is a series of separated color bands.
