## 2.1 Start Here: What Matter Is and How to Sort It
- Paragraph 1: Matter is anything that has **mass** and takes up **space (volume)**. If you can weigh it or it takes up room, it is matter.
- Paragraph 2: The main job in this unit is classification. Instead of guessing, you want a simple path: first decide whether the sample is a **pure substance** or a **mixture**.
- Paragraph 3: Pure substances have a fixed composition. Elements contain one type of atom, while compounds contain different atoms chemically bonded in a set ratio. Mixtures contain more than one substance without chemical bonding. Homogeneous mixtures look uniform throughout, while heterogeneous mixtures have visibly different parts or regions.

## 2.2 Pure Substances: Elements vs. Compounds

- List item 2: Do not miss this: “pure” does not mean “only one element.” A compound can still be a pure substance.

## 2.3 How to Read Chemical Formulas Without Mixing Them Up
- Paragraph 1: Chemists use formulas to show what a substance is made of. Read carefully — this is where most mix-ups between compounds and mixtures happen.
- Paragraph 2: **Capital letters start each element symbol.** Symbols are 1 or 2 letters. The first is always capitalized: Na (sodium), Fe (iron), C (carbon).
- Paragraph 3: **Subscript numbers show atom count.** H2O = 2 hydrogen + 1 oxygen. H2O2 = 2 hydrogen + 2 oxygen. No subscript means exactly 1.
- Paragraph 4: **Diatomic elements exist as bonded pairs.** H2, O2, N2, F2, Cl2, Br2, I2 are all elements — every atom is the same type, just bonded.
- Paragraph 5: **State symbols go in parentheses.** (s) = solid, (l) = liquid, (g) = gas, (aq) = dissolved in water.

- List item 2: More than one element symbol in one formula does **not** mean mixture. If the atoms are chemically bonded in a fixed ratio, it is a compound.
- List item 3: One repeating chemical formula such as H2O or CO2 names a pure substance.
- List item 4: A plus sign between substances, such as NaCl+H2O, shows a mixture of separate substances.
- List item 5: If you are confused, say the formula out loud in pieces: symbols first, subscripts second, state last.

## 2.8 Separating Mixtures: Which Method Fits Which Job
- Paragraph 1: Mixtures can be separated by **physical methods**. The method you choose depends on which physical property differs between the components.
- List item 1: Compounds cannot be separated physically — they require a chemical reaction to break apart.
- List item 2: That distinction is one of the cleanest differences between compounds and mixtures. Do not miss it.
- List item 3: Chromatography can show that black ink is really a *mixture* of different colored pigments.
- List item 4: A sample can look uniform and still be a mixture.
- List item 5: If you are stuck on a separation question, ask which physical property is different: particle size, boiling point, density, or magnetism.
- Table row 1: Method | How It Works | Best For
- Table row 2: Filtration | Filter catches large solids; liquid passes through | Solid + liquid (heterogeneous)
- Table row 3: Evaporation | Heat drives off liquid; solid left behind | Dissolved solid in liquid
- Table row 4: Distillation | Differences in boiling points separate liquids | Two liquids with different bp
- Table row 5: Chromatography | Components move at different speeds through a material | Identifying pigments / inks
- Table row 6: Magnetism | Magnet pulls out magnetic material | Iron in a mixture
- Table row 7: Centrifugation | Spinning separates by density | Particles suspended in liquid

# Examples Tab

## Example 1 · Classifying Three Beakers
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Use observations to decide whether each beaker contains a pure substance, a homogeneous mixture, or a heterogeneous mixture.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** Beaker A is a clear, uniform liquid that boils sharply at 100°C at 1 atm. Beaker B is a clear liquid with dissolved salt that boils above 100°C at the same pressure. Beaker C contains a white solid floating in a clear liquid.
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** The best classification for each beaker and the evidence supporting it.
- Paragraph 4: Uniform appearance plus one sharp boiling point indicates a pure substance. At 1 atm, a boiling point of 100°C is consistent with pure water, but the classification step here is simply **pure substance**.
- Paragraph 5: Looks uniform — you cannot see separate parts — so it cannot be heterogeneous. But the elevated boiling point shows it is not a pure substance. Dissolved salt raises the boiling point above 100°C. Result: homogeneous mixture.
- Paragraph 6: You can see distinct parts — a solid phase and a liquid phase that are not uniformly mixed. The white solid could be undissolved salt, chalk, or any solid that does not dissolve. Visible non-uniform regions = heterogeneous.
- Paragraph 7: Exam shortcut
- List item 1: At the same pressure, a sharp boiling or melting point points to a pure substance.
- List item 2: A boiling or melting range points to a mixture.

## Example 2 · Identifying Physical vs. Chemical Changes
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Decide whether each change is physical or chemical.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** An iron fence rusts, a copper wire is cut, table salt dissolves in water, and baking soda reacts with vinegar.
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** The type of change in each case and the evidence that supports it.
- Paragraph 4: Iron (Fe) reacts with oxygen and water to form iron oxide (Fe2O3) - a completely new substance with different properties (orange, brittle, flaky). The change is not reversible, and a new substance has formed.
- Paragraph 5: Cutting changes the shape and size, but the copper is still copper — same formula (Cu), same color, same conductivity. No new substance was formed.
- Paragraph 6: The salt seems to disappear, but no chemical reaction occurred. In water, NaCl separates into Na+ and Cl- ions, and the water is still H2O. Evaporate the water and the ions recombine into solid NaCl, so no new substance formed.
- Paragraph 7: The bubbles are CO2 gas produced in a chemical reaction between acetic acid and sodium bicarbonate. New substances form, including carbon dioxide gas, so this is a chemical change. The key rule is still new-substance formation, not just the presence of bubbles.

## Example 3 · Reading a Particle Diagram Step-by-Step
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Classify the sample shown in the particle diagram.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** A closed container with three pairs of touching blue circles, two groups of one large black circle bonded to two small red circles, and several lone orange circles.
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** Whether the sample is pure or a mixture, and whether the mixture is homogeneous or heterogeneous.
- Paragraph 4: Blue-blue pairs = diatomic element (for example, N2). Red-black-red groups = compound (for example, CO2). Lone orange circles = monatomic element (for example, Ar). Total: 3 different particle types.
- Paragraph 5: There are 3 different types of particles → it is a **mixture**. A pure substance has only one type of particle.
- Paragraph 6: The particles are described as spread throughout the container without distinct layers or clumps → **Homogeneous mixture**.
- Paragraph 7: Homogeneous mixture of two elements (N2 and Ar) and one compound (CO2). This is a simplified model of air with a trace noble gas included.

## Example 4 · Reading a Chemical Formula Carefully
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Read the formula Fe2O3 (s) and classify the substance.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** The formula contains Fe, O, subscripts 2 and 3, and the state symbol (s).
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** The atom counts, the physical state, and whether the substance is an element, compound, or mixture.
- Paragraph 4: Fe is iron and O is oxygen. Because the formula includes two different element symbols, the sample contains two types of atoms.
- Paragraph 5: The subscript 2 means 2 iron atoms. The subscript 3 means 3 oxygen atoms. Subscripts belong only to the symbol directly before them.
- Paragraph 6: The symbol (s) means the substance is in the solid state. State symbols tell you physical state, not classification.
- Paragraph 7: Fe and O are chemically bonded in one repeating ratio, so Fe2O3 is a **compound**, not a mixture.

## Example 5 · Choosing a Separation Method
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Match each mixture to the physical separation method that works best.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** Sand and water, salt water when you want the salt back, and black ink on filter paper.
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** The best separation method for each mixture and the property that makes that method work.
- Paragraph 4: Sand does not dissolve in water — it forms a heterogeneous mixture. A filter paper will trap the sand particles while water passes through (filtrate). The sand particles are too large to fit through the pores of the filter paper.
- Paragraph 5: The salt is dissolved and cannot be filtered. Heating drives off the water as steam, leaving solid NaCl behind in the evaporating dish. This works because salt does not evaporate at the temperatures used.
- Paragraph 6: The ink is a homogeneous mixture of several colored pigments. Water (mobile phase) travels up the paper (stationary phase) and carries the pigments at different speeds based on their affinity for water. The result is a series of separated color bands.
