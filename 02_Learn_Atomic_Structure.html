Introductory General Chemistry  ·  Unit 02

Atomic Structure

Atoms are the fundamental building blocks of all matter. This unit traces how scientists discovered the internal structure of the atom, defines the subatomic particles and how to count them, and introduces isotopes, chemical symbols, and chemical formulas — the notation system chemists use to describe matter at the atomic scale.

2.1 Dalton's Atomic Theory

In the early 1800s, John Dalton proposed the first modern atomic theory based on experimental evidence. His model made five key claims that still form the foundation of chemistry today:

Elements are made of tiny, indivisible particles called atoms.

All matter is ultimately composed of atoms, which cannot be created, destroyed, or divided by ordinary chemical means.

All atoms of a given element are identical.

Every carbon atom is the same as every other carbon atom in its chemical properties. Atoms of different elements are different from each other.

Compounds form when atoms of different elements combine in fixed, whole-number ratios.

Water is always 2 hydrogen atoms for every 1 oxygen atom — no matter the source. This explains the law of definite proportions.

In chemical reactions, atoms are rearranged — never created or destroyed.

This is the atomic explanation for the law of conservation of mass. The same atoms are present before and after any reaction; they just bond differently.

▸

We now know Dalton's theory isn't perfectly correct — isotopes mean not all atoms of an element are identical, and nuclear reactions can change atoms. But for all ordinary chemistry, Dalton's model works.

2.2 Subatomic Particles

Three key experiments in the late 1800s and early 1900s revealed that atoms are not actually indivisible — they contain smaller particles.

Key experiments that revealed electrons, protons, neutrons, and the nuclear model of the atom.
Experiment Scientist Discovery
Cathode ray tube J.J. Thomson (1897) Electrons — small, negatively charged particles in all atoms
Oil drop experiment R. Millikan (1909) Measured the fundamental charge of a single electron: −1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ C
Gold foil experiment E. Rutherford (1911) Small, dense, positively charged nucleus at the center; mostly empty space
Nuclear bombardment J. Chadwick (1932) Neutrons — neutral particles in the nucleus with mass ≈ proton
▸

Rutherford's gold foil experiment: Most alpha particles passed straight through, but a few bounced back nearly 180°. This proved the nuclear model — a tiny dense positive nucleus surrounded by mostly empty space where electrons reside.

2.3 The Nuclear Atom — Protons, Neutrons & Electrons

The modern atom has a tiny, dense nucleus containing protons and neutrons, surrounded by electrons in a much larger region of space. The nucleus diameter is about 1/100,000 the diameter of the whole atom.

Comparison of the three main subatomic particles with their symbols, charges, masses, and locations.
Particle Symbol Charge Mass (amu) Location
Proton p⁺ +1 1.0073 Nucleus
Neutron n⁰ 0 1.0087 Nucleus
Electron e⁻ −1 0.00055 Outside nucleus
Key Relationships Atomic number (Z) = number of protons
Mass number (A) = protons + neutrons
Neutrons = A − Z
Neutral atom: electrons = protons = Z
2.4 Atomic Number, Mass Number & Nuclear Symbols

Every element is defined by its atomic number (Z) — the number of protons. No two elements share the same Z. The mass number (A) is the total count of protons plus neutrons in the nucleus.

We write nuclear symbols in the form:

Nuclear Symbol Notation ᴬ_Z X   where X = element symbol, A = mass number, Z = atomic number

Example: ²³Na means sodium with A = 23, Z = 11 → neutrons = 23 − 11 = 12

We also write isotopes using a hyphen notation: sodium-23 or Na-23. Both mean the same thing.

▸

The atomic number defines the element. You can change the number of neutrons (isotopes) or electrons (ions) and still have the same element. But change the number of protons and you have a completely different element.

2.5 Isotopes and Average Atomic Mass

Isotopes are atoms of the same element (same Z, same number of protons) that differ in their number of neutrons and therefore their mass number. Most elements exist as a mixture of isotopes in nature.

Natural isotopes for hydrogen, carbon, and chlorine with their proton, neutron, and abundance data.
Element Isotope Protons Neutrons Natural Abundance
Hydrogen ¹H (protium) 1 0 99.985%
Hydrogen ²H (deuterium) 1 1 0.015%
Carbon ¹²C 6 6 98.93%
Carbon ¹³C 6 7 1.07%
Chlorine ³⁵Cl 17 18 75.77%
Chlorine ³⁷Cl 17 20 24.23%

The average atomic mass on the periodic table is a weighted average of all naturally occurring isotopes:

Average Atomic Mass Formula avg mass = Σ (isotope mass × fractional abundance)

Cl example: (34.969 × 0.7577) + (36.966 × 0.2423) = 35.45 amu
▸

Always convert percent abundance to a decimal before multiplying (75.77% → 0.7577). A common student error is forgetting this conversion, which gives an answer ~100× too large.

2.6 Ions — Atoms with a Charge

A neutral atom has equal numbers of protons and electrons. When electrons are gained or lost, the atom becomes an ion — a charged particle.

How gaining or losing electrons changes a neutral atom into a cation or anion.
Change Result Charge Name
Lose electrons More protons than electrons Positive Cation
Gain electrons More electrons than protons Negative Anion
Electron Count in an Ion Electrons = atomic number (Z) − charge

Na⁺: Z = 11, charge = +1 → electrons = 11 − 1 = 10
Cl⁻: Z = 17, charge = −1 → electrons = 17 − (−1) = 18
Ca²⁺: Z = 20, charge = +2 → electrons = 20 − 2 = 18
▸

Ions still retain the same atomic number and mass number as the neutral atom — forming an ion only changes the electron count, never the proton or neutron count.

2.7 Chemical Formulas

Chemical formulas use element symbols and subscripts to represent compounds. There are three main types:

Different types of chemical formulas and what each one shows for glucose.
Formula Type Shows Example (glucose)
Molecular formula Exact number of each atom in one molecule C₆H₁₂O₆
Empirical formula Simplest whole-number ratio of atoms CH₂O
Structural formula Which atoms are bonded to which, and how (drawn diagram)

Subscripts in formulas apply only to the atom symbol immediately to their left. Parentheses group a unit, and a subscript outside the parentheses multiplies everything inside.

Counting Atoms in Formulas Ca₃(PO₄)₂:
Ca: 3 atoms
P: 1 × 2 = 2 atoms
O: 4 × 2 = 8 atoms
Total = 13 atoms per formula unit
▸

Isomers are compounds with the same molecular formula but different structural arrangements — and therefore different properties. C₂H₆O can be ethanol (a drinkable alcohol) or dimethyl ether (a gas). Same atoms, completely different substance.

2.8 Connecting Atomic Mass to the Mole

One atomic mass unit (amu) is defined as exactly 1/12 the mass of a carbon-12 atom. This tiny unit bridges atomic and laboratory scales through Avogadro's number, the number of particles in one mole (mol).

Key Constants 1 amu = 1.6605 × 10⁻²⁴ g
Avogadro's number: Nₐ = 6.022 × 10²³ particles/mol
Molar mass = atomic/molecular mass expressed in g/mol

If carbon-12 has a mass of 12.000 amu per atom, then exactly 6.022 × 10²³ carbon-12 atoms have a mass of exactly 12.000 grams. This is why the atomic mass in amu numerically equals the molar mass in g/mol.

▸

This connection is the bridge between the microscopic world (individual atoms) and the macroscopic world (grams on a balance). It's why chemists measure in moles — covered in depth in Unit 06.

Ex 1 Finding Subatomic Particle Counts

Given: Iron-56 (Fe-56)  |  Find: number of protons, neutrons, and electrons in the neutral atom

Find the atomic number from the periodic table

Iron (Fe) has atomic number Z = 26. This means every iron atom has exactly 26 protons. The atomic number defines the element.

Read the mass number from the isotope name

The "56" in Fe-56 is the mass number A = 56. This is the total number of particles in the nucleus.

Calculate neutrons: A − Z

Neutrons = A − Z = 56 − 26 = 30 neutrons

Electrons equal protons in a neutral atom

Neutral atom → electrons = protons = Z = 26 electrons

Answer Summary Fe-56: 26 protons, 30 neutrons, 26 electrons
Ex 2 Electrons in an Ion

Given: Ca²⁺ ion  |  Find: number of electrons

Find the atomic number of calcium

Ca has Z = 20, which means a neutral calcium atom has 20 protons and 20 electrons.

Interpret the ion charge

A 2+ charge means the atom has lost 2 electrons (cation). Each electron lost increases the charge by +1.

Apply the electron formula

Electrons = Z − charge = 20 − 2 = 18 electrons

Answer Ca²⁺ has 18 electrons (same as argon — an isoelectronic noble gas configuration)
Ex 3 Calculating Average Atomic Mass

Given: Chlorine has two stable isotopes: Cl-35 (mass = 34.969 amu, abundance = 75.77%) and Cl-37 (mass = 36.966 amu, abundance = 24.23%)  |  Find: average atomic mass

Convert percent abundances to decimals

75.77% → 0.7577    24.23% → 0.2423
Check: 0.7577 + 0.2423 = 1.0000 ✓ (abundances must sum to 1)

Multiply each isotope mass by its fractional abundance

Cl-35 contribution: 34.969 × 0.7577 = 26.496 amu
Cl-37 contribution: 36.966 × 0.2423 = 8.957 amu

Sum the contributions

Average atomic mass = 26.496 + 8.957 = 35.453 amu

Weighted Average Chain
(34.969 amu × 0.7577) + (36.966 amu × 0.2423) = 35.45 amu
This matches the periodic table value for chlorine: 35.45 amu ✓
Ex 4 Molecular vs. Empirical Formula

Given: Glucose, C₆H₁₂O₆  |  Find: empirical formula and total atom count

Read the subscripts in the molecular formula

C₆H₁₂O₆ contains: 6 carbon atoms, 12 hydrogen atoms, 6 oxygen atoms
Total = 6 + 12 + 6 = 24 atoms per molecule

Find the greatest common factor of all subscripts

Subscripts: 6, 12, 6.   GCF = 6.

Divide each subscript by the GCF

C: 6 ÷ 6 = 1  |  H: 12 ÷ 6 = 2  |  O: 6 ÷ 6 = 1
Empirical formula: CH₂O

Answer Summary Molecular: C₆H₁₂O₆ (24 atoms/molecule)
Empirical: CH₂O (simplest 1:2:1 ratio)
Note: Fructose also has the formula C₆H₁₂O₆ — they are isomers
Ex 5 Counting Atoms with Parentheses

Given: Ca₃(PO₄)₂  |  Find: number of each type of atom

Count atoms outside parentheses

Ca₃: 3 calcium atoms. The subscript 3 applies only to Ca.

Apply the outer subscript to everything inside parentheses

The subscript 2 outside (PO₄) multiplies all atoms inside:
P: 1 × 2 = 2 phosphorus atoms
O: 4 × 2 = 8 oxygen atoms

Total atom count

Ca: 3  +  P: 2  +  O: 8 = 13 atoms total per formula unit
