# Concepts Tab

## 13.1 Start Here: What a Solution Is and What the Parts Are Called
- Paragraph 1: A **solution** is a homogeneous mixture. The **solute** is the substance being dissolved, and the **solvent** is the substance doing the dissolving.
- Paragraph 2: Get those three words straight now, because later calculations depend on them. Mixing up solute, solvent, and solution is one of the most common early mistakes in this unit.
- Paragraph 3: In a solution, the solute is spread evenly throughout the solvent.
- Paragraph 4: "Like dissolves like" is the key rule: polar solvents dissolve polar solutes, and nonpolar solvents dissolve nonpolar solutes.
- Paragraph 5: Particle count rule
- List item 1: Strong electrolytes (ionic compounds, strong acids/bases) dissociate 100%.
- List item 2: Weak electrolytes (weak acids/bases) dissociate partially.
- List item 3: Nonelectrolytes do not dissociate at all.
- List item 4: This distinction drives colligative property calculations.
- Table row 1: Term | Definition | Example
- Table row 2: **Solute** | Substance being dissolved (usually smaller amount) | NaCl in saltwater
- Table row 3: **Solvent** | Substance doing the dissolving (usually larger amount) | H2O in saltwater
- Table row 4: **Aqueous solution** | Water is the solvent | Most biological fluids
- Table row 5: **Electrolyte** | Solute that dissociates into ions, conducts electricity | NaCl → Na+ + Cl-
- Table row 6: **Nonelectrolyte** | Solute that stays as molecules, does not conduct | Glucose, C6H12O6

## 13.2 Solubility: Unsaturated, Saturated, or Supersaturated?
- Paragraph 1: **Solubility** is the maximum amount of solute that can dissolve in a given amount of solvent at a specific temperature.
- Paragraph 2: Do not miss the temperature part. A solution can be saturated at one temperature and unsaturated at another.
- Paragraph 3: To classify a solution, compare the amount dissolved with the solubility limit at that temperature.

**Temperature matters:** For most solid solutes in water, solubility *increases* as temperature increases. For gases dissolved in liquids, solubility usually *decreases* as temperature increases.

## 13.5 Dilution: Same Solute, Bigger Volume, Lower Concentration
- Paragraph 1: Dilution is the process of adding solvent to reduce the concentration of a solution. The number of moles of solute stays constant — only the volume increases.
- Paragraph 2: Start here if dilution problems keep fooling you. The key idea is that dilution is not a reaction. No new solute appears and none disappears.
- Paragraph 3: Initial State
- Paragraph 4: The same solute is packed into less solution, so the particles are more crowded.
- Paragraph 5: n = 6 moles

`V₁` smaller, `C₁` larger.
- Paragraph 6: Dilution is not a reaction. The solute amount stays constant, so **n1 = n2**.
- Paragraph 7: Final State
- Paragraph 8: The same six solute moles are spread through more solution, so the concentration drops.
- Paragraph 9: n = 6 moles

`V₂` larger, `C₂` smaller.
- Paragraph 10: Dilution spreads the same solute moles through a larger solution volume, so concentration decreases while the amount of solute stays constant.
- List item 1: V_2 is NOT the volume of water you added — it's the total final volume of the diluted solution.
- List item 2: If a problem says "add 450 mL of water to 50 mL of solution," the final volume V_2 = 500 mL.
- List item 3: Always ask: "Is this the total volume, or the volume added?" before plugging in.
- Label 1: Dilution Equation

## 13.6 Percent Concentration: m/m, m/v, and v/v
- Paragraph 1: Three common percent-based concentration expressions used in labs and industry:
- Paragraph 2: Notice that the denominator changes depending on the type. The percent sign is easy to remember — what it is actually comparing is where the setup breaks.
- Paragraph 3: Percent reminder

- List item 2: Solution = solute + solvent.
- List item 3: If 15 g NaCl is dissolved in 135 g water, the solution mass is 150 g.

## 13.7 Molality: Moles per Kilogram of Solvent
- Paragraph 1: Molality is moles of solute per *kilogram of solvent* (not solution). It is not related to temperature because it is based on mass, not volume — making it very important for colligative property calculations. Two very important colligative properties are freezing point depression and boiling point elevation.
- Paragraph 2: Watch out because molality looks and sounds a lot like molarity, but the denominator is different. Instead of liters of solution, molality uses kilograms of solvent. That is also why molality stays dependable when temperature changes the solution volume.

## 13.8 Colligative Properties: Count Particles, Not Chemical Names
- Paragraph 1: Colligative properties depend on how many dissolved particles are present in a given amount of solvent, not on the chemical identity of those particles.
- Paragraph 2: This is where the electrolyte idea from the start of the unit starts to matter. More dissolved particles means a larger effect, which is why the van 't Hoff factor matters.
- Paragraph 3: The three rows below show how particle count multiplies the colligative effect at the same molality.
- Paragraph 4: This is the key comparison: at the same molality, the solution that makes more dissolved particles shows the larger colligative effect.
- Paragraph 5: Why does this happen? Solute particles disrupt the escape of solvent molecules, so more energy is needed — raising the boiling point.
- Paragraph 6: This is why salt and beet solutions melt snow and ice in cold weather — the freezing point drops below 0 °C. Why does this happen? Solute particles interfere with lattice formation; the solution freezes at a *lower* temperature. New fp = 0 − ΔTf.
- Paragraph 7: Pressure needed to prevent osmosis across a semipermeable membrane. Clinically important for IV solutions.
- Paragraph 8: Adding solute reduces the fraction of solvent molecules at the surface, lowering vapor pressure. Drives both boiling point elevation and freezing point depression.
- List item 1: The van 't Hoff factor **i** = number of dissolved particles per formula unit after dissociation.
- List item 2: NaCl: i = 2 (Na+ + Cl-).
- List item 3: CaCl2: i = 3 (Ca2+ + 2 Cl-).
- List item 4: Glucose: i = 1 (no dissociation).
- Table row 1: Same molality | *i* | Relative effect size
- Table row 2: 1.0 m glucose | 1 | baseline
- Table row 3: 1.0 m NaCl | 2 | about 2× larger
- Table row 4: 1.0 m CaCl2 | 3 | about 3× larger

## 13.9 Solution Stoichiometry: Turn Concentration into Moles First
- Paragraph 1: Solution stoichiometry uses the same mole ratios as any other stoichiometry problem.
- Paragraph 2: Do not rush past the first move. The setup almost always starts with **mol = M × L** — if you skip that, the rest of the problem falls apart. Then use the balanced equation or net ionic equation to relate those moles to the substance you need.
- Paragraph 3: **For precipitation reactions, identify the insoluble product first.**
- List item 1: Mole ratios come from the balanced equation — never from molecular masses.
- List item 2: For solutions: moles = M × L before and after every stoichiometric step.
- List item 3: For limiting reagent in solution: find mol of each reactant, compare with mol ratio.

- Paragraph 3: **Find:** freezing point of the solution

## Example 4 — Precipitation Stoichiometry
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Use solution stoichiometry to find the mass of precipitate formed.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** 50.0 mL of 0.200 M AgNO3, excess NaCl, M(AgCl) = 143.32 g/mol
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** grams of AgCl precipitate
- Paragraph 4: **Balanced equation:** AgNO3(aq) + NaCl(aq) → AgCl(s) + NaNO3(aq)

## Example 5 — Reading a Solubility Curve
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Use the solubility limit to classify the mixture and predict what remains undissolved.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** 80 g KNO3 added to 100 g H2O at 40 °C; solubility = 63 g KNO3 per 100 g H2O
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** solution classification and grams of undissolved KNO3
- Paragraph 4: The solubility at 40 °C is about 63 g KNO3 per 100 g H2O. Since 80 g > 63 g, not all of the KNO3 can dissolve.
- Paragraph 5: The solution is **saturated**, and 80 − 63 = **17 g of undissolved KNO3** remains as solid at the bottom.
