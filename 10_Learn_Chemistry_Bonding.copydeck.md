## 10.1 Start Here: Why Atoms Bond and What Type of Bond to Expect
- Paragraph 1: Atoms bond because bonding lowers their total energy. In short, the bonded arrangement is more stable than the separated atoms.
- Paragraph 2: Do not start by memorizing bond types as isolated facts. Start with the element types: metal + nonmetal, nonmetal + nonmetal, or metal + metal. Then use electronegativity to support your answer, not to make your first guess.
- Paragraph 3: In introductory chemistry, you should recognize four main bonding pictures: ionic, metallic, polar covalent, and nonpolar covalent.
- Paragraph 4: Use this order when you classify bonding in introductory chemistry:
- Paragraph 5: Then use the difference in **electronegativity** values (ΔEN) to decide whether a covalent bond is nonpolar or polar.
- Paragraph 6: **Electronegativity** measures how strongly an atom attracts shared electrons in a bond. Fluorine (EN = 4.0) is the most electronegative element, while cesium and francium are among the least electronegative metals.
- Paragraph 7: For metallic bonding, the key idea is different. Metal atoms pool their valence electrons into a shared, mobile electron cloud rather than using ΔEN cutoffs.

## 10.2 Lewis Symbols: Showing Valence Electrons Clearly
- Paragraph 1: A **Lewis symbol**, or electron-dot symbol, shows an element's chemical symbol surrounded by dots. Each dot represents one valence electron.
- Paragraph 2: Start here if Lewis structures feel hard before they even start. If you cannot see valence electrons quickly, the later structure work will feel much harder than it needs to.
- Paragraph 3: Valence electrons are the electrons in the outermost energy level, and they are the ones that participate in bonding. For main-group elements, the number of valence electrons matches the group number.
- Paragraph 4: Place the first four dots one at a time on the four sides of the symbol before you start pairing them. This mirrors Hund's rule for the valence shell.
- Paragraph 5: Lewis symbols for ions
- List item 1: Cations lose electrons, so their dot count goes down
- List item 2: Na+ has 0 dots because it lost its 1 valence electron
- List item 3: Anions gain electrons, so their dot count goes up
- List item 4: Cl- has 8 dots and is shown in brackets with the charge outside
- List item 5: The ion charge tells you exactly how many electrons to add or remove

## 10.3 Drawing Lewis Structures Step by Step with NASBU
- Paragraph 1: NASBU helps you make one Lewis-structure decision at a time. First count how many electrons the atoms want. Then count how many valence electrons they actually have. From those two counts, you can find how many electrons must be shared in bonds and how many are left as lone pairs.
- Paragraph 2: Do not miss the point of this method: it keeps you from guessing where the bonds go. Use it when a structure feels messy, especially with polyatomic ions and multiple bonds.
- Paragraph 3: Quick example: for H2O, **A = 8** valence electrons and **S = 4**, so the structure has 2 bonds and 4 unshared electrons.
- Paragraph 4: Count how many electrons are needed for every atom to have a full shell. Most atoms want 8 electrons, while hydrogen only needs 2. This gives the target total for a complete Lewis structure.
- Paragraph 5: Add the valence electrons the atoms actually bring. For ions, add electrons for a negative charge and subtract electrons for a positive charge. Think of A as your real electron budget.
- Paragraph 6: Find how many electrons must be shared in bonds: **S = N - A**. These are the electrons that need to sit between atoms so everyone can get as close as possible to a full shell.
- Paragraph 7: Convert shared electrons into bond lines: **B = S2**. This tells you the total number of covalent bonds to draw. After that, sketch the skeleton by choosing a sensible center atom: hydrogen is never central, carbon is usually central when present, and halogens usually stay terminal. Then use double or triple bonds if needed so the total number of bond lines matches B.
- Paragraph 8: Find the electrons left for lone pairs: **U = A - S**. Place these electrons on outer atoms first, then on the center if any remain. After that, check common exceptions such as hydrogen, boron, resonance, and expanded octets.
- Paragraph 9: Exceptions to the octet rule
- List item 1: Hydrogen is satisfied with only 2 electrons
- List item 2: Boron is often stable with 6 electrons
- List item 3: Period 3 and heavier central atoms can have expanded octets
- List item 4: P, S, and Cl are common examples with 10 or 12 electrons around the center
- List item 5: Never force an expanded octet on a Period 2 element
- Label 1: NASBU Quick Reference

## 10.5 Resonance Structures: When One Lewis Drawing Is Not Enough
- Paragraph 1: Some molecules cannot be accurately described by a single Lewis structure. When the electrons in a molecule can be arranged in two or more equally valid structures — differing only in where double bonds and lone pairs are placed, not in which atoms are connected — those structures are called **resonance structures**.
- Paragraph 2: Notice the common mistake: redrawing atoms in different positions and calling it resonance. That is not resonance. The atoms stay fixed. Only the electrons move.
- Paragraph 3: The real molecule is not one structure or the other. It is a **resonance hybrid** — a single blended structure where the electron density is spread out (delocalized) across all the relevant bonds. The double-headed arrow (⟺) between resonance structures is not a chemical equilibrium; it means the two drawings represent the same real molecule.

- Paragraph 2: Stop here before moving to shape: a molecule with polar bonds is not automatically a polar molecule. Check the 3D shape and ask whether the dipoles cancel.
- Paragraph 3: Ask the question this way: **do the bond dipoles cancel in this shape?** If they cancel, the molecule is nonpolar. If they do not cancel, the molecule is polar.

## 10.8 Intermolecular Forces: Why Molecular Shape Affects Properties
- Paragraph 1: **Intermolecular forces** are attractions *between* molecules — they are not the covalent or ionic bonds *within* a molecule. IMFs are much weaker than intramolecular bonds, but they govern physical properties: boiling point, melting point, viscosity, and solubility. The stronger the IMF, the more energy is needed to separate molecules, and the higher the boiling point.
- Paragraph 2: This is where the bonding unit starts paying off. Lewis structure, shape, and polarity are not separate topics anymore. They help you explain why one substance boils higher, dissolves better, or interacts differently than another.
- Paragraph 3: Quick ΔEN review for IMFs
- Paragraph 4: When you identify IMFs, use this order:
- Paragraph 5: Every molecule has London dispersion forces
- Paragraph 6: London dispersion forces (LDFs) exist in **all** molecules — polar and nonpolar alike. They arise because electrons are always in motion. At any instant, electrons may be unevenly distributed, creating a fleeting **instantaneous dipole**. That temporary δ+/δ- on one molecule distorts the electron cloud of a nearby molecule, inducing an **induced dipole** in it. The two temporary dipoles attract each other momentarily.

## Example 1 · Drawing the Lewis Structure of SO2 (NASBU)
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Draw the Lewis structure for sulfur dioxide using NASBU.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** SO2
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** correct Lewis structure and resonance picture
- Paragraph 4: Follow each NASBU step explicitly. SO2 is a strong example because the count gives a mixed single/double-bond picture and leads naturally to resonance.
- Paragraph 5: S usually wants 8 electrons. Each O also wants 8. Needed total: 8 + 8 + 8 = **24 electrons needed**.
- Paragraph 6: S contributes 6 valence electrons. Two O atoms contribute 2 × 6 = 12 more. Available total: 6 + 12 = **18 electrons available**.
- Paragraph 7: Use the formula **S = N - A**. So S = 24 - 18 = **6 shared electrons**.
- Paragraph 8: Convert shared electrons to bonds: **B = S2 = 62 = 3 bonds**.

## Example 2 · Predicting Shape of NH3 using VSEPR
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Use VSEPR to predict the shape of ammonia.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** NH3
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** electron geometry, molecular geometry, and approximate bond angle
- Paragraph 4: N contributes 5 valence e^-. Three H atoms contribute 3 × 1 = 3 e^-, so the total is 8 e^-.
- Paragraph 5: Place N in the center and form 3 N–H bonds, which uses 6 e^-. The remaining 2 e^- become 1 lone pair on N.
- Paragraph 6: Steric number = bonding groups + lone pairs = 3 + 1 = **4**. Four electron groups → base geometry is tetrahedral.
- Paragraph 7: Electron geometry, counting all 4 groups, is **tetrahedral**.
- Paragraph 8: Molecular geometry, counting only the bonded atoms, is **trigonal pyramidal**. The lone pair is not named in the molecular shape, but it still affects the bond angles.
- Paragraph 9: **Reminder:** count all electron groups for electron geometry, but count only bonded atoms for molecular geometry.
- Paragraph 10: A perfect tetrahedral angle is 109.5°.
- Paragraph 11: The lone pair repels the bonding pairs more strongly, so the H–N–H angle is compressed to about **107°**.

## Example 3 · Is PCl3 Polar? (Full Analysis)
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Decide whether PCl3 is polar.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** PCl3
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** whether the molecule is polar, with reasoning
- Paragraph 4: EN of P = 2.19, EN of Cl = 3.16. ΔEN = 3.16 - 2.19 = 0.97. Since 0.97 is between 0.4 and 1.7, the P–Cl bonds are **polar covalent**. Each bond has a partial negative charge (δ–) on Cl and partial positive (δ+) on P.
- Paragraph 5: P has 5 valence electrons, and each Cl has 7, so the molecule has **5 + 3(7) = 26** valence electrons total. The Lewis structure has three P–Cl bonds and one lone pair on P. That gives 4 electron groups around P, so the electron geometry is tetrahedral and the molecular geometry is **trigonal pyramidal**.
- Paragraph 6: In a trigonal pyramidal molecule, the three P–Cl bond dipoles do **not** cancel. The lone pair changes the 3D shape, so the dipoles add to a net dipole.
- Paragraph 7: PCl3 has polar bonds, and those bond dipoles do not cancel in its trigonal pyramidal shape. Therefore, PCl3 is a polar molecule.
- Paragraph 8: By contrast, PCl5 is symmetric and is nonpolar even though its bonds are polar.
