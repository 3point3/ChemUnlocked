# Concepts Tab

## 11.1Start Here: Heat vs. Temperature
- Paragraph 1: Get this straight first: heat and temperature are related, but they are not the same thing.
- Paragraph 2: That difference matters all through this unit. It is also why a large amount of warm water can hold more total thermal energy than a tiny cup of hotter water.
- Paragraph 3: Do not miss this
- List item 1: **Heat** is energy transferred because of a temperature difference.
- List item 2: **Temperature** is a measure of the average kinetic energy of particles.
- List item 3: A large bucket of warm water can contain more total thermal energy than a tiny cup of hotter water.
- List item 4: Temperature alone does not tell you total heat because mass matters too.
- Table row 1: Idea | Meaning | Example
- Table row 2: Heat | Energy moving from hotter to colder matter | A hot metal block warms cooler water
- Table row 3: Temperature | How energetic particles are on average | 100°C water and 100°C steam have the same temperature but different total heat content

## 11.2Energy Units: Know What the Number Means
- Paragraph 1: Thermochemistry uses several related energy units. Make sure you can convert between them before solving problems.
- Paragraph 2: If you are confused on units, fix it now. A correct setup can still fail if you mix up joules, calories, kilojoules, and food Calories.
- Table row 1: Unit | Name | Common use

## 11.4Phase Changes: When Energy Changes the State, Not the Temperature
- Paragraph 1: During a phase change, temperature stays **constant**.
- Paragraph 2: Notice the trap here: if the sample is melting or boiling, the added energy is not raising temperature. It is pulling particles apart enough to change phase.
- Paragraph 3: The energy goes into breaking or forming intermolecular forces instead of changing temperature, so you must use a different equation for each type of phase change.
- Label 1: Phase-change equations
- Table row 1: Process | Energy direction | Type
- Table row 2: Melting | Absorbs heat | Endothermic
- Table row 3: Freezing | Releases heat | Exothermic
- Table row 4: Boiling or evaporation | Absorbs heat | Endothermic
- Table row 5: Condensation | Releases heat | Exothermic

## 11.5Heating Curves: Read the Segment Before You Pick the Formula
- Paragraph 1: A heating curve shows how temperature changes as heat is added. You must split every heating-curve problem into separate segments and apply the correct equation to each — never use a single equation for the whole problem.
- Paragraph 2: The curve below shows all five segments for water from ice to steam. Each segment has a label — match it to the correct equation before you calculate.
- List item 1: **Sloped segment** — temperature is rising &rarr; use q = mc&Delta;T (identify which phase is present to get the right c)
- List item 2: **Flat segment at 0°C** — melting or freezing &rarr; use q = m&Delta;H_fus
- List item 3: **Flat segment at 100°C** — boiling or condensing &rarr; use q = m&Delta;H_vap
- List item 4: **Total heat** = sum of q from every step that applies to your problem

## 11.6Enthalpy: Endothermic vs. Exothermic
- Paragraph 1: **Enthalpy change (&Delta;H)** tells you the overall heat change of a reaction at constant pressure.
- Paragraph 2: Start here if the signs keep flipping on you. The sign of ΔH is always about the **system**. If the system releases heat, ΔH is negative. If the system absorbs heat, ΔH is positive.
- Paragraph 3: On an energy diagram, &Delta;H is the vertical difference between reactants and products. E_A is the vertical rise from the reactant level to the peak.
- Paragraph 4: Section 11.7 focuses on activation energy and catalysts in more detail.
- List item 1: **&Delta;H** compares the energy of reactants and products.
- List item 2: **E_A** is the energy barrier that must be overcome to start the reaction.

## 11.7Catalysts and Activation Energy
- Paragraph 1: A **catalyst** lowers activation energy by providing an alternate reaction pathway. It does *not* change &Delta;H — the reactant and product energy levels stay exactly the same.
- Paragraph 2: Do not miss this distinction: catalysts change how hard it is to start the reaction, not where the reaction starts or ends energetically.

## 11.8Hess&rsquo;s Law and Heats of Formation
- Paragraph 1: **Hess&rsquo;s law:** because enthalpy is a state function, the overall &Delta;H for a reaction equals the sum of &Delta;H values for any set of steps that add up to that reaction.
- Paragraph 2: If this part feels abstract, focus on the procedure. Reverse, scale, and add equations carefully. The chemistry idea is that total enthalpy change depends only on start and finish, not the path in between.
- Paragraph 3: **Both methods are Hess&rsquo;s law.** Method 1 adds known thermochemical equations. Method 2 uses a table of formation reactions so the addition is built into one shortcut formula.
- Paragraph 4: **Given equations:**
- Paragraph 5: The target reaction needs **CO as a product**, but equation 2 has CO as a reactant. Reverse equation 2 and reverse the sign of &Delta;H.
- Paragraph 6: Now add equation 1 and the reversed equation 2. CO2 cancels because it appears once on each side.
- Paragraph 7: For equation problems, always ask three questions in order: **reverse?** **scale?** **cancel?** Then add the &Delta;H values only after the equations are in the correct form.
- Paragraph 8: Use standard heats of formation at 25°C from a data table.
- Paragraph 9: Use **&Delta;H°_rxn = &Sigma; n&Delta;H°_f, products &minus; &Sigma; n&Delta;H°_f, reactants**, then multiply each product formation enthalpy by its coefficient.
- Paragraph 10: Remember that O2(g) is an element in its standard state, so its &Delta;H°_f is zero.
- Paragraph 11: The negative sign means the combustion of methane is **exothermic**.
- List item 1: **Method 1: Hess&rsquo;s law with given equations** — use this when you are given full thermochemical equations.
- List item 2: **Method 2: Standard heats of formation** — use this when you are given a table of &Delta;H°_f values.
- List item 3: Elements in their standard states have &Delta;H°_f = 0 by definition.
- List item 4: Examples include O2(g), Fe(s), and C(graphite).
- List item 5: That means they contribute nothing to the Hess&rsquo;s law sum in a formation-enthalpy calculation.
- List item 6: If a problem gives full equations, use reverse-scale-add. If it gives a &Delta;H°_f table, use products minus reactants.
- Label 1: Method 2: Standard Enthalpy of Reaction
- Table row 1: Substance | &Delta;H°_f (kJ/mol)
- Table row 2: CH4(g) | &minus;74.8
- Table row 3: O2(g) | 0
- Table row 4: CO2(g) | &minus;393.5
- Table row 5: H2O(l) | &minus;285.8

## 11.9Thermochemical Stoichiometry: Using &Delta;H Like a Conversion Factor
- Paragraph 1: When a &Delta;H value is attached to a balanced equation, it applies to that reaction **as written** — for the exact molar amounts shown by the coefficients.
- Paragraph 2: This connects straight back to [Unit 09](/09_learn_chemistry_stoichiometry). The only new idea is that the balanced equation now carries an energy amount along with the mole ratio.
- Paragraph 3: Treat the enthalpy ratio like any other stoichiometric conversion factor after you convert to moles.
- List item 1: If the reaction releases heat, the enthalpy change is negative.
- List item 2: If the reaction absorbs heat, the enthalpy change is positive.
- List item 3: Always convert grams to moles first, then apply the mole ratio from the balanced equation to scale the enthalpy.
- List item 4: Do not skip the mol conversion.
- Label 1: Reading &Delta;H from a Balanced Equation

# Examples Tab

## Example 1 — Specific Heat Calculation
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Find the heat needed to warm liquid water.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** m = 125 g, T_i = 20.0°C, T_f = 68.0°C, c = 4.184 J/g°C
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** q
- Paragraph 4: m = 125 g c = 4.184 J/g°C &Delta;T = 68.0 &minus; 20.0 = 48.0°C

## Example 2 — Multi-step Heating Curve
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Find the total heat for a multi-step heating process.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** 20.0 g H2O, 25.0°C liquid → 100.0°C liquid → boil completely → 118.0°C steam
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** q_total
- Paragraph 4: **Constants:** c_water = 4.184 J/g°C, &Delta;H_vap = 2260 J/g, c_steam = 2.01 J/g°C
- Paragraph 5: Liquid water stays in the same phase while the temperature rises, so this segment uses **q = mc&Delta;T**.
- Paragraph 6: Phase change at constant temperature, so use **q = m&Delta;H_vap**.
- Paragraph 7: Steam + temperature change, so use **q = mc&Delta;T**.

## Example 3 — Thermochemical Stoichiometry
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Use a thermochemical equation to find the heat released.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** 2H2(g) + O2(g) &rarr; 2H2O(l), &Delta;H = &minus;572 kJ; 5.00 mol H2
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** heat released
- Paragraph 4: As written, 2 mol H2 releases 572 kJ, so the equation provides the heat-per-mole ratio.
- Paragraph 5: Because the reaction releases heat, the enthalpy change for this amount is **&Delta;H = &minus;1430 kJ**.

## Example 4 — Hess&rsquo;s Law
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Find &Delta;H for C(s) + &frac12;O2(g) &rarr; CO(g).
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** Reaction 1: C(s) + O2(g) &rarr; CO2(g), &Delta;H = &minus;393.5 kJ; Reaction 2: CO(g) + &frac12;O2(g) &rarr; CO2(g), &Delta;H = &minus;283.0 kJ
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** &Delta;H for the target reaction
- Paragraph 4: CO must be a product in the target reaction. In Reaction 2 it is a reactant, so reverse Reaction 2 and change the sign of &Delta;H.
- Paragraph 5: CO2 cancels. Net: C(s) + &frac12;O2(g) &rarr; CO(g)
