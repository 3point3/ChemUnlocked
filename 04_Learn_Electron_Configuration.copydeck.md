# Concepts Tab

## 4.1 Start Here: What Electron Configuration Is Really Showing
- Paragraph 1: Every atom has electrons, but they are not arranged randomly. They occupy specific allowed energies, and **electron configuration** is the shorthand chemists use to show that arrangement.
- Paragraph 2: Here is the story you are about to follow: light shows that atoms have fixed energies, Bohr gives the first energy-level model, the modern model adds shells, sublevels, and orbitals, and then electron configuration becomes a map of where the electrons go.
- Paragraph 3: Electrons can only have certain allowed energies. They do not slide continuously between values.
- Paragraph 4: Atoms absorb light to move electrons up and emit light when electrons drop back down. Those jumps reveal the pattern of allowed energies inside the atom.
- Paragraph 5: By the end, a notation like **1s2 2s2 2p4** will mean something physical: which energy levels exist, which sublevels exist inside them, and how many electrons occupy each one.
- List item 1: Electron configuration is not just a code to memorize. It is a summary of the atom's energy pattern.
- List item 2: The whole topic starts with light because light is how atoms reveal those energy patterns.
- List item 3: Do not miss this: if you understand the structure, the notation gets much easier to read.

## 4.3 Atomic Emission Spectra, Bohr, and What n Means
- Paragraph 1: When a gas is heated or electrically excited, its atoms absorb energy and electrons jump to **higher energy levels** (excited states). When those electrons fall back to lower levels, they release that energy as light — but only at specific wavelengths. This produces a **line emission spectrum**, not a continuous rainbow.
- Paragraph 2: Niels Bohr explained this by proposing that electrons can occupy only certain allowed energy levels. In Bohr's model, the number **n** labels those levels: **n = 1, 2, 3, ...**

## 4.4 Shells, Sublevels, Orbitals, and Where Electrons Actually Go
- Paragraph 1: Each main energy level, or **shell**, contains smaller parts called **sublevels**. The sublevels are named **s, p, d,** and **f**.
- Paragraph 2: Each sublevel contains **orbitals**, and each orbital can hold up to **2 electrons**. Start here with the structure: **shell → sublevel → orbital → electrons**.
- Paragraph 3: The number of sublevels in a shell equals the value of *n*. So shell 1 has only one sublevel (1s), shell 2 has two sublevels (2s and 2p), shell 3 has three (3s, 3p, 3d), and so on.

## 4.5 Shell Capacity Is Not the Same as Filling Order
- Paragraph 1: The total number of electrons that fit in each principal shell comes from adding the sublevels inside that shell together:
- Paragraph 2: **Important:** these totals tell you the *maximum capacity* of a shell. They do **not** tell you which sublevel fills next. Filling order follows sublevel energy, so 4s starts before 3d even though 3d belongs to shell 3.

- List item 6: Filling-order question: "After 3p, what fills next in this course?" Answer: 4s.

- Paragraph 4: Each orbital holds a maximum of **2 electrons**, and they must have **opposite spins** (one ↑, one ↓). You cannot place two spin-up electrons in the same orbital.
- Paragraph 5: When filling a sublevel with multiple orbitals (p, d, or f), put **one electron in each orbital first** (all with the same spin, ↑) before doubling up in any orbital. Electrons prefer to spread out and stay unpaired when they can.
- Paragraph 6: *Why?* Electrons repel each other. Spreading out into separate orbitals reduces electron-electron repulsion and lowers the atom's energy.

## 4.7 Writing Electron Configurations Step by Step
- Paragraph 1: Start with the standard filling pattern. First decide the last-filled sublevel, then write each sublevel in order and count electrons carefully. If you are confused, do not jump straight to shorthand yet.
- Paragraph 2: After the standard pattern is secure, then learn the common exceptions such as Cr and Cu.
- Paragraph 3: Work through the examples below first. Then read Example 4 for Cr and Cu once the standard pattern feels automatic.
- List item 1: Noble-gas shorthand replaces the inner electrons with the symbol of the noble gas before your element on the periodic table.
- List item 2: For example, K (Z=19) is written [Ar] 4s1 because Ar (Z=18) is the noble gas just before K.
- List item 3: The [Ar] stands for all 18 inner electrons, so you only need to write the outer electrons.
- List item 4: Common mistake: choosing the next noble gas after the element instead of the one before it.

## Ex 3 Hydrogen Energy Levels and Spectral Lines
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Use a hydrogen transition to connect energy change, wavelength, and spectral region.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** An electron in hydrogen falls from n = 4 to n = 2.
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** (a) energy and wavelength of the photon emitted and (b) whether the light is visible
- Paragraph 4: Because the problem asks for photon energy from a hydrogen transition and then wavelength, use the Bohr energy-level equation **E_n = −2.18 × 10^−18(1/n^2)**, then **ΔE = E_final − E_initial**, and finally **λ = hc/|ΔE|**.
