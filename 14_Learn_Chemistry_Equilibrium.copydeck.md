- Write equilibrium expressions (K) for homogeneous and heterogeneous reactions.
- Use the reaction quotient Q to predict which direction a reaction will shift.
- Apply Le Châtelier's Principle to changes in concentration, pressure, and temperature.
- Set up and solve ICE tables to find equilibrium concentrations.

# Concepts Tab

## 14.1 Start Here: What Chemical Equilibrium Actually Means
- Paragraph 1: Some reactions do not just run forward once and stop. Many are **reversible**, which means the products can react to form reactants again. We show that with a double arrow (⇌).
- Paragraph 2: As reactants get used up, the forward reaction slows down. At the same time, the reverse reaction speeds up because more product is available.
- Paragraph 3: When those two rates become equal, the system reaches **equilibrium**.
- Paragraph 4: Here is the part students often miss: at equilibrium, *concentrations stop changing*, but particles are still reacting in both directions. This is a **dynamic equilibrium**, not a frozen one.
- List item 1: Equal rates ≠ equal concentrations.
- List item 2: Equal rates means equal rates — not equal concentrations, not a 50/50 split.
- Label 1: General Reversible Reaction

## 14.2 Q vs K: How to Tell Which Way a Reaction Will Shift
- Paragraph 1: Start here: **Q** tells you where the system is right now, and **K** tells you where equilibrium wants that ratio to end up. If you can compare those two values, you can predict the direction of shift before doing any ICE table work.
- Paragraph 2: For the general reversible reaction aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD, use this ratio of product concentrations to reactant concentrations, each raised to its stoichiometric coefficient:
- Paragraph 3: When that ratio is calculated using *equilibrium* concentrations, it becomes the **equilibrium constant K**. For one reaction at one temperature, K has one fixed value.
- Paragraph 4: **Concrete read:** if K = 4.0 × 10^3, products are strongly favored at equilibrium. If K = 2.0 × 10^−5, reactants are strongly favored. If you are confused, read K as a snapshot of which side wins out at equilibrium.
- List item 1: A large K (≫1) means products are favored at equilibrium.
- List item 2: A small K (≪1) means reactants are favored.
- List item 3: Notice the mistake to avoid: Q tells you the current direction of shift. K tells you the equilibrium target for that reaction at that temperature.
- Label 1: Reaction Quotient and Equilibrium Constant
- Table row 1: Comparison | Meaning | Direction of Shift
- Table row 2: Q < K | Too many reactants relative to equilibrium | Forward (→) to make more products
- Table row 3: Q = K | System is at equilibrium | No net shift
- Table row 4: Q > K | Too many products relative to equilibrium | Reverse (←) to make more reactants

## 14.3 Which Substances Go Into K and Which Ones Stay Out
- Paragraph 1: In a **homogeneous equilibrium**, all species are in the same phase, such as all gases or all aqueous ions. In a **heterogeneous equilibrium**, more than one phase is present.
- Paragraph 2: The most commonly skipped rule: **pure solids and pure liquids are omitted from the K expression**. Their concentrations do not meaningfully change, so they are built into the constant itself.
- Paragraph 3: **Concrete check:** for CaCO3(s) ⇌ CaO(s) + CO2(g), both solids stay out, so CO2 is the only term that appears in K.

## 14.5 ICE Tables: Setting Up Equilibrium Problems Step by Step
- Paragraph 1: If you are confused, slow down and make the setup do the thinking for you. Use an **ICE table** only after you know which way the reaction must move. ICE stands for **I**nitial concentrations, **C**hange in concentrations, and **E**quilibrium concentrations.
- Paragraph 2: Use the balanced equation to place the correct species and exponents into K. If your equation is wrong here, the rest of the math will be wrong too.
- Paragraph 3: If Q < K, the reaction shifts right. If Q > K, the reaction shifts left. If the system starts with no products, then Q = 0, so it shifts right.
- Paragraph 4: Write the initial concentration of each species.
- Paragraph 5: If the reaction shifts right, reactants decrease and products increase. If the reaction shifts left, products decrease and reactants increase. Use the coefficients to scale each change. This is where sign mistakes usually happen.
- Paragraph 6: For each species, equilibrium concentration = initial + change.
- Paragraph 7: Substitute the equilibrium expressions into K and solve for x.
- Paragraph 8: **What a real ICE table looks like:** here is a simple setup for the water-gas shift reaction starting with only reactants present.
- Paragraph 9: Because the reaction starts with no products, Q = 0, so the system must shift right. That is why the reactants get **−x** and both products get **+x**. Notice that the signs come from the direction of shift, not from a guess.
- Paragraph 10: Every concentration in the equilibrium expression must come from the **E** row of the ICE table.
- Paragraph 11: This is the equation you would solve once the problem gives you a value for **K**.
- Paragraph 12: Before you simplify
- List item 1: If K is very small (<10⁻⁴) and initial concentrations are not tiny, you can often assume x is negligible compared to the initial concentration.
- List item 2: Always verify: x must be <5% of initial concentration.
- List item 3: Do not use the small-x shortcut unless the numbers justify it.

## 14.6 Catalysts: What Changes Faster and What Does Not Change at All
- Paragraph 1: A **catalyst** lowers the activation energy of both the forward and reverse reactions equally. That means equilibrium is reached faster, but the equilibrium position, the value of K, and the final concentrations are *not changed*.
- Paragraph 2: Keep this straight: rate is about how fast the system gets there. Equilibrium position is about where it ends up.

- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Compare Q to K to predict the shift direction.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** N2(g) + 3H2(g) ⇌ 2NH3(g), K = 6.0 × 10⁻², [N2] = 1.0 M, [H2] = 2.0 M, [NH3] = 3.0 M
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** direction of shift
- Paragraph 4: Q = 1.125 and K = 0.060, so Q > K.
- Paragraph 5: That means the mixture has **too many products compared with equilibrium**, so the reaction shifts in the reverse direction (←) until the ratio drops back to K.

## Example 3 · ICE Table — Finding Equilibrium Concentrations
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Use an ICE table to find equilibrium concentrations.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** H2(g) + I2(g) ⇌ 2HI(g), K = 49.0, [H2]_0 = 0.500 M, [I2]_0 = 0.500 M, [HI]_0 = 0 M
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** equilibrium concentrations of all species
- Paragraph 4: This tells you exactly which equilibrium-row expressions must be substituted later.
- Paragraph 5: At the start, [HI] = 0, so Q = 0.
- Paragraph 6: Since Q < K, the reaction shifts right to make more HI.
- Paragraph 7: The **column heading stays HI**. The coefficient 2 appears in the change row as **+2x** and in the equilibrium expression as the exponent on [HI].
- Paragraph 8: Take the square root of both sides (both sides are perfect squares):
- Paragraph 9: 2x = 7.00(0.500 − x) → 2x = 3.50 − 7.00x
- Paragraph 10: 9.00x = 3.50 → x = 0.389 M
- Paragraph 11: [H2] = 0.500 − 0.389 = 0.111 M
- Paragraph 12: [I2] = 0.111 M
- Paragraph 13: [HI] = 2(0.389) = 0.778 M
- Paragraph 14: Check:

## Example 4 · Le Châtelier's Principle — Pressure Change
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Predict the effect of a pressure increase using Le Châtelier's principle.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** N2(g) + 3H2(g) ⇌ 2NH3(g); total pressure increases because volume decreases
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** direction of shift
- Paragraph 4: Now count the gas moles on each side. Reactants: 1 mol N2 + 3 mol H2 = **4 mol gas**. Products: 2 mol NH3 = **2 mol gas**.
- Paragraph 5: Increasing pressure (by decreasing volume) stresses the system. To reduce this stress, the system shifts toward the side with *fewer moles of gas*.
- Paragraph 6: The reaction shifts forward (→), producing more NH3 and reducing the total number of gas molecules.
- Paragraph 7: This is why the Haber process is run at high pressure: it improves NH3 yield.
- Paragraph 8: Important check: pressure only changes the equilibrium position if the two sides have different total moles of gas. If the gas moles are equal on both sides, there is no shift, and **K still stays the same**.
