Chemical Equilibrium

Equilibrium describes the state where a reversible reaction's forward and reverse processes occur at equal rates — not a frozen state, but a dynamic balance. This unit develops the tools to describe, quantify, and predict how that balance shifts when conditions change.

13.1 What Is Chemical Equilibrium?

Many chemical reactions are reversible — the products can react to re-form the reactants. We write reversible reactions with a double arrow (⇌). As the forward reaction proceeds, it slows (reactants are consumed); as products build up, the reverse reaction speeds up. Eventually both rates become equal, and equilibrium is reached.

At equilibrium, concentrations stop changing, but molecules are still constantly reacting in both directions. This is called a dynamic equilibrium — it is not a static dead-end.

aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD
▸

Equal rates ≠ equal concentrations. At equilibrium the forward and reverse rates match, but the amounts of reactants and products can be very different from each other.

13.2 The Reaction Quotient Q and Equilibrium Constant K

For the general reversible reaction aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD, we define a ratio of product concentrations to reactant concentrations, each raised to its stoichiometric coefficient:

Reaction Quotient / Equilibrium Constant Q = [C]c[D]d/[A]a[B]b

When this ratio is calculated using equilibrium concentrations, it equals the equilibrium constant K. K is a fixed number for a given reaction at a given temperature.

ComparisonMeaningDirection of Shift
Q < KToo many reactants relative to equilibriumForward (→) to make more products
Q = KSystem is at equilibriumNo net shift
Q > KToo many products relative to equilibriumReverse (←) to make more reactants
▸

A large K (≫1) means products are favored at equilibrium. A small K (≪1) means reactants are favored. K ≈ 1 means significant amounts of both are present.

13.3 Homogeneous vs. Heterogeneous Equilibria

In a homogeneous equilibrium, all species are in the same phase — for example, all gases or all dissolved in water. In a heterogeneous equilibrium, species are in two or more phases (e.g., a solid and a gas, or a solid and an aqueous solution).

The key rule for heterogeneous equilibria: pure solids and pure liquids are omitted from the K expression. Their concentrations are constant and are absorbed into the value of K.

Example Reaction K Expression Why?
CaCO₃(s) ⇌ CaO(s) + CO₂(g) K = [CO₂] Both solids omitted
C(s) + O₂(g) ⇌ CO₂(g) K = [CO₂] [O₂] Solid C omitted
N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g) K = [NH₃]² [N₂][H₂]³ All gases included
13.4 Le Châtelier's Principle

Le Châtelier's Principle: If an equilibrium system is disturbed (stressed), it will shift in the direction that partially counteracts the disturbance and re-establishes equilibrium.

DisturbanceSystem's ResponseEffect on K
Add reactantShift forward (→), consume added reactantK unchanged
Remove reactantShift reverse (←), replace removed reactantK unchanged
Add productShift reverse (←), consume added productK unchanged
Remove productShift forward (→), replace removed productK unchanged
Increase pressure (decrease V)Shift toward fewer moles of gasK unchanged
Decrease pressure (increase V)Shift toward more moles of gasK unchanged
Increase temperatureShift endothermic directionK changes
Decrease temperatureShift exothermic directionK changes
Add catalystReaches equilibrium faster; no shiftK unchanged
▸

Temperature is the only disturbance that changes K. Everything else changes Q and the system shifts to restore K — but K itself stays fixed.

13.5 ICE Tables — Equilibrium Calculations

When we know initial concentrations and K, we can find equilibrium concentrations using an ICE table: Initial concentrations, Change in concentrations (expressed as ±x), and Equilibrium concentrations (I + C).

Step 1 — Write the balanced equation and K expression

Identify all species and their stoichiometric coefficients. These become the exponents in K.

Step 2 — Fill in the I row

Record given initial concentrations. If a product starts at zero, write 0.

Step 3 — Fill in the C row using x

Reactants lose concentration (−x, or −2x, etc.) and products gain concentration (+x, +2x, etc.), scaled by stoichiometry. If Q < K, the shift is forward.

Step 4 — Express E row and substitute into K

E = I + C for each species. Substitute into the K expression and solve for x (often requires the quadratic formula or simplification assumption).

▸

Simplification check: if K is very small (<10⁻⁴) and initial concentrations are not tiny, you can often assume x is negligible compared to the initial concentration. Always verify: x must be <5% of initial concentration.

13.6 Catalysts and Equilibrium

A catalyst lowers the activation energy of both the forward and reverse reactions equally. This means equilibrium is reached faster, but the equilibrium position (the value of K and the final concentrations) is not changed.

Think of it this way: the catalyst speeds up both lanes of a highway. Traffic gets to equilibrium faster, but the same number of cars ends up on each side.

Catalyst → faster rate, same K, same equilibrium concentrations

Ex 1 Writing the K Expression

Given: The reaction 2NO(g) + O₂(g) ⇌ 2NO₂(g)   Find: The equilibrium constant expression K.

Step 1 — Identify products and reactants with coefficients

Products: NO₂ with coefficient 2. Reactants: NO with coefficient 2, O₂ with coefficient 1.

Step 2 — Write K as [products]coefficients / [reactants]coefficients
K = [NO₂]²[NO]²[O₂]
Step 3 — Check: all species are gaseous → homogeneous equilibrium → all included

No pure solids or liquids to omit. Final answer: K = [NO₂]² / ([NO]²[O₂])

Ex 2 Comparing Q to K to Predict Shift Direction

Given: For N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g), K = 6.0 × 10⁻² at 500°C. A reaction vessel contains [N₂] = 1.0 M, [H₂] = 2.0 M, [NH₃] = 3.0 M.  Find: In which direction will the reaction shift?

Step 1 — Write the Q expression (same form as K)
Q = [NH₃]²[N₂][H₂]³
Step 2 — Substitute current concentrations
Q = (3.0)²(1.0)(2.0)³ = 9.08.0 = 1.125
Step 3 — Compare Q to K

Q = 1.125 >> K = 0.060. Since Q > K, there are too many products. The reaction shifts in the reverse direction (←) to consume NH₃ and produce N₂ and H₂ until equilibrium is restored.

Ex 3 ICE Table — Finding Equilibrium Concentrations

Given: H₂(g) + I₂(g) ⇌ 2HI(g), K = 49.0 at 450°C. Initially: [H₂]₀ = 0.500 M, [I₂]₀ = 0.500 M, [HI]₀ = 0 M.  Find: Equilibrium concentrations of all species.

Step 1 — Set up the ICE table
H₂I₂2HI

Step 2 — Substitute E expressions into K
K = (2x)²(0.500−x)² = 49.0

Take the square root of both sides (both sides are perfect squares):

2x0.500 − x = 7.00
Step 3 — Solve for x

Verify: K = (0.778)² / (0.111)² = 0.6053 / 0.01232 ≈ 49.1 ✓

Ex 4 Le Châtelier's Principle — Pressure Change

Given: The Haber process: N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g)  Find: Predict the effect of increasing the total pressure by decreasing the container volume.

Step 1 — Count moles of gas on each side

Reactants: 1 mol N₂ + 3 mol H₂ = 4 mol gas. Products: 2 mol NH₃ = 2 mol gas.

Step 2 — Apply Le Châtelier's Principle

Increasing pressure (by decreasing volume) stresses the system. To reduce this stress, the system shifts toward the side with fewer moles of gas.

Step 3 — State the result

The reaction shifts forward (→), producing more NH₃ and reducing the total number of gas molecules. This is why the Haber process is run at high pressure — it improves NH₃ yield.
