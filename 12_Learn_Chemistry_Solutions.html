Solutions

Solutions are homogeneous mixtures found throughout chemistry, biology, and industry. This unit covers how to quantify concentration, predict colligative effects, interpret solubility, and use solution stoichiometry to track what happens in solution-phase reactions.

12.1What Is a Solution?

A solution is a homogeneous mixture in which a solute (the substance dissolved) is uniformly dispersed throughout a solvent (the dissolving medium). "Like dissolves like" — polar solvents dissolve polar solutes; nonpolar solvents dissolve nonpolar solutes.

TermDefinitionExample
SoluteSubstance being dissolved (usually smaller amount)NaCl in saltwater
SolventSubstance doing the dissolving (usually larger amount)H₂O in saltwater
Aqueous solutionWater is the solventMost biological fluids
ElectrolyteSolute that dissociates into ions, conducts electricityNaCl → Na⁺ + Cl⁻
NonelectrolyteSolute that stays as molecules, does not conductGlucose, C₆H₁₂O₆
★

Strong electrolytes (ionic compounds, strong acids/bases) dissociate 100%. Weak electrolytes (weak acids/bases) dissociate partially. Nonelectrolytes do not dissociate at all. This distinction drives colligative property calculations.

12.2Solubility: Saturated, Unsaturated, Supersaturated

Solubility is the maximum amount of solute that dissolves in a given amount of solvent at a specific temperature. Three states of a solution:

Unsaturated — less solute than the maximum. More can dissolve. The solution is clear, stable.
Saturated — exactly the maximum amount dissolved at that temperature. Adding more solute results in undissolved solid at the bottom.
Supersaturated — more than the maximum is dissolved. This is an unstable, metastable state achieved by dissolving at high temperature and carefully cooling. A seed crystal or disturbance triggers rapid crystallization.

Temperature effects: For most solid solutes in water, solubility increases with temperature. For gases dissolved in liquids, solubility decreases with temperature (why warm soda goes flat).

12.3Molarity (M)

The most common concentration unit in chemistry. Molarity expresses moles of solute per liter of solution (not solvent).

Formula M = mol solute / L solution

Rearrangements you'll use constantly:

mol solute = M × L      L solution = mol / M
⚠

Common error: Volume must be in liters. Always convert mL → L by dividing by 1000 before plugging in. Also, molarity is moles per liter of solution, not per liter of solvent.

12.4Dilution — C₁V₁ = C₂V₂

Dilution is the process of adding solvent to reduce the concentration of a solution. The number of moles of solute stays constant — only the volume increases.

Dilution Equation C₁V₁ = C₂V₂
where C = molarity, V = volume (any units, but SAME units on both sides)
⚠ Critical Trap: V₂ is the FINAL volume
  • V₂ is NOT the volume of water you added — it's the total final volume of the diluted solution.
  • If a problem says "add 450 mL of water to 50 mL of solution," the final volume V₂ = 500 mL.
  • Always ask: "Is this the total volume, or the volume added?" before plugging in.
12.5Percent Composition

Three common percent-based concentration expressions used in labs and industry:

TypeFormulaUnitsUse
% mass/mass (% m/m)(g solute / g solution) × 100% (dimensionless)Solid solutes, concentrated acids
% mass/volume (% m/v)(g solute / mL solution) × 100g per 100 mLIV bags, pharmaceuticals
% volume/volume (% v/v)(mL solute / mL solution) × 100% (dimensionless)Alcohol content, mixtures of liquids
▸

Always use g of solution (not solvent) in m/m. Solution = solute + solvent. If 15 g NaCl is dissolved in 135 g water, the solution mass is 150 g.

12.6Molality (m)

Molality is moles of solute per kilogram of solvent (not solution). It is temperature-independent because it is based on mass, not volume — making it essential for colligative property calculations.

Formula m = mol solute / kg solvent
PropertyMolarity (M)Molality (m)
DenominatorL of solutionkg of solvent
Temperature dependent?Yes (volume changes)No (mass is constant)
Use forStoichiometry, reactionsColligative properties
12.7Colligative Properties

Colligative properties depend on the number of solute particles (moles), not their identity. Four main colligative properties:

Boiling Point Elevation (ΔTb)
ΔTb = Kb × m × i     Kb(water) = 0.512 °C·kg/mol

Solute particles disrupt escape of solvent molecules; more energy needed → higher boiling point.

Freezing Point Depression (ΔTf)
ΔTf = Kf × m × i     Kf(water) = 1.86 °C·kg/mol

Solute particles interfere with lattice formation; the solution freezes at a lower temperature. New fp = 0 − ΔTf.

Osmotic Pressure (π)
π = iMRT     R = 0.08206 L·atm/mol·K, T in Kelvin

Pressure needed to prevent osmosis across a semipermeable membrane. Clinically important for IV solutions.

Vapor Pressure Lowering

Adding solute reduces the fraction of solvent molecules at the surface, lowering vapor pressure. Drives both boiling point elevation and freezing point depression.

★

The van 't Hoff factor i = number of particles per formula unit after dissociation. NaCl: i = 2 (Na⁺ + Cl⁻). CaCl₂: i = 3 (Ca²⁺ + 2 Cl⁻). Glucose: i = 1 (no dissociation).

12.8Solution Stoichiometry

Solution stoichiometry uses the same mole-ratio logic as regular stoichiometry — the only difference is that moles = M × L converts concentration and volume into moles to enter the mole map.

Step 1 — Moles of known: mol = M × L
Step 2 — Mole ratio: use balanced equation coefficients
Step 3 — Convert: moles → grams (× molar mass) or → volume (÷ M)

Double-displacement (precipitation) reactions in solution:

AgNO₃(aq) + NaCl(aq) → AgCl(s) ↓ + NaNO₃(aq)
Identify the precipitate using solubility rules. Net ionic: Ag⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) → AgCl(s)
Refresher: Stoichiometric Relationships
  • Mole ratios come from the balanced equation — never from molecular masses.
  • For solutions: moles = M × L before and after every stoichiometric step.
  • For limiting reagent in solution: find mol of each reactant, compare with mol ratio.
12.9Particle Diagrams for Solutions

Particle diagrams show solute and solvent particles at the microscopic level. Key features to represent accurately:

  • Ionic solutes (NaCl): show separate Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions dispersed through water molecules.
  • Molecular solutes (glucose): show intact molecules, no ions.
  • Insoluble precipitates (AgCl): show solid clusters at the bottom, separate from dissolved ions.
  • Net ionic: spectator ions disappear; only the precipitate and reacting ions remain.

Glucose(aq) — intact molecules, no ions

Example 1 — Calculating Molarity

Problem: What is the molarity of a solution made by dissolving 11.7 g of NaCl (M = 58.44 g/mol) in enough water to make 500.0 mL of solution?

Convert grams → moles
Factor-label
11.7 g NaCl × 1 mol NaCl 58.44 g NaCl = 0.200 mol NaCl
Convert mL → L

500.0 mL ÷ 1000 = 0.5000 L

Calculate molarity
M = 0.200 mol ÷ 0.5000 L = 0.400 M
Example 2 — Dilution (C₁V₁ = C₂V₂)

Problem: How many mL of 12.0 M HCl must be diluted to make 250.0 mL of 0.500 M HCl?

Identify variables

C₁ = 12.0 M, C₂ = 0.500 M, V₂ = 250.0 mL (the FINAL volume), V₁ = ?

Solve for V₁
V₁ = C₂ × V₂ C₁ = 0.500 M × 250.0 mL 12.0 M = 10.4 mL

Procedure: measure 10.4 mL of concentrated HCl, add to a 250 mL volumetric flask, fill to the line with water.

Example 3 — Freezing Point Depression

Problem: What is the freezing point of a solution made by dissolving 25.0 g NaCl in 200.0 g water? (Kf = 1.86 °C·kg/mol, NaCl → i = 2)

Moles of NaCl
25.0 g ÷ 58.44 g/mol = 0.4277 mol
Molality
m = 0.4277 mol ÷ 0.2000 kg = 2.139 m
ΔTf
ΔTf = Kf × m × i = 1.86 × 2.139 × 2 = 7.96 °C
New freezing point
fp = 0 °C − 7.96 °C = −7.96 °C
Example 4 — Precipitation Stoichiometry

Problem: How many grams of AgCl precipitate form when 50.0 mL of 0.200 M AgNO₃ is mixed with excess NaCl solution? (AgCl M = 143.32 g/mol)

Balanced equation: AgNO₃(aq) + NaCl(aq) → AgCl(s) + NaNO₃(aq)

Moles of AgNO₃
mol = 0.200 M × 0.0500 L = 0.0100 mol AgNO₃
Mole ratio: 1 AgNO₃ : 1 AgCl
0.0100 mol AgNO₃ × (1 mol AgCl / 1 mol AgNO₃) = 0.0100 mol AgCl
Convert mol → grams
0.0100 mol × 143.32 g/mol = 1.43 g AgCl
Example 5 — Reading a Solubility Curve

Problem: At 40 °C, 80 g of KNO₃ is added to 100 g of water. The solubility of KNO₃ at 40 °C is about 63 g/100 g H₂O. Classify the solution and describe what you would observe.

Compare to solubility limit

The solubility at 40 °C = 63 g. We added 80 g. 80 g > 63 g.

Classify

The solution is saturated, and 80 − 63 = 17 g of undissolved KNO₃ remains as solid at the bottom.

What if we heat to 80 °C?

At 80 °C, solubility ≈ 170 g/100g H₂O — all 80 g would dissolve. If we then cooled carefully back to 40 °C without disturbing it, we could create a supersaturated solution.
