Chemical Bonding

In this unit you will learn how atoms form ionic and covalent bonds, how to draw Lewis structures using the NASBU method, how to identify bond polarity using electronegativity, how to draw resonance structures, and how to use VSEPR theory to predict the shape and geometry of molecules.

9.0 What You Will Learn — and What to Watch Out For

This unit covers how and why atoms bond, how to draw electron structures, and how to predict the shape and polarity of molecules. By the end, you should be able to:

  • Distinguish ionic, polar covalent, and nonpolar covalent bonds using electronegativity
  • Draw Lewis structures using the five-step NASBU method
  • Draw resonance structures and explain what they mean physically
  • Use VSEPR theory to predict electron geometry and molecular geometry
  • Explain how lone pairs compress bond angles
  • Determine whether a molecule has a net dipole moment (is polar)
▸

Common mistakes to avoid: using total electrons instead of valence electrons in Lewis structures; putting the most electronegative atom in the center instead of the least; confusing electron geometry with molecular geometry; thinking resonance structures are different molecules; and assuming any molecule with polar bonds must be polar (symmetry can cancel dipoles).

9.1 Types of Chemical Bonds

Atoms bond together because doing so lowers their total energy — bonds form when the combined energy of bonded atoms is lower than the energy of the separated atoms. There are three main types of bonds, and the key to predicting which type forms between two atoms is the difference in their electronegativity values (ΔEN).

Electronegativity is a measure of how strongly an atom attracts shared electrons toward itself in a bond. Fluorine (EN = 4.0) is the most electronegative element. Cesium and francium are the least electronegative metals.

Rule of Thumb — Predicting Bond Type from ΔEN
ΔEN = |ENatom1 − ENatom2|    If ΔEN < 0.4 → nonpolar covalent  |  0.4 ≤ ΔEN < 1.7 → polar covalent  |  ΔEN ≥ 1.7 → ionic
▸

The ΔEN cutoffs are guidelines, not hard rules. Some textbooks use 0.5 and 1.8. What matters is the trend: larger electronegativity differences lead to more ionic character. The bond between Na (EN 0.93) and Cl (EN 3.16) has ΔEN = 2.23 — strongly ionic. The O–H bond in water has ΔEN = 1.24 — polar covalent.

9.2 Lewis Symbols for Atoms and Ions

A Lewis symbol (also called an electron dot symbol) shows an element's chemical symbol surrounded by dots — one dot for each valence electron. Valence electrons are the electrons in the outermost energy level and are the only ones that participate in bonding. For main-group elements, the number of valence electrons equals the group number.

Place the first four dots one at a time on each of the four sides of the symbol (top, right, bottom, left) before pairing any. This follows Hund's rule applied to the s and p orbitals of the valence shell.

For ions, adjust the dot count. Cations (positive ions) lose electrons — Na⁺ has 0 dots (it lost its 1 valence electron). Anions (negative ions) gain electrons — Cl⁻ has 8 dots, shown in brackets with a minus sign outside. The charge tells you how many electrons to add or remove.

9.3 Drawing Lewis Structures — The NASBU Method

NASBU is a five-step method for drawing correct Lewis structures every time. Each letter stands for one step. Follow these steps in order and you will never get lost.

N
N — Count the total Number of valence electrons

Add up the valence electrons from every atom in the molecule. For ions, subtract one electron for each positive charge, or add one electron for each negative charge. This total is the number of electrons you must place in your structure — not one more, not one fewer.

A
A — Arrange atoms (least electronegative in the center)

Place the atom with the lowest electronegativity in the center of the structure. Connect all terminal (outer) atoms to the central atom with single bonds. Hydrogen is always a terminal atom — it can only form one bond. The central atom is almost never hydrogen.

S
S — Subtract the electrons used in single bonds

Each single bond you drew in Step A used 2 electrons. Multiply the number of single bonds by 2 and subtract from your total (N). The remaining electrons are the ones you still need to place as lone pairs.

B
B — Build octets on terminal atoms first (except H)

Distribute the remaining electrons as lone pairs on the terminal atoms first, giving each one 8 electrons total (count the bonding electrons too). Hydrogen is satisfied with 2 electrons total. Place any leftover electrons on the central atom.

U
U — check if the central atom's octet is Unsatisfied

Count the electrons around the central atom. If it has fewer than 8, it needs more. Convert lone pairs from terminal atoms into additional bonds (double or triple bonds) between the terminal atom and the central atom until the central atom reaches 8. Each lone pair converted becomes one additional bond, adding 2 electrons to the central atom's count.

NASBU Quick Reference
N → count valence electrons  |  A → arrange (least EN in center)  |  S → subtract bond electrons  |  B → build outer octets  |  U → unsatisfied center? → convert lone pairs to bonds
◉

Exceptions to the Octet Rule: Some atoms don't follow the 8-electron rule. Hydrogen needs only 2. Boron is stable with 6 (it is electron deficient). Elements in Period 3 and beyond (P, S, Cl, etc.) can hold expanded octets of 10 or 12 electrons because they have available d orbitals. Never force an expanded octet on a Period 2 element.

9.5 Resonance Structures

Some molecules cannot be accurately described by a single Lewis structure. When the electrons in a molecule can be arranged in two or more equally valid structures — differing only in where double bonds and lone pairs are placed, not in which atoms are connected — those structures are called resonance structures.

The real molecule is not one structure or the other. It is a resonance hybrid — a single blended structure where the electron density is spread out (delocalized) across all the relevant bonds. The double-headed arrow (⟺) between resonance structures is not a chemical equilibrium; it means the two drawings represent the same real molecule.

9.6 VSEPR Theory — Predicting Molecular Shape

VSEPR stands for Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion. The core idea is simple: electron pairs (both bonding pairs and lone pairs) around a central atom repel each other and spread out as far apart as possible. This repulsion determines the geometry around the central atom.

There are two different geometry labels for every molecule:

  • Electron geometry — the shape described by all electron pairs, including lone pairs. This is what VSEPR theory predicts first.
  • Molecular geometry — the shape described by only the atoms. Lone pairs are invisible in the molecular geometry name.
VSEPR Notation — What to Count
Steric number = (# of bonding groups around central atom) + (# of lone pairs on central atom)
Each single bond, double bond, and triple bond each count as ONE bonding group.

Lone pairs compress bond angles because lone pairs repel more strongly than bonding pairs. Going from 4 bonding + 0 LP (tetrahedral, 109.5°) → 3 bonding + 1 LP (trigonal pyramidal, ~107°) → 2 bonding + 2 LP (bent, ~104.5°), each lone pair pushes the bonds closer together. This is why H₂O has a smaller bond angle than NH₃, which has a smaller bond angle than CH₄.

9.7 Molecular Polarity and Dipole Moments

A bond can be polar (unequal sharing) without the whole molecule being polar. For a molecule to have a net dipole moment (to be a polar molecule), two conditions must both be true:

  1. The molecule must contain at least one polar bond (ΔEN ≥ 0.4).
  2. The bond dipoles must not cancel each other out — the molecule's shape must be asymmetric.

Bond dipoles are vectors. If a molecule's geometry is perfectly symmetric, the dipoles point in equal and opposite directions and the net dipole is zero — the molecule is nonpolar even though it has polar bonds.

Dipole arrows: each arrow points from δ+ toward δ− (toward the more electronegative atom)

Example 1 · Drawing the Lewis Structure of SO₂ (NASBU)

Problem: Draw the correct Lewis structure for sulfur dioxide (SO₂).

Follow each NASBU step explicitly. SO₂ is a classic example because it produces resonance structures.

N — Count valence electrons

S is in Group 16 → 6 valence e⁻. Each O is in Group 16 → 6 valence e⁻ × 2 = 12. Total: 6 + 12 = 18 valence electrons to place.

A — Arrange atoms

Compare electronegativities: S (2.58) vs. O (3.44). S is less electronegative, so S goes in the center. Connect each O to S with a single bond: O–S–O.

S — Subtract bonding electrons

We drew 2 single bonds = 2 × 2 = 4 electrons used. Remaining: 18 − 4 = 14 electrons still to place (7 lone pairs).

B — Build octets on terminal oxygens first

Each O needs 6 more electrons (it already has 2 from the bond). Place 3 lone pairs on each O: 2 × 6 = 12 electrons used. Remaining: 14 − 12 = 2 electrons → 1 lone pair goes on S.

U — Check the central atom's octet

Count around S: 2 bonding electrons (1 bond to each O) + 2 lone pair electrons = only 4. S needs 8. Convert one lone pair from one O into a double bond: S=O. Now S has: 2 (double bond) + 2 (single bond) + 2 (lone pair) = 6... still short. This is where resonance enters — S is in Period 3 and can exceed an octet. The structure with one S=O and one S–O is valid, and a second resonance structure has them swapped. The real SO₂ is the average of both, with bond order 1.5 for each S–O bond.

Example 2 · Predicting Shape of NH₃ using VSEPR

Problem: Predict the electron geometry, molecular geometry, and approximate bond angles of ammonia (NH₃).

Step 1 — Draw the Lewis structure first

N (Group 15) = 5 valence e⁻. Three H atoms = 3 × 1 = 3 e⁻. Total = 8 e⁻. Place N in center, bond 3 H atoms (3 bonds = 6 e⁻ used). Remaining 2 e⁻ → 1 lone pair on N. Structure: N with 3 N–H bonds and 1 lone pair on N.

Step 2 — Count electron groups (steric number)

Steric number = bonding groups + lone pairs = 3 + 1 = 4. Four electron groups → base geometry is tetrahedral.

Step 3 — Electron geometry vs. molecular geometry

Electron geometry (counting all 4 groups including the lone pair) = tetrahedral.
Molecular geometry (counting only the 3 atoms bonded to N) = trigonal pyramidal. The lone pair is "invisible" in the shape name but real in terms of its effect on bond angles.

Step 4 — Predict bond angles

A perfect tetrahedral angle is 109.5°. But the lone pair repels the three bonding pairs more strongly than they repel each other, compressing the H–N–H angle to approximately 107° (measured experimentally as 107.3°).

Example 3 · Is PCl₃ Polar? (Full Analysis)

Problem: Determine whether phosphorus trichloride (PCl₃) is a polar molecule. State your reasoning clearly.

Step 1 — Are the bonds polar?

EN of P = 2.19, EN of Cl = 3.16. ΔEN = 3.16 − 2.19 = 0.97. Since 0.97 is between 0.4 and 1.7, the P–Cl bonds are polar covalent. Each bond has a partial negative charge (δ–) on Cl and partial positive (δ+) on P.

Step 2 — Determine molecular geometry

P has 5 valence e⁻; each Cl contributes 1 to bond. Total = 5 + 3(7) = 26 valence e⁻. Three P–Cl bonds + 1 lone pair on P. Steric number = 4. Electron geometry = tetrahedral. Molecular geometry = trigonal pyramidal.

Step 3 — Do the bond dipoles cancel?

In a trigonal pyramidal shape, the three P–Cl bond dipoles all point from P toward each Cl (toward the more electronegative atom). They are arranged asymmetrically — not in a plane, not equal and opposite. The lone pair on P also adds electron density above the P atom. The dipoles do NOT cancel.

Conclusion

PCl₃ has polar bonds AND an asymmetric geometry. Therefore, it is a polar molecule with a net dipole moment. Compare this to PCl₅ (trigonal bipyramidal, symmetric) — that molecule is nonpolar despite having polar bonds.

Example 4 · Resonance in Carbonate Ion (CO₃²⁻)

Problem: Draw all resonance structures of the carbonate ion (CO₃²⁻) and explain what the real structure looks like.

Step 1 — Count valence electrons (including ion charge)

C = 4. Three O atoms = 3 × 6 = 18. Ion charge = 2− → add 2 electrons. Total = 4 + 18 + 2 = 24 valence electrons.

Step 2 — Apply NASBU; recognize the unsatisfied center

C in center. Three C–O single bonds (3 × 2 = 6 used). Remaining = 18 e⁻ = 9 lone pairs. Give each O three lone pairs (9 e⁻ total per O... but they each need 6 more: 3 O × 6 = 18 electrons for lone pairs). After placing lone pairs: 0 left. C has only 6 electrons — needs 8. Convert one O's lone pair to a C=O double bond. Now C has 8. ✓

Step 3 — Draw all three resonance structures

The double bond can be placed between C and any one of the three oxygens. This gives three equivalent resonance structures. They are connected by double-headed arrows (⟺), not equilibrium arrows.

Step 4 — Describe the resonance hybrid

The real CO₃²⁻ ion has all three C–O bonds of equal length (~136 pm) and equal bond order (~1.33). This is intermediate between a C–O single bond (143 pm) and a C=O double bond (123 pm). The negative charge is evenly delocalized across all three oxygen atoms — not concentrated on one. Molecular geometry: trigonal planar, 120° bond angles.
