## 3.1 Start Here: How the Atomic Model Changed
- Paragraph 1: Start with the big idea, not the dates: scientists kept changing the atomic model when new evidence forced them to. That pattern matters more than memorizing a timeline by itself.
- Paragraph 2: Each new model explained something the older model could not explain well enough.
- Paragraph 3: 1803
- Paragraph 4: **Idea:** atom = solid, indivisible sphere
- Paragraph 5: **Limit:** could not explain electrons
- Paragraph 6: 1897
- Paragraph 7: **Idea:** negative electrons embedded in a diffuse positive sphere
- Paragraph 8: **Limit:** no nucleus and no mostly empty space
- Paragraph 9: 1911
- Paragraph 10: **Idea:** tiny positive nucleus in mostly empty space
- Paragraph 11: **Evidence:** gold foil deflections
- Paragraph 12: 1913 and beyond
- Paragraph 13: **Bohr:** electrons occupy fixed energy levels
- Paragraph 14: **Quantum:** electrons are described by probability clouds, not exact paths
- Paragraph 15: **Big shift:** the atom changed from a featureless sphere to a tiny nucleus with electrons arranged by energy and probability.
- Paragraph 16: Each model replaced the one before it when new experimental evidence appeared — the nuclear model you use today came from that sequence.
- Paragraph 17: Here is what Dalton got right — and what the later models kept from his original theory.
- Paragraph 18: All matter is ultimately composed of atoms, which cannot be created, destroyed, or divided by ordinary chemical means.
- Paragraph 19: Dalton used this idea to explain why each element behaves in its own consistent way. We now know isotopes exist, but the proton count still defines the element.
- Paragraph 20: Water is always 2 hydrogen atoms for every 1 oxygen atom — no matter the source. This explains the law of definite proportions.
- Paragraph 21: This is the atomic explanation for the law of conservation of mass. The same atoms are present before and after any reaction; they just bond differently.
- Paragraph 22: That fourth point is the one that shows up in every stoichiometry and reaction unit — conservation of mass starts here.
- List item 1: We now know Dalton's theory is not perfectly correct.
- List item 2: Isotopes show that not all atoms of the same element are identical.
- List item 3: Nuclear reactions can also change one atom into another.
- List item 4: For ordinary chemistry, though, Dalton's core idea still works: matter is built from atoms that rearrange in reactions.

## 3.3 The Nuclear Atom: Protons, Neutrons, and Electrons
- Paragraph 1: The modern atom has a tiny, dense nucleus containing protons and neutrons, surrounded by electrons in a much larger region of space. Those electrons occupy structured energy levels and orbitals rather than a random blur, and that organization becomes the focus of [Unit 04](/04_learn_electron_configuration). The nucleus is extremely small compared with the full atom, which is why Rutherford's results mattered so much.
- Paragraph 2: **Do not miss this:** most of the counting in this unit comes from only four relationships. If you are confused, master those before moving on.
- Paragraph 3: The one students miss most often: neutrons. You do not read neutrons from the periodic table — you calculate them as A − Z.
- List item 1: [Unit 04](/04_learn_electron_configuration) builds directly from this section into how electrons are arranged.
- List item 2: [Unit 05](/05_learn_periodic_table_and_trends) shows how the nucleus and electron arrangement together drive atomic size, ion size, and periodic trends.

## 3.4 Atomic Number, Mass Number, and Nuclear Symbols
- Paragraph 1: Every element is defined by its **atomic number (Z)**, which is the number of protons. No two elements share the same Z. The **mass number (A)** is the total count of protons plus neutrons in the nucleus. Keep the jobs separate: Z identifies the element, A identifies the isotope.
- Paragraph 2: We write nuclear symbols in the form:
- Paragraph 3: We also write isotopes using a hyphen notation: **sodium-23** or **Na-23**. Both mean the same thing.
- List item 1: The atomic number *defines* the element.
- List item 2: You can change the number of neutrons (isotopes) or electrons (ions) and still have the same element.
- List item 3: But change the number of protons and you have a completely different element.
- Label 1: Nuclear Symbol Notation

## 3.5 Isotopes and Average Atomic Mass
- Paragraph 1: Isotopes are atoms of the **same element** with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons. That changes the mass number, not the identity of the element. Most elements exist as a mixture of isotopes in nature, which is why periodic-table atomic masses are usually decimals.
- Paragraph 2: Before calculating, make a quick prediction: the average atomic mass must fall **between** the isotope masses and be **closer to the more abundant isotope**. This prediction step catches most arithmetic mistakes before they cost you points.
- Paragraph 3: The **average atomic mass** on the periodic table is a weighted average of all naturally occurring isotopes:
- List item 1: Always convert percent abundance to a decimal before multiplying (75.77% → 0.7577).
- List item 2: Forget this conversion and the answer comes out about 100 times too large.
- List item 3: If your answer is not between the isotope masses, stop. Something went wrong.

## 3.6 Ions: Atoms with a Charge
- Paragraph 1: A neutral atom has equal numbers of protons and electrons. When electrons are gained or lost, the atom becomes an **ion**, a charged particle. Start here with the simplest rule: protons stay the same, electrons change.
- Paragraph 2: Notice the sign on Cl-: you are subtracting a negative number, so the electron count goes up, not down. That is where most arithmetic mistakes happen.
- List item 1: Ions still retain the same atomic number and mass number as the neutral atom.
- List item 2: Forming an ion changes the electron count, never the proton count.
- List item 3: Common mistake: subtracting charge from neutrons or changing protons. Do not do that.
- Label 1: The Electron Count Rule
- Table row 1: Change | Result | Charge | Name
- Table row 2: Lose electrons | More protons than electrons | Positive | Cation
- Table row 3: Gain electrons | More electrons than protons | Negative | Anion

## 3.7 Chemical Formulas: Count Atoms Carefully
- Paragraph 1: Chemical formulas use element symbols and subscripts to represent compounds. Focus on two questions: **which atoms are present** and **how many of each**. If you are confused here, fix it now — formula reading shows up again in nomenclature, moles, and stoichiometry.
- List item 1: Subscripts apply only to the atom symbol immediately to their left.
- List item 2: Parentheses group a unit — a subscript outside the parentheses multiplies every atom inside.
- List item 3: Molecular formula means the full count of atoms in one molecule or formula unit.
- List item 4: Empirical formula means the simplest whole-number ratio.
- List item 5: Always count atoms from the full formula before you simplify to the empirical formula.
- Label 1: Counting Atoms in Formulas
- Table row 1: Formula Type | Shows | Example (glucose)
- Table row 2: **Molecular formula** | Exact number of each atom in one molecule | C6H12O6
- Table row 3: **Empirical formula** | Simplest whole-number ratio of atoms | CH2O

## 3.8 Connecting Atomic Mass to the Mole
- Paragraph 1: One atomic mass unit (amu) is defined as exactly 1/12 the mass of a carbon-12 atom. This bridges atomic and laboratory scales through Avogadro's number. This section is a preview of mole chemistry — the full lesson is in [Unit 07](/07_learn_chemistry_moles).
- List item 1: If carbon-12 has a mass of 12.000 amu per atom, then 6.022 × 1023 carbon-12 atoms have a mass of exactly 12.000 grams — this is why atomic mass in amu numerically equals molar mass in g/mol.
- List item 2: When you use molar mass in calculations, the number comes from the periodic table atomic mass. Now you know why.
- List item 3: This connection is the bridge between the microscopic world of individual atoms and the macroscopic world of grams on a balance.
- List item 4: It is why chemists measure in moles, which you will build fully in [Unit 07](/07_learn_chemistry_moles).
- Label 1: Key Constants

# Examples Tab

## Example 1 · Finding Subatomic Particle Counts
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Determine the particle counts in a neutral isotope.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** Iron-56 (Fe-56)
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** number of protons, neutrons, and electrons in the neutral atom
- Paragraph 4: For any isotope, use these rules first: protons = Z, neutrons = A − Z, and for a neutral atom electrons = Z. For Fe-56, the periodic table gives Z = 26 and the isotope name gives A = 56.
- Paragraph 5: The "56" in Fe-56 is the mass number A = 56. This means the nucleus contains 56 total nucleons: protons + neutrons.
- Paragraph 6: Neutrons = A − Z = 56 − 26 = **30 neutrons**
- Paragraph 7: Neutral atom → electrons = protons = Z = **26 electrons**
- Label 1: Answer Summary

## Example 2 · Electrons in an Ion
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Find the electron count for a positive ion.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** Ca2+ ion
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** number of electrons
- Paragraph 4: For an ion, use **electrons = Z − charge**. Calcium has atomic number Z = 20, so a neutral calcium atom would start with 20 electrons before the charge is applied.
- Paragraph 5: A 2+ charge means the atom has lost 2 electrons (cation). Each electron lost increases the charge by +1.
- Paragraph 6: Electrons = Z − charge = 20 − 2 = **18 electrons**
- Label 1: Answer

## Example 3 · Calculating Average Atomic Mass
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Calculate a weighted average from isotope data.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** Chlorine has two stable isotopes: Cl-35 (mass = 34.969 amu, abundance = 75.77%) and Cl-37 (mass = 36.966 amu, abundance = 24.23%)
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** average atomic mass
- Paragraph 4: Use **average atomic mass = Σ (isotope mass × fractional abundance)**. Before substituting, predict the result: it must be between 34.969 amu and 36.966 amu, and it should be closer to Cl-35 because Cl-35 is more abundant.
- Paragraph 5: 75.77% → 0.7577 24.23% → 0.2423

Check: 0.7577 + 0.2423 = 1.0000 ✓ (abundances must sum to 1)
- Paragraph 6: Cl-35 contribution: 34.969 × 0.7577 = 26.496 amu

Cl-37 contribution: 36.966 × 0.2423 = 8.957 amu
- Paragraph 7: Average atomic mass = 26.496 + 8.957 = **35.453 amu**

## Example 4 · Molecular vs. Empirical Formula
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Compare the full molecular formula to the simplest whole-number ratio.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** Glucose, C6H12O6
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** empirical formula and total atom count
- Paragraph 4: The molecular formula gives the full atom counts, and the empirical formula gives the simplest whole-number ratio. So first read the full subscripts, then reduce them only after the total counts are clear.
- Paragraph 5: C6H12O6 contains: 6 carbon atoms, 12 hydrogen atoms, 6 oxygen atoms.

Total = 6 + 12 + 6 = **24 atoms per molecule**
- Paragraph 6: Subscripts: 6, 12, 6. GCF = 6.
- Paragraph 7: C: 6 ÷ 6 = 1 | H: 12 ÷ 6 = 2 | O: 6 ÷ 6 = 1

Empirical formula: **CH2O**
- Label 1: Answer Summary

## Example 5 · Counting Atoms with Parentheses
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Count atoms when a grouped unit has an outside subscript.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** Ca3(PO4)2
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** number of each type of atom
- Paragraph 4: In a formula with parentheses, the outside subscript multiplies every atom inside the parentheses. Count any atoms outside the parentheses normally, then multiply the grouped unit.
- Paragraph 5: Ca3: 3 calcium atoms. The subscript 3 applies only to Ca.
- Paragraph 6: The subscript 2 outside (PO4) multiplies all atoms inside:

P: 1 × 2 = 2 phosphorus atoms

O: 4 × 2 = 8 oxygen atoms
- Paragraph 7: Ca: 3 + P: 2 + O: 8 = **13 atoms total per formula unit**
