How to Study Smarter, Not Longer

Most students study the wrong way — not because they're lazy, but because the strategies that feel most effective are often the ones that work least well. Here's what the research actually says.

⊕ ChemUnlocked Study Skills ◷ 6 min read ✦ Research-backed

There's a persistent belief among students that the quality of studying is measured in hours. Study longer, score higher. It's intuitive — and almost entirely wrong. Decades of cognitive science research point to a different conclusion: how you study matters far more than how long you sit with your notes.

The strategies below feel less comfortable than re-reading your notes or highlighting your textbook. That discomfort is actually a sign they're working — your brain is being asked to do something genuinely effortful. Effortful learning sticks.

✗ Common Myth
Re-reading your notes and highlighting key terms is the most reliable way to prepare for a test.
✓ What Research Shows
Re-reading produces an illusion of mastery. Recognition is not the same as recall. Practice retrieval instead.

Strategy 1: Interleave Your Subjects

Most students study one topic at a time until they feel comfortable with it, then move to the next. This is called blocking, and it feels natural because progress seems obvious. When you do ten mole conversion problems in a row, the tenth one is easier than the first — so it feels like learning.

The problem is that your brain adapts to the pattern, not the content. When the test arrives with problems in a different order — and they always do — that fluency disappears.

Strategy 01

Mix It Up Deliberately

Instead of studying all of chapter 6 before touching chapter 5, interleave them. Do three mole conversion problems, then two percent composition problems, then back to conversions. This forces your brain to retrieve the right method for each problem type — exactly what a test demands. It feels harder. It works better.

◈ Research Note
A study published in Psychological Science found that students who interleaved practice problems significantly outperformed those who used blocked practice on a final test — even though the blocked-practice students felt more confident during studying. The advantage held across subjects and age groups.

Strategy 2: Study in More Than One Place

This one surprises students every time: the physical environment where you study becomes part of the memory. If you always study chemistry at the same desk in the same room, some of your recall is anchored to that room. When you sit in a different room — like a test room — some of that anchor is gone.

Strategy 02

Vary Your Study Locations

Study the same material in two or three different places across the week. Your kitchen table, the library, a coffee shop, an empty classroom. Each new environment forces your brain to encode the material more independently — without environmental cues — which means better recall in any setting, including a test room. This is a small change that costs nothing and has genuine experimental support.

◈ Research Note
A 1978 study by Bjork and Bjork (yes, this has been known for decades) demonstrated that varying study environments substantially improves free recall. The effect has been replicated many times since and was popularized in a New York Times article titled "Forget What You Know About Good Study Habits."

Strategy 3: Use Multiple Modalities

Your brain processes information through different systems — visual, auditory, verbal, motor. Reading activates one of those systems. Reading and saying things out loud activates two. Drawing a diagram activates a third. The more of these systems you engage, the more pathways your brain builds to the same information — and the more routes you have to retrieve it later.

Strategy 03

Read, Say, Write, Draw

When studying a concept — say, the mole map — don't just read the definition. Say it out loud in your own words. Write a summary without looking at your notes. Then draw the concept from memory (the three nodes connected by arrows, with conversion factors labeled). Each pass through the material in a different format deepens encoding. This is not busy work — it's neurologically distinct from passive reading.

Strategy 4: Explain It to Someone Else

This is one of the most powerful and most underused strategies available to any student. When you explain a concept out loud to another person — a parent, a sibling, a friend who doesn't know the material — you are forced to organize your knowledge, identify what you actually understand versus what you only think you understand, and generate language for ideas that may have only existed in your head as vague impressions.

Strategy 04

The Feynman Technique

Named after the physicist Richard Feynman, this approach is simple: take any concept you're studying and explain it as if you're teaching it to someone who has never seen it before. No jargon. No shortcuts. Plain language. The moment you get stuck — and you will get stuck — that's exactly where your understanding breaks down. Go back to your notes, fix the gap, and try again. This process is uncomfortable in the best possible way.

The Myth of "I Need More Time"

Students who struggle on tests often conclude they didn't study enough. Sometimes that's true. But more often the problem is that the time they spent was filled with low-yield activities — re-reading, passive highlighting, copying notes verbatim — that produce confidence without producing knowledge.

Two focused hours using the strategies above will outperform six hours of passive review. The goal is not to minimize study time; it's to ensure that every hour you invest is doing actual cognitive work.

▸

The most honest thing a teacher can tell you: discomfort during studying is a signal, not a problem. If studying feels easy and comfortable, it probably isn't working very hard on your memory. Productive struggle — where you're effortfully trying to retrieve or apply something — is what creates durable learning.
