Intro to Chemistry & Lab Safety

Build the foundation for all of chemistry — what chemists study, how science works, how to stay safe in the lab, and how to measure and record data with precision.

0.1What Is Chemistry?

Chemistry is the study of matter — what it is made of, how it behaves, and how it changes. Because matter makes up everything, chemistry overlaps with every other science.

▸

Central science: Chemistry connects physics (atomic structure, energy) to biology (metabolism, DNA), making it essential for medicine, materials, and environmental science.

0.2The Scientific Method

The scientific method is a systematic process for investigating the natural world. It is iterative — results often send scientists back to an earlier step. Real science is rarely a straight line.

1
Observation / Question

Notice something interesting in the natural world. Formulate a clear, testable question. Example: "Does adding fertilizer increase plant height?"

2
Background Research

Review existing literature. Understanding prior work prevents reinventing the wheel and informs experimental design.

3
Hypothesis

Write a testable, falsifiable prediction in if–then form. Example: "If plants receive 5 g of fertilizer per week, then they will grow taller than unfertilized plants."

4
Experiment

Design a controlled experiment. Change only the independent variable; measure the dependent variable; hold all other conditions (constants) the same. Always include a control group for comparison.

Variable typeDefinitionIn the example
IndependentWhat you deliberately changeAmount of fertilizer
DependentWhat you measure as a resultPlant height
ConstantsEverything else held the samePot size, soil type, water, light
ControlGroup with no treatmentPlants with zero fertilizer
5
Data Collection & Analysis

Record quantitative (numerical) and qualitative (descriptive) observations. Organize in data tables. Apply statistical analysis to determine if differences are meaningful.

6
Conclusion

Compare results to your hypothesis. State whether your results support or refute it — and why. A rejected hypothesis is still a good result; it eliminates a wrong answer.

7
Communication & Peer Review

Science is communicated publicly so others can replicate, critique, and build on the work. Peer review filters errors before results are widely accepted.

▸

Theory vs. law: A scientific theory is a well-tested, comprehensive explanation (e.g., atomic theory). A scientific law describes a consistent, observed pattern without explaining why (e.g., law of conservation of mass). Neither is a "guess."

0.3Lab Safety Rules

Laboratories are safe when everyone follows established rules consistently. The most common lab accidents involve improper use of equipment, missing PPE, and failing to read hazard labels.

Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

🥽

Safety goggles — worn whenever chemicals, heat, or glassware are in use. Regular glasses do not count.

🧤

Gloves — nitrile gloves protect skin from corrosive, toxic, or skin-absorbed chemicals. Remove before touching your face.

🥼

Lab apron/coat — protects clothing and skin from spills. Long pants and closed-toe shoes are required.

Core Safety Rules

  • Never eat, drink, or apply cosmetics in the lab.
  • Know the location of the eyewash station, safety shower, fire extinguisher, and first aid kit before starting any lab.
  • Read the entire procedure before beginning.
  • Never smell a chemical directly — waft the vapors toward you with your hand.
  • Never add water to acid — always add acid to water ("AAA: Always Add Acid").
  • Dispose of chemicals in designated waste containers, never down the sink unless instructed.
  • Report all accidents and spills to your teacher immediately.
  • Long hair must be tied back; dangling jewelry must be removed.

Hazard Symbols (GHS)

0.6Significant Figures

Significant figures (sig figs) communicate the precision of a measurement. The last digit of any measurement is always estimated — it reflects the limit of the instrument's readability.

Rules for Counting Sig Figs

RuleExampleSig figs
All non-zero digits are significant4,5234
Zeros between non-zero digits are significant4,0234
Leading zeros are NOT significant0.00472
Trailing zeros with a decimal point ARE significant2.5004
Trailing zeros without a decimal are ambiguous25002, 3, or 4
Scientific notation removes all ambiguity2.50 × 10³3

>Sig Figs in Calculations

  • Multiplication/Division: Answer has the same number of sig figs as the measurement with the fewest sig figs.
  • Addition/Subtraction: Answer is rounded to the same decimal place as the measurement with the fewest decimal places.
  • Exact numbers (counts, definitions) have unlimited sig figs and do not limit your answer.
Examples 4.52 × 2.1 = 9.492 → rounds to 9.5 (2 sig figs, limited by 2.1)
12.36 + 1.2 = 13.56 → rounds to 13.6 (tenths place, limited by 1.2)
▸

Common mistake: Do NOT round intermediate steps — carry extra digits through the entire calculation and round only the final answer.

0.7Reading a Graduated Cylinder

A graduated cylinder measures liquid volume. Liquids in glass form a curved surface called the meniscus. For most liquids (water, aqueous solutions), the meniscus curves downward (concave). Always read from the bottom of the meniscus at eye level. Reading from above or below introduces parallax error.

10 mL Cylinder
Read the bottom of the meniscus.
This reads 6.8 mL.
Estimated between 6 & 7 mL graduations
100 mL Cylinder
Each small line = 1 mL.
This reads 42.0 mL.
The trailing zero matters — record it
25 mL Cylinder
Estimate to ±0.1 mL.
This reads 18.5 mL.
Halfway between 18 & 19 mL lines
▸

Precision rule: Always estimate one digit beyond the smallest graduation. A 10 mL cylinder graduated in 1 mL increments gives readings to ±0.1 mL. The last digit is always estimated.

0.8Using an Electronic Balance

An electronic balance measures mass in grams. The key feature is the TARE function, which zeroes out the mass of a container so you measure only the sample.

Electronic Balance
Display reads 15.432 g. The TARE button zeros out the mass of a container.

Correct Balance Technique:

1
Check levelThe bubble must be centered. Adjust feet if needed.
2
Power onAllow the balance to warm up (30–60 seconds).
3
Place containerPut your weigh boat or beaker on the pan.
4
Press TAREDisplay zeros out — the container mass is subtracted automatically.
5
Add sampleCarefully add your substance. Record all digits shown.
6
Record & cleanWrite the full reading. Remove sample and clean the pan.
▸

Why tare? Taring mathematically subtracts the container mass: masssample = masscontainer + sample − masscontainer. The balance does this automatically. If you forget to tare, your mass will include the weight of the container.

Common Errors to Avoid

  • Parallax: Reading a graduated cylinder from above or below eye level gives an incorrect meniscus position.
  • Forgetting to tare: Always tare the balance with the container before adding your sample.
  • Rounding too early: Carry all digits through calculations; round only the final answer.
  • Confusing mass and weight: Mass (kg, g) is a property of matter; weight is the gravitational force on that mass.
  • Hypothesis ≠ prediction: A hypothesis must be testable and falsifiable. "I think plants will grow better" is not a hypothesis.
  • Forgetting units: A number without a unit is not a measurement.

Ex 1Applying the Scientific Method

Scenario: A student notices that plants near a south-facing window grow taller than plants near a north-facing window. She wants to investigate whether light intensity causes the difference.

Step 1 — Observation & Question

Observation: South-window plants are taller. Question: Does higher light intensity cause plants to grow taller?

Step 2 — Hypothesis

If plants receive higher light intensity, then they will grow taller than plants receiving lower light intensity, because light drives photosynthesis and growth.

Step 3 — Experimental Design

Independent variable: light intensity (measured in lux). Dependent variable: plant height after 4 weeks (cm). Constants: pot size, soil type, water volume, plant species, temperature. Control group: plants receiving minimal light (10 lux).

Step 4 — Data Collection

Measure initial height of all plants. After 4 weeks, measure final height. Calculate growth = final − initial for each plant. Calculate the average growth per light level group.

Step 5 — Conclusion

If the high-light group grew significantly more than the low-light (control) group, the data support the hypothesis. Report results, discuss sources of error (e.g., temperature variation near windows), and suggest follow-up experiments.

Ex 2Significant Figures in a Calculation

Problem: A student measures the mass of a sample as 3.21 g and its volume as 1.5 mL. Calculate the density.

Step 1 — Write the formula
density = mass ÷ volume
Step 2 — Identify sig figs in each measurement

3.21 g → 3 sig figs   |   1.5 mL → 2 sig figs

Step 3 — Calculate (keep extra digits for now)
3.21 ÷ 1.5 = 2.14 g/mL
Step 4 — Round to correct sig figs

The limiting measurement is 1.5 mL (2 sig figs). Round 2.14 to 2 sig figs:

density = 2.1 g/mL
Ex 3Reading a Graduated Cylinder

Problem: A 50 mL graduated cylinder has major markings every 10 mL and minor markings every 1 mL. The bottom of the meniscus sits halfway between the 27 mL and 28 mL lines. What is the volume?

Step 1 — Identify the graduation interval

Minor markings are every 1 mL, so you can estimate to the nearest 0.1 mL.

Step 2 — Read the bottom of the meniscus

The meniscus bottom is halfway between 27 and 28 mL → estimated position = 27.5 mL.

Step 3 — Record with correct precision
V = 27.5 mL

Three sig figs — the 2 and 7 are certain; the 5 is estimated.

Ex 4Using the TARE Function

Problem: A beaker has a mass of 87.45 g. A student places it on the balance, presses TARE, then adds salt until the display reads 14.230 g. What is the mass of the salt? What would the balance show if the student forgot to tare?

Step 1 — With TARE pressed first

After taring, the balance display = 0.000 g (beaker mass removed). Adding the salt gives:

mass of salt = 14.230 g
Step 2 — Without TARE

If TARE was skipped, the display would show:

87.45 + 14.23 = 101.68 g

This incorrectly includes the beaker mass. The recorded sample mass would be too high by 87.45 g.

Step 3 — Sig figs on the answer

The balance reads to 0.001 g (3 decimal places), so the answer is reported as 14.230 g — the trailing zero is significant because the balance resolved it.
