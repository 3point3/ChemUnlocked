Introductory General Chemistry  ·  Unit 06

The Mole & Molar Relationships

In this unit you will learn how chemists count atoms using the mole, convert between mass, moles, and particles, calculate percent composition, and determine empirical and molecular formulas from experimental data.

6.1 What Is a Mole?

This section explains what a mole is, why chemists use it, and how Avogadro’s number connects particles to measurable amounts of matter.

A mole is simply a counting unit — just like the word "dozen" means exactly 12 of something or a "couple" means 2 of something, a mole is 6.022 × 1023 of something.

We use the mole because atoms are far too small to count individually and they would be very hard to measure until there are a huge number of them.

Avogadro's number was specifically chosen so that one mole of any element has a mass in grams numerically equal to its atomic mass. In other words, if an element has an atomic mass of 12 amu (atomic mass units), then one mole of that element will have a mass of exactly 12 grams.

6.022 × 1023
particles per mole — this is called Avogadro's Number (symbol: NA)
Scale check
If you counted one particle per second without stopping, it would take you 19 quadrillion years to count to the value of 1 mole — that is about 1.4 million times longer than the age of the universe.
Grains of sand
All of the grains of sand on every beach on Earth total approximately 7.5 × 10¹⁸ grains. That number is still about 80,000 times smaller than the value of 1 mole — showing just how enormous Avogadro's number truly is.
Why this specific number?
Avogadro's number is defined so that exactly 12 grams of carbon-12 (12C) contains exactly 1 mole of carbon atoms. This anchors the entire system of atomic masses to real, measurable masses in the lab.
n = N / Nₐ   ⟺   N = n × 6.022 × 10²³
6.2 Molar Mass

The molar mass (symbol: M) of a substance is the mass of exactly one mole of that substance, expressed in units of g/mol. For a single element, the molar mass is simply its atomic mass from the periodic table (in g/mol instead of amu). For a compound, you calculate the molar mass by multiplying each element's atomic mass by the number of times that element appears in the formula, then adding all the results together.

Moles ↔ Mass
n (mol) = m (g) / M (g/mol)    ⟺    m = n × M

6.3 The Mole Map

Every mole conversion you will ever do flows through the central unit of moles. You cannot go directly from a mass in grams to a number of particles — you must convert to moles first, and then to particles. For gases only, there is one additional conversion pathway: at Standard Temperature and Pressure (STP, defined as 0°C and 1 atm), exactly 1 mole of any ideal gas occupies 22.414 liters. This molar volume connects moles to volume, but it only applies to gases — not liquids or solids — and only at STP conditions. Like the other conversions, it still passes through the moles hub.

6.4 Percent Composition

Percent composition by mass tells you what fraction of a compound's total mass comes from each element. It is expressed as a percentage. You can calculate it from a known formula using the molar mass, or you can determine it experimentally from laboratory data (such as combustion analysis). Either way, the formula is the same:

% Composition by Mass — How to Calculate It
% element = (number of atoms of that element × atomic mass of that element) / molar mass of compound × 100

Worked example — Water (H₂O, M = 18.015 g/mol):

%H = (2 atoms × 1.008 g/mol) ÷ 18.015 g/mol × 100 = 11.19% hydrogen

%O = (1 atom × 15.999 g/mol) ÷ 18.015 g/mol × 100 = 88.81% oxygen

Notice that the percentages must add up to exactly 100% — this is a built-in check on your arithmetic.

◉

Exam Tip: Percent composition is the critical bridge between experimental laboratory data (masses collected during a combustion experiment) and the empirical formula (the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms in the compound). You will almost certainly encounter this connection on exams, so be sure you can work in both directions — from formula to percent, and from percent back to formula.

6.5 Empirical & Molecular Formulas

The empirical formula of a compound gives the simplest possible whole-number ratio of the atoms of each element present. For example, the empirical formula CH₂O means that for every 1 carbon atom there are 2 hydrogen atoms and 1 oxygen atom — but it does not tell you how many total atoms are in one actual molecule.

The molecular formula gives the actual number of each type of atom in one molecule of the compound. The molecular formula is always a whole-number multiple of the empirical formula. That multiplier is called n.

The Key Relationship Between Empirical and Molecular Formulas
Molecular Formula = n × (Empirical Formula)    where n = Mmolecular / Mempirical

To find an empirical formula from percent composition data, follow these five steps every time:

Step 1 — Convert percent values to grams by assuming a 100 g sample

When you assume you have exactly 100 g of the compound, each element's percent value becomes a mass in grams directly. For example, if a compound is 40.0% carbon, then in a 100 g sample you have 40.0 g of carbon. This trick eliminates the percent sign and gives you real masses to work with.

Step 2 — Convert each element's mass in grams to moles

Divide each element's mass by its molar mass (atomic mass from the periodic table, in g/mol). This converts grams into moles, which puts all elements on the same "counting" basis so you can compare them fairly.

Step 3 — Divide all mole values by the smallest mole value

Find whichever element has the fewest moles, then divide every element's mole count by that number. This rescales the ratios so the smallest value becomes 1.000. The resulting numbers are your preliminary atom ratios.

Step 4 — Round or multiply to get clean whole numbers

Your ratios from Step 3 should be very close to whole numbers (within ±0.05). If a ratio is close to a whole number, round it. If a ratio ends in approximately .5 (like 1.5 or 2.5), multiply every ratio by 2. If it ends in approximately .33 or .67, multiply by 3. These are the subscripts in your empirical formula.

Step 5 — Find the multiplier n to get the molecular formula (if given the molar mass)

Calculate the molar mass of your empirical formula. Then divide the given molecular molar mass by the empirical formula molar mass: n = Mmolecular / Mempirical. Round n to the nearest whole number, then multiply every subscript in your empirical formula by n to get the final molecular formula.

Example 1 · Mole–Mass–Particle Chain

Problem: How many molecules of glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) are contained in a 25.0 g sample? And what is the total mass of only the oxygen atoms in that same sample?

Road map: mass → moles → molecules (two conversion factors chained). Then a separate factor label setup for the oxygen mass question.

Step 1 — Calculate the molar mass of glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆)

Before writing any conversion factor you need the molar mass. Add the contribution of each element: 6(12.011) + 12(1.008) + 6(15.999) = 72.066 + 12.096 + 95.994 = M = 180.156 g/mol

Step 2 — Factor label chain: grams → moles → molecules

Write the given quantity first. Multiply by each conversion factor so unwanted units cancel diagonally across the fraction bar.

Factor label chain — horizontal setup
25.0 g C₆H₁₂O₆  ×  1 mol C₆H₁₂O₆180.156 g C₆H₁₂O₆  ×  6.022 × 10²³ molecules1 mol C₆H₁₂O₆  = ?
Units that cancel:  g C₆H₁₂O₆ · mol C₆H₁₂O₆
Unit left standing: molecules ✓

Calculate: (25.0 ÷ 180.156) × 6.022×10²³ = 8.36 × 10²² molecules

Step 3 — Factor label chain: find mass of oxygen in the sample
Factor label chain — horizontal setup
25.0 g C₆H₁₂O₆  ×  95.994 g O180.156 g C₆H₁₂O₆  = ?
Units that cancel:  g C₆H₁₂O₆
Unit left standing: g O ✓

Key idea: all C ends up in CO₂; all H ends up in H₂O. Use factor label chains to convert each product's mass to moles of the element. Find O by mass subtraction.
