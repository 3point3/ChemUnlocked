## 9.1 Start Here: What Stoichiometry Is Actually Doing
- Paragraph 1: The word **stoichiometry** (stoy-key-AH-meh-tree) sounds bigger than the idea really is. It is the math that tells you *how much* of each chemical is used or made in a reaction.
- Paragraph 2: This is the unit where the mole work from [Unit 07](/07_learn_chemistry_moles) and the balanced equations from [Unit 08](/08_learn_chemical_reactions) finally work together.
- Paragraph 3: Think of a recipe. If a cookie recipe says "use 2 cups of flour for every 1 cup of sugar," you can scale that ratio up or down.
- Paragraph 4: A balanced chemical equation works the same way. The **coefficients** are the fixed recipe amounts for the reaction.
- Paragraph 5: This means: **2 molecules** of hydrogen gas react with **1 molecule** of oxygen gas to make **2 molecules** of water. The ratio 2 : 1 : 2 never changes — no matter how big or small the batch.

## 9.2 Mole Ratios: The Conversion Tool That Drives the Whole Unit
- Paragraph 1: A **mole ratio** (also called a **stoichiometric factor**) is a fraction you build from the coefficients in a balanced equation. You use it like a unit conversion — it lets you switch from moles of one substance to moles of another.
- Paragraph 2: Notice what usually goes wrong here: grabbing numbers from subscripts or molar masses instead of the equation. Do not do that. The mole ratio comes only from the coefficients in the balanced equation.

- List item 1: You can flip any mole ratio upside down.
- List item 2: Pick the version that cancels the unit you want to get rid of.
- List item 3: The substance you start with goes in the denominator. The substance you want goes in the numerator.
- List item 4: This is the same factor-label method you used in the Moles unit.

## 9.4 Limiting Reactant: Which Reactant Runs Out First?
- Paragraph 1: What happens when you do not have exactly the right amounts of each reactant? One ingredient runs out first and stops the reaction. That ingredient is called the **limiting reactant** (or limiting reagent).
- Paragraph 2: Start here if you keep mixing up limiting and excess reactants. The limiting reactant is not the one with the smaller starting mass. It is the one that can make less product once the balanced equation is taken seriously.
- Paragraph 3: In chemistry, the limiting reactant controls how much product you can make. Once it is used up, the reaction stops — even if other reactants are left over.
- Paragraph 4: Compare raw grams and pick the smaller number.
- Paragraph 5: That fails because different substances have different molar masses and different coefficients.
- Paragraph 6: Convert each reactant through the balanced equation and ask how much product each could make.
- Paragraph 7: The reactant that makes less product is limiting.
- Paragraph 8: Common mistake
- List item 1: The instinct is to pick the reactant with fewer grams.
- List item 2: That is wrong — you must compare *moles* relative to the equation, not raw grams.
- List item 3: Always convert to moles first.

- Label 2: ✓ Correct comparison

## 9.5 Theoretical Yield, Actual Yield, and Percent Yield
- Paragraph 1: Once you know the limiting reactant, you know the maximum amount of product the reaction could make. That prediction is the theoretical yield. What you actually collect in the lab is usually smaller.
- Paragraph 2: Here are the three terms and what each one refers to. They build on each other — you need the theoretical yield before you can calculate percent yield.
- Paragraph 3: Actual yield goes on top because it is the amount you got. Theoretical yield goes on the bottom because it is the maximum predicted amount.
- Paragraph 4: LIMITING REACTANT & THEORETICAL YIELD STOICHIOMETRY

STEP 1: INITIAL STOICHIOMETRIC BALANCED EQUATION
4 NH3 + 5 O2 → 4 NO + 6 H2O
Given: 17.0 g | MM: 17.03 g/mol
Given: 32.0 g | MM: 32.00 g/mol
Target Product | MM: 30.01 g/mol

STEP 2: PARALLEL REACTANT PATHWAY ANALYSIS

Pathway A: NH3 Is it limiting?

1. Solve Initial Component Moles:
17.0 g NH3 × ( 1 mol / 17.03 g ) = 1.00 mol NH3

2. Extrapolate Yield Potential for Product (NO):
1.00 mol NH3 × ( 4 mol NO / 4 mol NH3 ) = 1.00 mol NO

Max Available Yield Target: 1.00 mol NO

Pathway B: O2 Is it limiting?

1. Solve Initial Component Moles:
32.0 g O2 × ( 1 mol / 32.00 g ) = 1.00 mol O2

2. Extrapolate Yield Potential for Product (NO):
1.00 mol O2 × ( 4 mol NO / 5 mol O2 ) = 0.80 mol NO

Max Available Yield Target: 0.80 mol NO

LIMITING REACTANT IDENTIFIED: O2

STEP 3: CONVERT STRUCTURAL LIMIT TO THEORETICAL YIELD MASS
0.80 mol NO × ( 30.01 g NO / 1 mol ) = 24.0 g NO (Theoretical Yield)

**This yield map shows the full limiting-reactant logic in one picture.** Convert each reactant to moles, ask how much product each one could make, choose the smaller product amount as the limiting path, then convert that product amount into the theoretical yield mass.

# Examples Tab

## Ex 1 Grams of Reactant → Grams of Product
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Convert grams of reactant to grams of product.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** 16.0 g CH4, excess O2, CH4 + 2 O2 → CO2 + 2 H2O
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** grams of CO2 produced
- Paragraph 4: **Road map:** balance/check the equation, then grams → moles → mole ratio → grams.
- Paragraph 5: Because the problem is grams of reactant → grams of product, the rule is: grams → moles → mole ratio → grams, using the balanced equation.
- Paragraph 6: For CH4: C = 12.01 g/mol | H4 = 4 × 1.008 = 4.032 g/mol
- Paragraph 7: Molar mass of CH4 = 12.01 + 4.032 = **16.04 g/mol**
- Paragraph 8: For CO2: C = 12.01 g/mol | O2 = 2 × 16.00 = 32.00 g/mol
- Paragraph 9: Molar mass of CO2 = 12.01 + 32.00 = **44.01 g/mol**
- Paragraph 10: Coefficients: 1 mol CH4 produces 1 mol CO2 → mole ratio = **1 mol CO21 mol CH4**
- Paragraph 11: Burning 16.0 g of methane produces **43.9 g of CO2**.

## Ex 2 Moles of Reactant → Moles of Product
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Convert moles of reactant to moles of product.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** 0.500 mol N2, excess H2, N2 + 3 H2 → 2 NH3
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** moles of NH3 produced
- Paragraph 4: **Road map:** because the problem already starts in moles, skip molar-mass work and go straight to the mole ratio.
- Paragraph 5: From the equation: 1 mol N2 produces 2 mol NH3

Mole ratio = **2 mol NH31 mol N2**
- Paragraph 6: 0.500 mol of N2 produces **1.00 mol of NH3**.

## Ex 3 Finding the Limiting Reactant
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Identify the limiting reactant and calculate the product mass.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** 5.00 g H2 and 24.0 g O2, 2 H2 + O2 → 2 H2O
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** limiting reactant and grams of H2O formed
- Paragraph 4: **Road map:** convert both reactants to moles, test each one separately, then keep the smaller product amount.
- Paragraph 5: The rule is: test each reactant through the balanced equation, compare how much product each could make, and keep the smaller product amount.
- Paragraph 6: Molar mass H2 = 2.016 g/mol → 5.00 g ÷ 2.016 = **2.48 mol H2**
- Paragraph 7: Molar mass O2 = 32.00 g/mol → 24.0 g ÷ 32.00 = **0.750 mol O2**
- Paragraph 8: *Using H2:* mole ratio = 2 mol H2O2 mol H2 = 1:1
- Paragraph 9: *Using O2:* mole ratio = 2 mol H2O1 mol O2
- Paragraph 10: **Compare the possible product amounts, not the starting grams.**
- Paragraph 11: H2 could make 2.48 mol H2O, but O2 could make only 1.50 mol H2O.
- Paragraph 12: **O2 produces less product, so O2 is the limiting reactant.**
- Paragraph 13: That means H2 is in excess and some will be left over.
- Paragraph 14: Molar mass H2O = 18.02 g/mol
- Paragraph 15: O2 is the limiting reactant. The reaction produces **27.0 g of water**.

## Ex 4 Calculating Percent Yield
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Calculate percent yield.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** aspirin theoretical yield = 14.4 g, actual yield = 11.2 g
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** percent yield
- Paragraph 4: **Road map:** label which value is actual and which is theoretical, then place actual over theoretical.
- Paragraph 5: Actual yield = **11.2 g** because this is the amount collected in the lab.
- Paragraph 6: Theoretical yield = **14.4 g** because this is the maximum amount predicted by stoichiometry.
- Paragraph 7: The student's percent yield is **77.8%**. This means the student collected 77.8% of the amount the reaction was predicted to make.
