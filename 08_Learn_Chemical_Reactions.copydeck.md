## 8.1 Start Here: What a Chemical Equation Is Really Saying
- Paragraph 1: A **chemical equation** is the chemist's shorthand for showing a reaction. It uses formulas instead of full words. The starting substances are the **reactants**. The new substances formed are the **products**. The arrow (→) means "produces."
- Paragraph 2: An equation is not just something to balance. It tells you what changed, what formed, and how those substances are related.
- Paragraph 3: Numbers in front of formulas are **coefficients**. They tell you how many particles or moles of each substance are involved. The small numbers inside a formula, like the 2 in H2O, are **subscripts**. Those belong to the substance itself. *Do not change subscripts to balance an equation. Change coefficients only.*

## 8.2 Balancing Chemical Equations Without Breaking the Formula
- Paragraph 1: A **balanced equation** has the same number of each type of atom on both sides. This follows the **Law of Conservation of Mass** — atoms are never created or destroyed in a chemical reaction, only rearranged.
- Paragraph 2: The two mistakes that keep showing up here: changing subscripts, and guessing coefficients without counting first. Start by treating balancing as organized accounting for atoms.
- Paragraph 3: Put the correct formulas for reactants on the left and products on the right. Do not change any subscripts.
- Paragraph 4: Make a tally of every element. Compare reactant count to product count. Identify which elements are unbalanced.
- Paragraph 5: Place whole-number coefficients in front of formulas to make the atom counts equal. Balance one element at a time. Leave elements that appear in only one place on each side for last.
- Paragraph 6: Recount every element on both sides. Make sure all coefficients are the smallest possible whole numbers (reduce if needed).
- Paragraph 7: Step 1
- Paragraph 8: Start with the right substances in the right places. Keep every subscript exactly as written in the formula.
- Paragraph 9: Step 2
- Paragraph 10: Reactants
- Paragraph 11: Products
- Paragraph 12: The counts do not match yet, so the equation is unbalanced.
- Paragraph 13: Step 3
- Paragraph 14: Fix nitrogen first
- Paragraph 15: Put **2** in front of NH3 so product nitrogen becomes 2.
- Paragraph 16: Then fix hydrogen
- Paragraph 17: Now products have 6 H, so put **3** in front of H2.
- Paragraph 18: Step 4
- Paragraph 19: Everything matches, and the coefficients 1 : 3 : 2 are already the smallest whole-number ratio.
- Paragraph 20: A common strategy
- List item 1: Start with an element that appears in only one formula on each side.
- List item 2: If a polyatomic ion stays together on both sides, you can often balance it as a unit.
- List item 3: Hydrogen and oxygen are often easiest to finish last.
- List item 4: If you feel tempted to change a subscript, stop. Go back and adjust a coefficient instead.

## 8.3 Reaction Types: How to Recognize the Pattern Fast
- Paragraph 1: Most reactions fit into a small number of categories. Knowing the type helps you predict the products even before running the reaction.
- Paragraph 2: Reaction type is about the pattern of change. Ask what is happening structurally: joining, breaking apart, replacing, swapping, or burning in oxygen.
- Paragraph 3: Use the particle-level models below to connect each reaction label to an actual rearrangement of atoms. The pattern matters more than memorizing the name.
- Paragraph 4: Ask what the atoms are doing: joining, breaking apart, replacing, swapping, or burning in oxygen.
- List item 1: **Synthesis:** smaller pieces join to make one product.
- List item 2: **Decomposition:** one reactant breaks into smaller pieces.
- List item 3: **Single replacement:** one element replaces another element in a compound.
- List item 4: **Double replacement:** two ionic compounds swap ions.
- List item 5: **Combustion:** O2 is a reactant, and oxides form. Hydrocarbon combustion commonly makes CO2 and H2O.
- Table row 1: Type | Pattern | Simple Example
- Table row 2: Synthesis | A + B → AB | 2Na + Cl2 → 2NaCl
- Table row 3: Decomposition | AB → A + B | 2H2O2 → 2H2O + O2
- Table row 4: Single Replacement | A + BC → AC + B | Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl2 + H2
- Table row 5: Double Replacement | AB + CD → AD + CB | AgNO3 + NaCl → AgCl + NaNO3
- Table row 6: Combustion | fuel + O2 → CO2 + H2O | CH4 + 2O2 → CO2 + 2H2O

## 8.4 Precipitation Reactions: When Mixing Solutions Makes a Solid
- Paragraph 1: A **precipitation reaction** happens when two solutions are mixed and a solid forms. The solid that forms is called a **precipitate**. Precipitates form because two ions combine to make an insoluble compound — one that does not dissolve in water.
- Paragraph 2: To predict whether a precipitate forms, first swap the ions to predict the products. Then use **solubility rules** to decide whether one product stays dissolved or becomes a solid.
- Paragraph 3: Start here if net ionic equations have felt mysterious. The decision is simpler than it looks: predict the two products, then ask which one does not stay aqueous.
- Paragraph 4: Write the two possible products with the pattern **AB + CD → AD + CB**.
- Paragraph 5: If one predicted product is insoluble, that product is the **precipitate**.
- Paragraph 6: Split only the aqueous ionic compounds into ions. The unchanged ions are **spectator ions**, and the solid stays together in the net ionic equation.
- Paragraph 7: Step 1
- Paragraph 8: Both reactants are dissolved, so the ions are free to meet and exchange partners in solution.
- Paragraph 9: Step 2
- Paragraph 10: Positive ions switch negative partners. That gives the two predicted products **AgCl** and **NaNO3**.
- Paragraph 11: Step 3
- Paragraph 12: Because AgCl does not stay dissolved, it forms the solid precipitate while NaNO3 stays aqueous.
- List item 1: A **net ionic equation** shows only the ions that actually change — the ones that form the precipitate.
- List item 2: Ions that do not change are called **spectator ions** and are left out of the net ionic equation.
- List item 3: If both products stay soluble, no precipitate forms and there may be no visible reaction.
- Label 1: Key Solubility Rules (starter set for this unit)

## 8.5 Acid-Base Reactions: The Neutralization Pattern
- Paragraph 1: An **acid** releases hydrogen ions (H+) in water. A **base** releases hydroxide ions (OH-) in water. When an acid and a base react, they **neutralize** each other. The products are water and a salt.
- Paragraph 2: Think of it as a special double-replacement pattern. If you can spot H+ and OH- combining to make water, you can usually see the chemistry much faster.
- Paragraph 3: In this unit, focus on **strong acid + strong base** neutralization reactions.
- Paragraph 4: The net ionic equation for a strong acid–strong base neutralization is:
- Paragraph 5: Step 1
- Paragraph 6: In water, the acid provides H+ and the base provides OH-. The other ions are along for the ride.
- Paragraph 7: Step 2
- Paragraph 8: The H+ pairs with OH- to make water. The remaining Na+ and Cl- form the salt.
- Paragraph 9: Step 3
- Paragraph 10: This is why the net ionic equation is so short: only the ions that form water belong in it.
- List item 1: Acid-base reactions are a special type of double-replacement reaction.
- List item 2: In these reactions, H+ from the acid reacts with OH- from the base to make water.
- List item 3: The remaining ions form the salt.
- Label 1: Neutralization Pattern
- Label 2: Net Ionic Equation

## 8.6 Redox Reactions: Tracking Electron Transfer
- Paragraph 1: In a **redox reaction**, electrons are transferred from one substance to another. This changes the **oxidation number** (charge bookkeeping) of the atoms involved.
- Paragraph 2: If you are confused here, connect it back to [Unit 03 · Atomic Structure](/03_learn_atomic_structure). Redox is really about what happens when atoms gain or lose control of electrons during a reaction.
- Paragraph 3: Use this to keep the direction straight: **OIL RIG** — Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain (of electrons).
- Paragraph 4: **Oxidation number rules** (in order of priority):
- Paragraph 5: Use these examples to practice the rules above. Cover the oxidation number and work it out from the rules before you check.
- List item 1: **Oxidation** = losing electrons → oxidation number *increases*
- List item 2: **Reduction** = gaining electrons → oxidation number *decreases*
- List item 3: The substance that gets oxidized is called the **reducing agent** (it gives away electrons).
- List item 4: The substance that gets reduced is the **oxidizing agent** (it accepts electrons).
- List item 5: They always work in pairs.
- Label 1: Oxidation Number Rules

## Ex 1 Balancing a Chemical Equation
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Balance the chemical equation.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** Fe + O2 → Fe2O3
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** balanced equation
- Paragraph 4: **Road map:** count atoms, choose coefficients, then recheck every element.
- Paragraph 5: The rule is: a balanced equation must have the same number of each atom on both sides, and you may change only coefficients. Start with the unbalanced equation and count atoms: reactants = 1 Fe and 2 O; products = 2 Fe and 3 O.
- Paragraph 6: Fe2O3 has 3 oxygen atoms. Put a **2** in front of Fe2O3 so the product side has **6 O**, an even number that can match O2.
- Paragraph 7: This also changes iron on the product side to **4 Fe**.
- Paragraph 8: The right side now has 6 oxygen atoms, so put a **3** in front of O2 to make 6 O on the left.
- Paragraph 9: The product side has 4 Fe, so put a **4** in front of Fe on the reactant side.
- Paragraph 10: Fe: 4 = 4 ✓ O: 6 = 6 ✓ Coefficients are the smallest whole numbers. ✓

- Paragraph 2: **Given:** AgNO3(aq) + NaCl(aq) → ?

- Paragraph 4: **Road map:** swap ions, check solubility, then cancel spectators.

## Ex 3 Identifying a Redox Reaction Using Oxidation Numbers
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Use oxidation numbers to analyze a redox reaction.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** 2Na + Cl2 → 2NaCl
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** which element is oxidized, which is reduced, and the oxidizing and reducing agents
- Paragraph 4: **Road map:** assign oxidation numbers on both sides, compare, then name the agents.
- Paragraph 5: The governing rule is: pure elements have oxidation number 0. So Na(s) = **0** and Cl2(g) = **0** before the reaction starts.
- Paragraph 6: In NaCl: Na is a group 1 metal → **+1**. For the compound to be neutral: Cl must be **-1**.
- Paragraph 7: Both changed → this is a redox reaction.
