Introductory General Chemistry  ·  Unit 14

Acids & Bases

Acid-base chemistry governs everything from the pH of your blood to the sting of a lemon. This unit develops the Brønsted-Lowry framework, connects acid/base strength to equilibrium constants, and builds the quantitative tools — pH, pOH, buffers, and titrations — used throughout analytical chemistry.

14.1 Brønsted-Lowry Acids and Bases

A Brønsted-Lowry acid is any species that can donate a proton (H⁺) to another species. A Brønsted-Lowry base is any species that can accept that proton. Every acid-base reaction is simply a proton transfer — the acid loses H⁺ and becomes its conjugate base; the base gains H⁺ and becomes its conjugate acid.

HA + B ⇌ A⁻ + BH⁺
acid + base ⇌ conjugate base + conjugate acid

A species is amphiprotic if it can either donate or accept a proton depending on conditions. Water is the most important amphiprotic species — it undergoes autoionization:

2 H₂O(l) ⇌ H₃O⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq)

The equilibrium constant for autoionization is the ion-product of water, Kw:

Kw = [H₃O⁺][OH⁻] = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴  (at 25 °C)
▸

Every conjugate acid-base pair is connected by exactly one proton. HCl donates H⁺ to become Cl⁻ — so HCl/Cl⁻ is a conjugate pair. The stronger the acid, the weaker its conjugate base, and vice versa.

14.2 pH and pOH

Because hydronium and hydroxide concentrations are often tiny numbers (e.g., 1.0 × 10⁻⁷ M), chemists use a logarithmic scale. The pH and pOH compress these wide ranges into manageable values:

pH = −log[H₃O⁺]     pOH = −log[OH⁻]
pH + pOH = 14  (at 25 °C)
pH Scale — Solution Classification at 25 °C
Condition[H₃O⁺] vs [OH⁻]pHExample
Acidic[H₃O⁺] > [OH⁻]< 7Stomach acid (pH ≈ 1.5)
Neutral[H₃O⁺] = [OH⁻]= 7Pure water
Basic[H₃O⁺] < [OH⁻]> 7Bleach (pH ≈ 12)
▸

Each unit change in pH represents a tenfold change in [H₃O⁺]. A solution of pH 3 has 100 times the hydronium concentration of a pH 5 solution — not 2 times.

14.3 Strong vs. Weak Acids and Bases

A strong acid ionizes completely in water — essentially 100% of the molecules donate their proton to water. A weak acid only partially ionizes, reaching an equilibrium described by the acid ionization constant Ka. The larger Ka, the stronger the acid.

HA(aq) + H₂O(l) ⇌ H₃O⁺(aq) + A⁻(aq)
Ka = [H₃O⁺][A⁻] / [HA]

For bases, the analogous constant is Kb. A key relationship connects a conjugate pair:

Ka × Kb = Kw = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴
Common Strong Acids and Bases (memorize these)
Strong AcidsStrong Bases
HCl, HBr, HINaOH, KOH, LiOH
HNO₃, H₂SO₄, HClO₄Ca(OH)₂, Ba(OH)₂, Sr(OH)₂

For binary acids (H bonded directly to a nonmetal), acid strength increases going down a group (bond to H weakens) and going left to right across a period (electronegativity pulls electron density away from H). For oxyacids, more oxygen atoms bonded to the central element means a stronger acid because they pull electron density away from the O–H bond.

14.4 pKa and pKb

Just as pH is the negative log of a concentration, pKa and pKb are the negative logs of the ionization constants Ka and Kb. Compressing equilibrium constants onto a log scale makes them easier to compare and use directly in buffer calculations.

pKa = −log(Ka)     pKb = −log(Kb)

Because a stronger acid has a larger Ka, it has a smaller pKa. The direction flips on the log scale — low pKa means strong acid, high pKa means weak acid. The same logic applies to bases.

pKa Values for Common Weak Acids at 25 °C
AcidKapKaRelative strength
Hydrofluoric acid (HF)6.8 × 10⁻⁴3.17Relatively strong weak acid
Acetic acid (CH₃COOH)1.8 × 10⁻⁵4.74Moderate
Carbonic acid (H₂CO₃)4.3 × 10⁻⁷6.37Moderate–weak
Ammonium ion (NH₄⁺)5.6 × 10⁻¹⁰9.25Very weak acid

For a conjugate acid-base pair, pKa and pKb always sum to 14 at 25 °C — the same relationship as pH and pOH:

pKa + pKb = 14  (at 25 °C)
▸

pKa is the pH at which exactly half the weak acid has ionized — meaning [HA] = [A⁻]. At that point the Henderson-Hasselbalch log term equals zero, so pH = pKa exactly. This is also why the best buffers operate within ±1 pH unit of pKa.

14.5 Polyprotic Acids

A polyprotic acid contains more than one ionizable proton and loses them one at a time in sequential steps. Each step has its own ionization constant, and these constants decrease dramatically with each step — because it is much harder to pull a positive proton away from an already-negative ion.

H₂SO₃: Ka1 = 1.5 × 10⁻²   (first proton — relatively easy)
          Ka2 = 6.3 × 10⁻⁸   (second proton — much harder)

Because Ka1 ≫ Ka2, the first dissociation dominates. For most purposes you can calculate the pH of a polyprotic acid using only the first ionization constant and treat each step separately.

14.6 Buffers

A buffer is a solution that resists changes in pH when small amounts of strong acid or strong base are added. Buffers contain an appreciable amount of both members of a weak conjugate acid-base pair — the acid component neutralizes added base, and the base component neutralizes added acid.

The pH of a buffer is calculated using the Henderson-Hasselbalch equation:

pH = pKa + log([A⁻] / [HA])

A buffer works best when [A⁻] ≈ [HA], meaning pH ≈ pKa. Adding too much acid or base exhausts one component and destroys the buffer — this limit is called the buffer capacity.

▸

Blood is buffered primarily by the carbonic acid / bicarbonate pair (H₂CO₃ / HCO₃⁻, pKa ≈ 6.1) and maintains a pH of 7.35–7.45. A shift outside this range is life-threatening.

14.7 Acid-Base Titrations

A titration is a procedure for determining the concentration of an unknown acid or base by adding a standardized solution of the opposite until the reaction is complete. The equivalence point is where moles of acid exactly equal moles of base. A titration curve plots pH vs. volume of titrant and reveals the equivalence point as a steep vertical rise.

Ex 1 Identifying Conjugate Acid-Base Pairs

Given: NH₃ + H₂O ⇌ NH₄⁺ + OH⁻    Find: Both conjugate pairs.

Identify the proton donor (acid)

H₂O donates one H⁺ to NH₃. So H₂O is the Brønsted-Lowry acid in this reaction.

Identify the proton acceptor (base)

NH₃ accepts that H⁺ and becomes NH₄⁺. So NH₃ is the Brønsted-Lowry base.

Name both conjugate pairs

Pair 1: H₂O (acid) / OH⁻ (conjugate base) — they differ by one H⁺.
Pair 2: NH₄⁺ (conjugate acid) / NH₃ (base) — they differ by one H⁺.

Ex 2 Calculating pH of a Strong Acid

Given: 0.025 M HCl    Find: pH and pOH

Recognize complete ionization

HCl is a strong acid — it ionizes 100% in water. Therefore [H₃O⁺] = 0.025 M exactly.

Calculate pH
Factor-Label Chain
pH = −log[H₃O⁺] = −log(0.025) = 1.60
Find pOH
pOH = 14 − pH = 14 − 1.60 = 12.40

Ex 3 pH of a Weak Acid Using Ka

Given: 0.100 M acetic acid (CH₃COOH), Ka = 1.8 × 10⁻⁵    Find: pH

Write the ionization equation and ICE table

CH₃COOH + H₂O ⇌ H₃O⁺ + CH₃COO⁻

Set up the Ka expression and solve for x
1.8 × 10⁻⁵ = x² / (0.100 − x) ≈ x² / 0.100

Ex 4 Buffer pH Using Henderson-Hasselbalch

Given: A buffer contains 0.100 M acetic acid and 0.150 M sodium acetate. Ka(acetic acid) = 1.8 × 10⁻⁵    Find: pH

Calculate pKa
pKa = −log(1.8 × 10⁻⁵) = 4.74
Apply Henderson-Hasselbalch
pH = pKa + log([A⁻] / [HA])
pH = 4.74 + log(0.150 / 0.100)
pH = 4.74 + log(1.50)
pH = 4.74 + 0.176 = 4.92
Interpret the result

The pH (4.92) is slightly above pKa (4.74) because there is more conjugate base than acid — as expected from Henderson-Hasselbalch. The buffer favors slightly basic conditions within its operating range.

Ex 5 Converting Between Ka, pKa, Kb, and pKb

Given: Acetic acid has Ka = 1.8 × 10⁻⁵    Find: pKa, Kb of acetate, and pKb of acetate.

Calculate pKa from Ka
pKa = −log(Ka) = −log(1.8 × 10⁻⁵) = 4.74

A pKa of 4.74 means acetic acid is a moderately weak acid — stronger than carbonic acid (pKa = 6.37) but weaker than HF (pKa = 3.17).

Find Kb of the conjugate base (acetate, CH₃COO⁻)
Ka × Kb = Kw = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴
Kb = Kw / Ka = (1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴) / (1.8 × 10⁻⁵) = 5.6 × 10⁻¹⁰
Calculate pKb — and verify the sum rule
pKb = −log(5.6 × 10⁻¹⁰) = 9.26
pKa + pKb = 4.74 + 9.26 = 14.00 ✓

The large pKb confirms that acetate is a very weak base — it barely reacts with water, which is why acetic acid solutions are only mildly acidic rather than strongly so.
