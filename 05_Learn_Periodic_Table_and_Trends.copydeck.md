# Concepts Tab

## 5.1 Start Here: How the Periodic Table Is Organized
- Paragraph 1: The **periodic table** arranges the elements by increasing **atomic number** (number of protons). It is built so elements with similar behavior land in the same column.
- Paragraph 2: Start with the two directions that matter most:
- Paragraph 3: Memory hook
- List item 1: "Period = row, Group = column." Period 2 elements all use the 2nd energy level as their outermost shell.
- List item 2: Group 1 elements all have 1 valence electron.
- List item 3: Do not mix up period and group. Getting those mixed up sends trend answers in the wrong direction.
- Table row 1: Direction | Called | How many? | What changes as you move?
- Table row 2: ↔ Horizontal row | **Period** | 7 periods | Elements in the same period have the same outermost energy level. Atomic number increases by 1 each step from left to right.
- Table row 3: ↕ Vertical column | **Group** (or Family) | 18 groups | Elements in the same group have similar valence electron patterns. Moving down a group adds one energy level each row.

## 5.2 Regions of the Periodic Table
- Paragraph 1: Start by reading the table in three visible regions: left-side metals, staircase metalloids, and right-side nonmetals, plus a few important named families. Get these clean before you move to trend arrows — the regions tell you where each trend is strongest.
- Paragraph 2: Here is how all three big trends map onto the table at once. Keep this as a reference as you work through 5.4–5.6.
- Paragraph 3: One Map for the Three Big Trends
- Paragraph 4: **Across a period:** smaller to the right
- Paragraph 5: **Down a group:** larger downward
- Paragraph 6: **Largest area:** bottom-left
- Paragraph 7: **Across a period:** higher to the right
- Paragraph 8: **Down a group:** lower downward
- Paragraph 9: **Highest area:** top-right
- Paragraph 10: **Across a period:** higher to the right
- Paragraph 11: **Down a group:** lower downward
- Paragraph 12: **Highest area:** top-right, especially F
- Paragraph 13: Quick anchor: radius points toward the bottom-left. Ionization energy and electronegativity point toward the top-right.
- List item 1: The **staircase line** separates metals (left) from nonmetals (right).
- List item 2: Elements touching the staircase are **metalloids** with mixed properties.
- List item 3: Silicon (Si) is the classic example: it conducts electricity, but only a little.
- Table row 1: Region | Location | Examples | Key traits
- Table row 2: **Alkali metals** (Group 1) | Far left column | Li, Na, K | 1 valence e-, very reactive, soft metals
- Table row 3: **Alkaline earth metals** (Group 2) | Second column | Be, Mg, Ca | 2 valence e-, reactive metals
- Table row 4: **Transition metals** | Middle block (Groups 3–12) | Fe, Cu, Zn | Typical metals that often form more than one ion charge
- Table row 5: **Metalloids** | Staircase border | Si, Ge, As | Properties between metals and nonmetals
- Table row 6: **Nonmetals** | Upper right | C, N, O, S | Poor conductors, gain electrons
- Table row 7: **Halogens** (Group 17) | Second to last column | F, Cl, Br | 7 valence e-, very reactive nonmetals
- Table row 8: **Noble gases** (Group 18) | Far right column | He, Ne, Ar | 8 valence e- (He: 2), almost no reactivity

## 5.3 Valence Electrons and the Group Number
- Paragraph 1: **Valence electrons** are the electrons in the outermost occupied energy level. They are the electrons most involved in chemical bonding. For **main-group elements only** (Groups 1, 2, and 13–18), the group number tells you the number of valence electrons. This is one of the most useful shortcuts in the whole course.
- List item 1: Elements in the same group have the **same number of valence electrons**, which is why they behave similarly.
- List item 2: Do **not** use this shortcut for transition metals in Groups 3–12.
- List item 3: Sodium (Na) and potassium (K) are both in Group 1, both have 1 valence electron, and both react violently with water.
- Label 1: Shortcut for main-group elements
- Table row 1: Element | Group | Valence electrons | Dot structure dots

- Table row 3: Mg (magnesium) | 2 | 2 | Mg

- Table row 9: Ar (argon) | 18 | 8 | Ar

## 5.4 Atomic Radius
- Paragraph 1: The **atomic radius** is roughly half the distance between two bonded atoms of the same element. It tells us how big an atom is. Start here with the big idea: more shells usually means bigger, and more nuclear pull in the same shell usually means smaller.
- Paragraph 2: As you move right across a period, the number of protons increases, but the electrons are all in the *same* energy level. More protons pull the electrons inward more strongly. The atom shrinks.
- Paragraph 3: Example — Period 3: Na (186 pm) → Mg (160 pm) → Al (143 pm) → Si (111 pm) → P (106 pm) → Cl (99 pm)
- Paragraph 4: As you move down a group, each new period adds a whole new energy level. More energy levels mean electrons are farther from the nucleus. The atom grows.
- Paragraph 5: Example — Group 1: Li (152 pm) → Na (186 pm) → K (227 pm) → Rb (248 pm) → Cs (265 pm)
- List item 1: **The biggest atoms are in the bottom-left corner** (Cs, Fr).
- List item 2: The smallest atoms are in the top-right corner (He, F).
- List item 3: Picture the periodic table as a hillside: atoms get smaller as you climb toward the upper right.

## 5.5 Ionization Energy
- Paragraph 1: **Ionization energy (IE)** is the energy needed to remove one electron from a neutral atom in the gas phase.
- Paragraph 2: If you get stuck, ask one question: how hard is this atom fighting to keep its electron?
- Paragraph 3: As the number of protons increases, the nucleus pulls electrons more strongly, so it takes more energy to remove one. Noble gases are usually the highest in their period.
- Paragraph 4: The outer electrons are farther from the nucleus and more shielded by inner electrons, so they are easier to remove.
- Paragraph 5: Example — Group 1: Li (520 kJ/mol) → Na (496) → K (419) → Cs (376)
- List item 1: High IE: the atom holds that electron tightly.
- List item 2: Low IE: the electron is easier to remove.
- List item 3: IE trends are exactly **opposite** to atomic radius trends.
- List item 4: Smaller atom = harder to remove an electron.
- List item 5: Larger atom = easier to remove an electron.
- List item 6: Common mistake: ranking IE the same way as radius. They go opposite directions.

## 5.6 Electronegativity
- Paragraph 1: **Electronegativity (EN)** measures how strongly an atom pulls shared electrons toward itself in a bond. Fluorine (F) is the most electronegative element at 3.98.
- Paragraph 2: Electronegativity is **not** the same as ionization energy. Ionization energy is about removing an electron; electronegativity is about pulling on shared electrons in a bond.

**This reference keeps the electronegativity trend tied to real periodic-table positions.** EN increases toward fluorine across a period and decreases as you move down a group.

**Across a period (left → right): EN increases**
More protons → stronger pull on shared electrons.

**Down a group (top → bottom): EN decreases**
Larger atom → nucleus is farther from bonding electrons → weaker pull.

Using ΔEN as a classroom guideline for bond type

ΔEN = |EN of element A − EN of element B|
About 0.0-0.4 → usually nonpolar covalent
About 0.5-1.7 → usually polar covalent
Greater than about 1.7 → usually ionic

## 5.7 Ion Formation and Ionic Radius
- Paragraph 1: When an atom gains or loses electrons to form an **ion**, its size changes significantly. Here is the size rule: losing electrons usually shrinks the particle, while gaining electrons usually makes it larger.
- Paragraph 2: Na atom: 186 pm → Na+ ion: 102 pm (45% smaller!)
- Paragraph 3: Reason: Fewer electrons, same number of protons. The remaining electrons are pulled in tighter.
- Paragraph 4: Cl atom: 99 pm → Cl- ion: 181 pm (83% bigger!)
- Paragraph 5: Reason: More electrons, same number of protons. Electrons repel each other and spread out.
- Paragraph 6: Isoelectronic series
- Paragraph 7: These four rows show what actually happens to size when an atom becomes an ion — use them to check your reasoning on the two steps above.
- List item 1: Ions with the same electron count can be compared by proton count.
- List item 2: More protons = smaller ion.
- List item 3: N3-, O2-, F-, Ne, Na+, Mg2+, Al3+ all have 10 electrons.
- List item 4: Al3+ (13 protons) is the smallest.
- List item 5: N3- (7 protons) is the largest.
- Label 1: Quick Size Rule
- Table row 1: Species | What changed? | Radius | What the size change tells you
- Table row 2: Na | Neutral atom | 186 pm | Still has its 3rd energy level.
- Table row 3: Na+ | Lost 1 electron | 102 pm | Losing the 3s electron removes the outer level, so the ion gets much smaller.
- Table row 4: Cl | Neutral atom | 99 pm | Has 17 electrons balancing 17 protons.
- Table row 5: Cl- | Gained 1 electron | 181 pm | Adding an electron increases repulsion in the valence shell, so the ion gets larger.

# Examples Tab

## Example 1 · Identifying Period, Group, and Region
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Use sulfur's position to identify its place and behavior on the periodic table.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** Element sulfur (S), atomic number 16
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** period, group, number of valence electrons, and region of the periodic table
- Paragraph 4: Sulfur (S) is in **Period 3** and **Group 16**.
- Paragraph 5: Sulfur is a **main-group** element in Group 16, so it has **6 valence electrons**.
- Paragraph 6: Sulfur is on the right side of the table and is not on the staircase line. It is a **nonmetal**.
- Paragraph 7: S: 1s2 2s2 2p6 3s2 3p4. The outermost occupied energy level is 3, and it contains 6 electrons.
- Paragraph 8: Sulfur: Period 3 · Group 16 · 6 valence electrons · Nonmetal

## Example 2 · Ranking Atomic Radii
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Rank the atoms from smallest to largest atomic radius.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** Na, Mg, K, Cl
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** smallest → largest radius
- Paragraph 4: Na, Mg, and Cl are all in Period 3. Across a period, atomic radius gets smaller from left to right, so the Period 3 order is **Na > Mg > Cl**.
- Paragraph 5: K is below Na in Group 1. Atomic radius gets larger as you move down a group, so K is larger than Na.
- Paragraph 6: **Cl < Mg < Na < K**
- Table row 1: Element | Period | Group
- Table row 2: Na | 3 | 1
- Table row 3: Mg | 3 | 2
- Table row 4: K | 4 | 1
- Table row 5: Cl | 3 | 17

## Example 3 · Using Electronegativity to Classify a Bond
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Use electronegativity values to classify the bond type.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** Bond between potassium (K) and fluorine (F)
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** bond type
- Paragraph 4: K: 0.82 | F: 3.98 (F is the most electronegative element)
- Paragraph 5: 3.16 > 1.7, so this falls in the **ionic** range of the classroom guideline.
- Paragraph 6: This makes sense: K is a metal in the far bottom-left, F is a nonmetal in the top-right. Large EN differences always occur between metals and nonmetals.
- Paragraph 7: In introductory chemistry, these ΔEN cutoffs are used as a guideline for classifying bond type.
- Paragraph 8: **KF is classified as ionic** because the electronegativity difference is very large.

## Example 4 · Comparing Atom and Ion Size
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Decide whether the atom or ion is larger.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** Sodium atom (Na) and sodium ion (Na+)
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** which species is larger
- Paragraph 4: Na is a Group 1 metal. It loses 1 electron to form Na+.
- Paragraph 5: Na atom: 11 protons, 11 electrons → Na+ ion: 11 protons, 10 electrons
- Paragraph 6: Na loses its entire outermost energy level (3s1). The remaining 10 electrons are all in level 2, much closer to the nucleus.
- Paragraph 7: Na atom: 186 pm → Na+ ion: 102 pm
- Paragraph 8: **Na atom is larger than Na+** because losing the outermost electron makes the ion smaller.

## Example 5 · Finding Valence Electrons from Position
- Paragraph 1: **Problem:** Find valence electrons without writing a full electron configuration first.
- Paragraph 2: **Given:** Phosphorus (P), atomic number 15
- Paragraph 3: **Find:** number of valence electrons
- Paragraph 4: P is in **Period 3, Group 15**.
- Paragraph 5: For main-group elements, the last digit of the group number gives the number of valence electrons.
- Paragraph 6: Group 15 → **5 valence electrons**
- Paragraph 7: P: 1s2 2s2 2p6 3s2 3p3 → 3s2 + 3p3 = 5 electrons in level 3 ✓
