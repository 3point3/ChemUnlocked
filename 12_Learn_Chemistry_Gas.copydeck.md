# Concepts Tab

## 12.1 Start Here: How Gases Behave and What Pressure Really Means
- Paragraph 1: Gases are the most spread-out state of matter. Their particles are far apart, move quickly in random directions, and keep colliding with the walls of the container.
- Paragraph 2: **Pressure** is force per unit area (P = F/A). In a gas, that force comes from particle collisions with the container walls. If you keep that particle picture in mind, the gas laws make much more sense.
- Paragraph 3: This is also why a sharp knife cuts better than a dull one: the same force applied over a smaller area creates greater pressure.
- Paragraph 4: In chemistry, **1 atmosphere (1 atm)** is the standard reference pressure. Real atmospheric pressure can change with weather and altitude, but many chemistry problems use 1 atm as the starting reference. A barometer measures atmospheric pressure by the height of mercury it can support, and the SI unit of pressure is the pascal (Pa = N/m²).
- Paragraph 5: These five pressure units all measure the same thing. Recognize which unit a problem gives you and convert before setting up any gas law equation.
- Label 1: Pressure Conversion Factors — Memorize These
- Table row 1: Unit | Symbol | = 1 atm | Common Use
- Table row 2: Atmosphere | atm | 1.000 | Standard chemistry problems
- Table row 3: Millimeters mercury | mmHg | 760.0 | Barometers, medical settings
- Table row 4: Torr | torr | 760.0 | Equivalent to mmHg
- Table row 5: Kilopascal | kPa | 101.325 | SI unit, international use
- Table row 6: Pascal | Pa | 101,325 | Formal SI calculations

## 12.2 Boyle's Law: Pressure and Volume Move Opposite Ways
- Paragraph 1: At **constant temperature and constant amount of gas**, pressure and volume are *inversely proportional*.
- Paragraph 2: Do not miss the condition words here. If temperature is not constant, Boyle's Law is not the right shortcut anymore.
- Paragraph 3: If pressure doubles, volume is cut in half. The product P × V stays constant.
- Paragraph 4: Required Conditions
- Paragraph 5: **Constant temperature (T)** and **constant amount of gas (n)**.
- Paragraph 6: State 1
- Paragraph 7: Molecules have more space, so they hit the container walls less often.
- Paragraph 8: **P1** is lower because wall collisions are less frequent.
- Paragraph 9: Inverse Relationship
- Paragraph 10: P1V1 = P2V2
- Paragraph 11: At constant T and n, squeezing the gas into less space raises the pressure.
- Paragraph 12: State 2
- Paragraph 13: The same number of particles are forced into less space, so wall collisions increase.
- Paragraph 14: **P2** is higher because the particles strike the walls more often.
- Paragraph 15: The particle model shows why Boyle's Law works: with the same number of gas particles at constant temperature, squeezing the gas into less space increases wall collisions and raises pressure.
- Paragraph 16: **Real-world example:** A scuba diver exhales air bubbles at depth (~3 atm). As the bubbles rise toward the surface (~1 atm), the pressure drops to about one-third, so the volume triples.
- Paragraph 17: The P vs. V graph for Boyle's Law always forms a **hyperbola**.

## 12.3 Charles's Law: Volume and Temperature Rise Together
- Paragraph 1: At **constant pressure and constant amount of gas**, volume is *directly proportional* to absolute temperature in Kelvin. Double the Kelvin temperature → double the volume. The V vs. T graph is a straight line through the origin when T is in Kelvin.
- Paragraph 2: The most common mistake in this whole unit happens here: leaving temperature in Celsius. Do not do that. Gas-law temperature must be in Kelvin.
- Paragraph 3: Required Conditions
- Paragraph 4: **Constant pressure (P)** and **constant amount of gas (n)**. Temperature must be in **Kelvin**.
- Paragraph 5: State 1
- Paragraph 6: Cooler gas particles move more slowly, so the volume stays smaller when pressure remains constant.
- Paragraph 7: At the lower Kelvin temperature, the gas occupies the smaller volume **V1**.
- Paragraph 8: Direct Relationship
- Paragraph 9: V1T1
=
V2T2
- Paragraph 10: When pressure stays constant, hotter gas expands because the particles move faster and need more space.
- Paragraph 11: State 2
- Paragraph 12: Hotter gas particles move faster, so the piston rises and the volume increases while the outside pressure stays the same.
- Paragraph 13: At the higher Kelvin temperature, the gas expands to the larger volume **V2**.
- Paragraph 14: The particle picture, equation, and volume change all show the same rule: at constant pressure, hotter gas occupies more space.
- Paragraph 15: **Why Kelvin?** At absolute zero (0 K = −273°C), gas volume theoretically reaches zero. Kelvin starts at this true zero point, making the proportional relationship mathematically valid. Using Celsius gives wrong answers.
- Label 1: Charles's Law (P and n constant) — T ALWAYS in Kelvin!

## 12.4 Gay-Lussac's Law: Pressure Changes When a Rigid Container Is Heated
- Paragraph 1: At **constant volume and constant amount of gas**, pressure is *directly proportional* to absolute temperature in Kelvin. This applies to any rigid container. Heating → higher pressure.
- Paragraph 2: Start here when the problem mentions a rigid can, sealed tank, or fixed-volume container. That is the clue that volume is constant and pressure is the thing that responds.
- Paragraph 3: Required Conditions
- Paragraph 4: **Constant volume (V)** and **constant amount of gas (n)**. The container must be **rigid**, and temperature must be in **Kelvin**.
- Paragraph 5: State 1
- Paragraph 6: Cooler particles move more slowly, so their wall collisions are less frequent and less forceful inside the fixed-volume tank.
- Paragraph 7: T1 = 300 K

**P1** is lower because the slower particles do not strike the walls as hard.
- Paragraph 8: Direct Relationship
- Paragraph 9: P1T1

P2T2
- Paragraph 10: Because volume cannot expand, heating the gas raises particle speed and pressure instead.
- Paragraph 11: State 2
- Paragraph 12: Hotter particles move faster, so the same tank experiences more frequent and more forceful wall collisions.
- Paragraph 13: T2 = 600 K

**P2** is higher because the faster particles hit the walls harder and more often.
- Paragraph 14: In a rigid container, volume cannot expand. Heating the gas increases particle speed, so pressure rises instead.
- Paragraph 15: **Real-world example:** An aerosol spray can in a hot car can explode. The rigid metal walls prevent volume change, so the rising temperature directly drives up the internal pressure — sometimes past the structural limit of the can.
- Label 1: Gay-Lussac's Law (V and n constant) — T ALWAYS in Kelvin!

## 12.5 Avogadro's Law and Molar Volume at STP
- Paragraph 1: At **constant temperature and pressure**, volume is directly proportional to moles. At the same temperature and pressure, equal volumes of gases contain the same number of molecules.
- Paragraph 2: Notice the wording carefully: equal volumes mean equal numbers of **molecules**, not automatically equal numbers of atoms.
- Paragraph 3: Required Conditions
- Paragraph 4: **Constant temperature (T)** and **constant pressure (P)**. At STP, **1 mol of any ideal gas occupies 22.4 L**.
- Paragraph 5: State 1
- Paragraph 6: With fewer gas molecules present, the sample occupies the smaller volume when temperature and pressure stay fixed.
- Paragraph 7: At fixed T and P, the smaller amount of gas corresponds to the smaller volume **V1**.

## 12.6 The Combined Gas Law: One Gas Sample, Two Sets of Conditions
- Paragraph 1: Use the Combined Gas Law when **one sample of gas changes from an initial state to a final state** and the amount of gas stays constant. This law is useful when pressure, volume, and temperature are connected across two sets of conditions.
- Paragraph 2: If you are confused, look for the story structure: before and after, same gas sample, no gas added or lost. That is your cue.
- Paragraph 3: Use this table to see all five laws side by side. The 'Held Constant' column is what you check first when reading a gas law problem.
- Label 1: Combined Gas Law (n constant) — T in Kelvin, P in same units both sides
- Table row 1: Law | Held Constant | Equation | Graph of | Shape
- Table row 2: Boyle's | T, n | P_1V_1 = P_2V_2 | P vs. V | Hyperbola

## 12.7 The Ideal Gas Law: When PV = nRT Is the Better Tool
- Paragraph 1: Combines all four gas variables into one equation for one set of conditions. Unlike the Combined Gas Law, you do not need before-and-after conditions — just what is true right now. Solve for whichever variable is unknown.
- Paragraph 2: The temptation is to avoid `PV = nRT` or reach for it too early. Use it when you have one state, not two, and when pressure, volume, temperature, and moles all matter together.
- Paragraph 3: Exam Tip
- List item 1: Ideal gas behavior assumes no intermolecular forces and negligible molecular volume.
- List item 2: Real gases deviate most at very high pressure or very low temperature.
- List item 3: For introductory chemistry, always assume ideal behavior unless the problem states otherwise.
- Label 1: Ideal Gas Law — T in Kelvin; choose R to match your pressure unit
- Label 2: Derived From Ideal Gas Law

## 12.8 Dalton's Law: Total Pressure and Gas Collected Over Water
- Paragraph 1: In a mixture of non-reacting gases, each gas exerts its own pressure independently. The **partial pressure** of each gas equals the pressure it would exert alone at the same T and V. Total pressure is the sum of all partial pressures.
- Paragraph 2: Do not miss the classic trap here: if the gas is collected over water, the measured pressure is not just your gas. You have to subtract water vapor first.
- Paragraph 3: When a gas is collected over water, the measured pressure includes both the gas you want and water vapor. Subtract the water vapor pressure first to get the pressure of the dry gas. Then use that dry gas pressure in the Ideal Gas Law.
- List item 1: Water vapor pressure at common temperatures: 20°C → 17.5 mmHg | 25°C → 23.8 mmHg | 30°C → 31.8 mmHg | 37°C → 47.1 mmHg.
- List item 2: These must be looked up in a table — they cannot be calculated from the gas laws.
- Label 1: Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures

## 12.9 Kinetic Molecular Theory: Why the Gas Laws Work
- Paragraph 1: The gas laws arise from the behavior of molecules described by the **Kinetic Molecular Theory (KMT)**:
- Paragraph 2: This section ties the whole unit together. If the formulas start to feel memorized instead of understood, come back here and connect each law to particle motion.
- Paragraph 3: The actual volume of molecules is negligible compared to the container. This is why gases are almost entirely empty space and are easily compressed.
- Paragraph 4: They travel in straight lines between collisions. This motion produces pressure when molecules hit container walls.
- Paragraph 5: No kinetic energy is lost in collisions. Total kinetic energy stays constant at constant temperature.
- Paragraph 6: Ideal gas molecules move completely independently between collisions.
- Paragraph 7: KE_avg = 3/2k_BT. This directly explains Charles's Law (T↑ → molecules move faster → push walls harder → volume expands at constant P) and Gay-Lussac's Law (T↑ → harder wall collisions → P increases at constant V).
